Falling Energy Prices Could Still Hit Your Hometown Hard

  • Jerry Kronenberg
  • Jan 16, 2015 8:15 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Falling oil prices could send property values tumbling in Houston and other energy-dependent cities but rising elsewhere. But don't expect to see the impact until around 2016, a Trulia.com study says.

"Big oil-price drops have historically been associated with [delayed] job losses and falling home prices in energy-producing regions," Trulia Chief Economist Jed Kolko wrote in an analysis of how petroleum prices have affected home values since 1980.

Oil has plummeted from more than $100 a barrel last summer to around $50 a barrel in recent days.

Kolko recently studied America's 100 largest housing markets and found that since 1980, such declines have sent home prices reeling in seven U.S. metro areas that have significant energy-related employment. (Those are Houston; New Orleans; Oklahoma City; Bakersfield, Calif.; Baton Rouge, La.: Tulsa, Okla.; and Fort Worth, Texas.)

For instance, a 1980s oil bust sparked a 25% drop in average Houston home prices between mid-1983 and late 1987 — losses that the Lone Star State's most-populous metro area didn't fully recoup until a decade later.

In an interview, Kolko said property values in oil-rich markets often mirror drops in petroleum prices because energy companies lay workers off in downturns, and "fewer [local] jobs means weaker housing demand."

But the effect historically takes time to appear, he added.

Kolko's analysis found that job cuts in oil-rich cities don't typically happen until about six months after energy prices tank, and property values don't fall until one or two years after that.

For example, Houston home prices didn't really crater until late 1987, even though the job market plummeted a year earlier and oil prices recorded their biggest year-over-year decline in 1986's first half.

The economist attributed the lag to the fact that property prices "tend to move slowly" even after local labor markets sag.

Kolko suspects that's partly why homes in the seven communities he studied actually saw average list prices rise 10.5% in 2014 — way above the 7.7% norm for America's 100 largest cities as a whole. "Markets with large oil industries have had strong growth in the past several years," he said.

But now, Kolko expects home prices in oil-rich communities to begin falling in the next year or two unless energy markets recover, although he added that it's "very hard to say how big or long the effect might be."

Conversely, the economist predicts today's rock-bottom energy prices will send home values rising in the rest of America — especially the Northeast and Midwest.

Kolko said consumers in those regions won't face energy-related job losses, but will pay way less for gasoline and utilities, leaving lots left over for home purchases. "Lower oil prices drop the cost of driving and heating in many regions, and that gives consumers more to spend [on] housing," he said.

— By Jerry Kronenberg for MainStreet
