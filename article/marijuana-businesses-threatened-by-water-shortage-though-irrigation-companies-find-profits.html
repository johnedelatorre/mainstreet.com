Marijuana Businesses Threatened By Water Shortage, Though Irrigation Companies Find Profits

  • Marguerite Arnold
  • Sep 5, 2014 11:48 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — One of the more fascinating sectors of the blooming marijuana vertical is where all things cannabinoid hit the ground (or hydro environment). The market segment overall has its colorful fans (including on the penny stock exchanges), but this interest is increasingly echoed by the “big boys” of finance (albeit for right now in the ranks of major pharma).

Read More: Medical Marijuana Efficacy Shown Through Multiple Studies

Morgan Stanley gave British cannabinoid maker GW Pharma (which creates its drugs out of the whole plant as opposed to synthetic cannabinoids concocted in a lab) an enthusiastic “Establishment Wall Street Thumbs Up” earlier this spring. In the meantime, the hydro market, while not yet quite ready for Wall Street prime time, is cleaning up its act at the penny stock level.

The reason for this development is that this part of the market, however unglamorous as the tubes, piping and plumbing (if not real estate management and security, along with compliance) may seem, is in fact what makes this part of the cannabis vertical so potentially profitable. P&L is easily quantifiable in widgets sold or acres of former warehouse space under management, if not actual manpower to maintain the same. In addition, such technology applications are helping growers control costs in a way that is as easily proved as the nearest balance sheet.

One of the growing costs to the industry that such companies control right from the start, is the amount of water used by growers. This year in particular, these issues are hot discussion topics throughout the part of the industry that deals with production.

Read More: Is a Water Shortage Imminent?

Marco Hegyi, the CEO of Growlife (PHOT), a Seattle-based company that distributes grow technology to growers in 17 states, knows viscerally that water consumption if not access is one of the largest issues facing the cannabis industry but not limited to it.

“We are getting a lot of interest for indoor grow that is alot more efficient” he said. “Other growers are coming to the hydro area. We just happen to be the operating system that many different “hardware” users want to use.”

Read More: Growlife Establishes National Marijuana Presence

This part of the marijuana industry vertical also, for the most part, maintains at least one step away from handling or transacting in the actual plant. This is still a major liability for those who do from an “indoor crop risk” perspective (one exists) to those who must rely on federal water supplies for their crops. In May this year, the Interior Department announced that marijuana growers could not access federal water. This hit growers in particularly Washington state hard. Many growers had to shell out thousands of dollars to sidestep federal water access.

For these reasons the market that deals in indoor hydro grows one way or another, has been abuzz all year.

However, there is another reason, which has also floated throughout the conversation and her name is Mother Nature.

According to Ata Gonzalez, founder and CEO of GPharma labs who maintains both outdoor and indoor grows in California, “It is dry out there. This year it has really been an issue. Our wells seem to be tapped out.”

Gonzalez has had to resort to trucking in water for his crops, the first year he has had to do so. “It has not really rained like it should in California since at least 2010,” he remarked ruefully.

This sole issue may drive, more than any other, the development of the production market globally (not just in the United States).

As Hegyi believes, indoor and hydro, “stand to revolutionize not only cannabis production, but food as well.” He reports getting interest from growers all over the country (and for every crop) in Growlife’s new approach to farming.

In California (still the nation’s largest state market) earlier in the year, it was widely reported that water shortages could potentially drive the cost of marijuana back up (costs have continued to decrease for the last five to six years after Bush left office), but said fluctuations have yet to occur or at least be widespread enough to be reported. Despite all the hype about legalizing recreational markets in Washington state and Colorado, there is one thing that has remained a constant about California. There have been no “market shortages” this year. Even with the worst drought in history.

According to industry data out of California, it takes about there to five gallons of water per day to grow an outdoor plant.

Prices for cannabis grown outdoors in California have been running (in places like Humbolt County) about half of that of indoor hydro.

Gonzalez explained the difference between indoor and outdoor plants. While it traditionally has been cheaper to engage in outdoor growing, he understands that this is an equation that may change and may hinge on this one water-availability issue.

“Outdoor plants need a lot of water," he said. "They are a lot thirstier than indoor grown cannabis. Indoor we do not use as much water by any means.”

He is also unsure of how much the additional water cost this year will finally tip the scales to bring his crop inside, but that said, he is seriously already thinking about the almost inevitable transition.

And while the matter of water may be of local concern now certainly, and as part of a crop grown in the United States (for whatever purpose), these issues will from now on apply. As part of the medical, food or energy supply, marijuana also is a part of the national security infrastructure of the United States.

As is, of course, its use of any kind of significant amount of water. How these issues will unfold against the backdrop of overall reform is anyone’s guess right now. In the meantime, the vertical that supplies an industry that is blooming globally this year, is managing to establish itself in such fertile ground.

--Written by Marguerite Arnold for MainStreet
