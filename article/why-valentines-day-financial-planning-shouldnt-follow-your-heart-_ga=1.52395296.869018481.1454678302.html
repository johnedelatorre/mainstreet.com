Why Valentine's Day Financial Planning Shouldn't Follow Your Heart

  • Jason Notte
  • Feb 9, 2016 4:30 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

On this Valentine's Day weekend, take to heart the words of the great Gwen Guthrie: no romance without finance.

Ordinarily, we wouldn't consider an '80s ode to materialism like “Ain't Nothing Going On But The Rent” sound fiscal advice, but this is increasingly what you folks are looking for on Valentine's Day. In a recent poll of 2,000 people conducted by finance site NerdWallet, 40% of all respondents reported a partner’s financial situation is more important than how they look. On top of that, nearly half (48%) of those surveyed say they wouldn’t date someone with bad credit.

On the other end of that equation, those in financial peril seem to prefer digging themselves out of debt alone to tying someone else to their troubles. A survey by MassMutual conducted last year found that 47% of singles would rather experience an unexpected financial challenge over a romantic breakup. That percentage jumps for those who are married or living with a partner (68%) and for Millennials who bore the worst of the financial crisis (61%).

Valentine's Day itself isn't exactly a big help in keeping those books balanced. According to the National Retail Federation, 54.8% of consumers will celebrate Valentine’s Day, spending an average of $146.84 on flowers, jewelry, candy, apparel and more, up from $142.31 last year. Total spending is expected to reach $19.7 billion.

“Low gas prices and guaranteed promotions from retailers large and small should help consumers as they look for the perfect gift for their friends and family,” says Matt Shay, chief executive of the National Retail Federation. “Looking ahead, we’re optimistic consumers are in a good place when it comes to spending on discretionary items like gifts.”

NerdWallet's consumers were especially hopeful, wtih 62% of Americans planning to celebrate Valentine’s Day, 96% of those planning to spend some amount of money on holiday activities and spending $180 on average. NerdWallet noted that Millennials, in particular, tend to throw more money around on Valentine’s Day. Younger men and women in relationships plan to spend an average of $290 on Valentine’s Day activities — more than any other age group. Millennial men in relationships plan to shell out an average of $371.

Their money would be better spent paying off credit card debt, paying bills on time and making the occasional mortgage payment. It turns out that 60% of women and 43% of men consider potential partner’s financial situation is important to them. Some 61% of those who make $100,000 or more, beyond the 48% of total Americans, say they wouldn’t date someone with bad credit. Those with more education (and more student loan debt) have especially strong feelings about this point, with 63% of college graduates saying theywouldn’t date someone with bad credit. Meanwhile, just 40% of people with a high school degree or less would say the same.

Also, don't think that anyone is going to find your embarrassing displays of insolvency cute or puckish. Know that old sitcom trope where a waiter, clerk or manager hilariously cuts up a character's declined credit card? Well, in reality, that's going to end your date night pretty quickly.

“It’s all something to ponder before your romantic night out, whether you’re diving into a first date or enjoying Valentine’s Day with your longtime significant other,” says NerdWallet analyst Courtney Miller. “But it might be best to talk about the kind of things that money can’t buy and to save the credit score discussion for another time.”

However, if your date is incredibly shallow, there's a chance that you can charm your way out of it if you clean up nicely. Though 60% of women say a partner's finances are important to them in their dating life (compared to just 35%) of men, 44% of the women and 35% of men think a partner's financial situation outweighs the person's attractiveness. That's more than half of women and a whopping two-thirds of men who totally wouldn't mind being stuck with the bill or going down to the impound lot and signing for their date's repossessed car if that person is hot enough. However, Millennials aren't quite as desperate, with 54% of younger men considering a potential partner’s financial situation is more important than physical attractiveness, though just 43% of millennial women feel the same.

”You got to have a J-O-B if you want to be with me,” Gwen Guthrie once said. Roughly 30 years later, that's only true if you're not much to look at.
