CFPB Sues Pay-for-Play Student Debt Relief Scammers for Preying on the Vulnerable

  • John Sandman
  • Dec 12, 2014 8:47 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), in a joint law suit filed with the Florida attorney general’s office, took steps on Thursday to shut down College Education Services, a student debt relief company that was charging exorbitant fees for federal loan repayment benefits that are available for free from the Department of Education.

The CFPB also filed suit against Student Loan Processing.US for illegally marketing student debt relief services. The Bureau issued an advisory warning people away from paying for free federal services.

“Student loans are already a significant debt for many Americans,” said CFPB Director Richard Cordray. “College Education Services and Student Loan Processing.US added to that hardship by taking advantage of troubled borrowers and failing to describe their services honestly.”

“When scam artists prey on student loan borrowers, we will take action to halt their illegal activity,” Cordray added.

The U.S. Department of Education offers numerous plans to borrowers with federal student loans to make payments more affordable. These options include those that allow borrowers to set their monthly payment based on their income, known as Income Based Repayment. The CFPB said in a statement that the Department of Education does not charge any fees to apply for or enroll in these plans, for which many student loan borrowers qualify.

The CFPB also noted that the high level of frustration many borrowers experience through routine processing operations make them road kill for scammers.

Marcia Elena Vargas, owner of College Education Services, and Frank Liz, an employee, marketed and advertised debt relief services to borrowers with defaulted student loans. The Tampa-based company hawked it wares on the Internet and operated websites with the domain names CollegeDefaultedStudentLoan.com and HelpStudentLoanDefault.com.

The CFPB alleges that the firm charged illegal advance fees before any debt relief was provided, among other fraudulent activities. Federal law requires that at least one debt be settled or renegotiated before any fees can be collected. In addition, College Education Services’ telemarketers presented themselves as “counselors” who failed to deliver on unrealistic claims. They also falsely claimed that they could provide relief from wage or benefit garnishments. A garnishments is a court order that allows a creditor to take money directly from a debtor’s bank account.

“We have unfortunately seen student loan debt relief business grow and engage in more abusive practices,” said the National Consumer Law Center’s Deanne Loonin, director of the NCLC’s Student Loan Debt Assistance project. “We are alarmed at the proliferation of these companies, particularly the use of new and more aggressive marketing tactics.”

Also sued by the CFPB was Student Loan Processing.US, which acted as a front for Irvine Web Works, Inc. of Laguna Nigel, Calif. The CFPB alleges that since at least July 2011, the company and its owner, James Krause, have been marketing services to borrowers while falsely claiming an affiliation with the Department of Education. Like College Education Services, Student Loan Processing .US charged an upfront fee -- 1% of the federal student loan balance or $250, whichever was higher. The CFPB is seeking restitution for swindled consumers and a civil penalty against Student Loan Processing.US.

The CFPB has asked a Federal district court to enter a consent order against College Education Services, Vargas and Liz that would permanently bar them from the debt relief business.

Vargas, Liz and the company were hit with a $25,000 civil penalty. The relatively low-ball figure was based on what the CFPB said was “the defendants’ inability to pay a more substantial amount.” But the CFPB also said that the company “reaped millions of dollars in advance fees from thousands of consumers before it ceased operations around February 2013.” Where the money went is not clear.
