Before Recession, Black-Owned Businesses Soared

  • Laurie Kulikowski
  • Feb 9, 2011 12:01 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

WASHINGTON (TheStreet) -- Black-owned small businesses in the U.S. surged by 60.5% between 2002 and 2007, more than triple the national growth rate of small firms, but the group still accounts for some of the smallest gross receipts, according to new data from the U.S. Census Bureau.

As of 2007, black-owned nonfarm businesses totaled 1.9 million, roughly 7.1% of all nonfarm businesses in the country, according to the Census' 2007 Survey of Business Owners looking at black-owned businesses.

Receipts generated by black-owned businesses increased 55%, to $137.5 billion, in that five years, the report said.

"Black-owned businesses continued to be one of the fastest-growing segments of our economy, showing rapid growth in both the number of businesses and total sales during this time," according to a statement by Census Bureau Deputy Director Thomas Mesenbourg.

Still, most black-owned firms are fairly small. Approximately 87% of the firms have gross receipts of less than $50,000 per year, vs. 65% of all U.S. firms, the report said. Just 1% of black-owned firms have gross receipts totaling $1 million or more per year, which is actually a 35% increase from 2002, to 14,507 businesses.

The data release Tuesday was part of the agency's scheduled detailed report of findings by ethnicity and by gender. The survey provides detailed information every five years for black-owned businesses, including the number of firms, sales and receipts, number of paid employees and annual payroll, the agency said. About 2.3 million businesses were sampled in the report.

Some 921,000 people (0.8% of total employment at the time) were employed by a small, black-owned firm in 2007.

While the results are encouraging, it must be noted that the latest data are from before the recent financial crisis and resulting recession.

Still, officials say the figures have likely improved further since 2007, given the economic environment.

"Entrepreneurship usually increases during bad times," says Harry Alford, CEO and president of the National Black Chamber of Commerce. "We're still growing. Our membership is having our best year" since the organization began 17 years ago. Alford remembers that in 1993, as he and his wife launched the chamber, Census data showed just 300,000 black-owned businesses bringing in roughly $30 billion in revenue. The chamber now represents about 95,000 black-owned businesses, according to its website.

"Today, 1.9 million black-owned businesses doing $137 billion in revenue -- that's progress and it's picking up the pace. Each five-year report there is a bigger increase," Alford says.

Businesses in several areas are showing significant growth, Alford notes, including international trade, commercial construction, information technology and energy sectors.

"People are starting to explore," Alford says.

"Entrepreneurship usually increases during bad times. Our membership is having our best year since 1993."

-- Harry Alford, CEO of National Black Chamber of Commerce

Marc Morial, president and CEO of the National Urban League, agreed the recession likely accelerated growth in small, black-owned businesses.

Some of these businesses may have been in addition to the owner's primary full-time jobs, he said during Tuesday's conference call to discuss the Census findings.

"I do think that there were many people who retreated to entrepreneurship trying to find ways to survive" if they were laid off or out of work, Morial said. "They're going to quote-unquote hustle in a legitimate way."
