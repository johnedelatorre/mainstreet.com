Millennials Scared into Saving

  • Chris Metinko
  • Jul 7, 2014 9:58 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Having survived the "Great Recession of 2008," Millennials are acutely aware of the importance of saving in financial survival.

However, as with older generations of Americans, knowing what's best and doing what's best appear to be two different things for one of the newer generations.

A new study shows a strong majority – four in five – think of savings as "survival" and realize they should be starting to save now. Of course — like most Americans — nearly half saying they are not saving for retirement and those who are not saving enough, according to the study by Wells Fargo.

Only about 55% reported saving for their retirement, and there is a wide gap between males and females when it comes to planning for their golden years. Just more than 60% of men said they already have started to save for retirement, while only 50% of women reporting likewise. The difference may be due to a wide disparity in median household income, which Millennials men reporting an income of $77,000 while women in the cohort average $56,000.

"The silver lining of the recession that started over five years ago is that a majority of millennials get that saving is a necessity and even equate it with 'surviving' tough times," said Karen Wimbish, director of retail retirement at Wells Fargo. "But Millennial women are starting out their working lives making far less than men and, as a consequence, are saving less and feeling less contentment at the start of their working lives."

While about half of all Millennials say they are "satisfied" with their savings, it is again slanted towards males with 58% of men feeling that way versus 41% of women.

"We need more equality in the workplace because men shouldn't be making 20,000 more than women in 2014," said Dan Schawbel, a managing partner of Millennial Branding, a Gen Y research and consulting firm. "Since women aren't making as much, they don't have as much to invest in the stock market and have trouble saving because they have loans to pay off."

Many Millennials say that instead of saving, they are actually struggling with debt. More than 40% report "it is their biggest financial concern currently," while exactly 40% say their debt is "overwhelming." Just 23% of Baby Boomers feel that way. More than half of Millennials — 56% — say they are "living paycheck to paycheck," regardless of gender.

Not surprising, 29% of those living with debt named student loans as their number one financial concern after paying off day-to-day bills. However, 44% of Baby Boomers name retirement as their number one financial concern. Millennials report their debt breaks down, on monthly average, as 16% credit card debt, 15% mortgage debt, 12% student loan debt, 9% auto debt and 5% medical debt.

"People have to closely examine what they are spending their money on and figure out the best way to comfortably manage debt and savings levels," Wimbish said.

Schawbel adds Millennials won't have the same standard as living as their parents did and don't have the financial knowledge necessary to save and invest, because they don't learn it in school.

The study also reveals 57% of Millennials cite "family" when asked whom they trust for credible information to help them make financial decisions.

"Millennials rely on their parents for financial advice when they should be seeking third party experts because not all parents have a good understanding of finance," Schawbel said.

The Wells Fargo survey considered Millennials those between the ages of 22 and 33.
