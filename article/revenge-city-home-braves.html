Revenge of the City in the Home of the Braves

  • Dana Blankenhorn
  • May 19, 2014 8:16 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — The big story of this decade is the revenge of the central city.

Also See: Housing Optimism Arrives

The central location where economic value is created has shifted, from the office complex to the college campus. This is reshaping the economics of real estate.

The move to offices heralded the rise of the Sun Belt, cities like Atlanta that were designed to serve commutes from nine-to-five jobs to the suburbs. "Edge Cities," built along the freeways, let the suburbs sprawl continue further. Today, metro Atlanta extends into North Carolina and nearly to Alabama.

This change was symbolized by the Atlanta Braves' decision last year to move from downtown to suburban Cobb County, helped along by $300 million in tax breaks, according to the conservative Tax Foundation.

But while political leaders were not looking, computers came to do most of what offices had done. Each of the last two recessions has seen layers of middle management let-go, replaced by new technology. Today the Edge City towers are cube farms producing the only good of value that must still be created by hand, software.

Value now comes from university campuses. The process that began at Stanford and MIT is transforming all cities, including Atlanta.

A campus life is not an office life. Ideas can appear at any time of the day or night. Economic life is no longer 9-5, it's 24-7, and you have to live near it to be part of it.

As our economic lives have shifted, so cities have been becoming denser. New economic cores have developed around college campuses focused on research, and the suburbs have begun to atrophy like the "Rust Belt," the factory-based economies of the old cities that decayed a generation ago. You can see this on Census Explorer, a mapping site from the U.S. Bureau of the Census.

In 2010, the census tract where Turner Field, the current Braves' stadium, was located had a median family income of just $24,125. A decade earlier it had been $12,780, in constant dollars. But a half-mile east, in Grant Park, 2010 median income was $91,734, up from $53,973, in constant dollars, a decade earlier.

What about the Braves' new home? There, at the intersection of I-75 and I-285, median household income stood at $54,861 in 2010, down from $63,509 in constant dollars a decade earlier. In the census tract just west of the stadium, median income during the decade had actually fallen, from $59,277 in 2000 to $42,370 10 years later.

This is being reflected in housing values.

Zillow reports that the average home in Grant Park, just east of the old stadium, is now worth $264,300, and the market there is described as "hot," with median values up from $221,000 a decade ago.

Homes in Smyrna, just west of the new stadium, now cost $180,400, according to Zillow. The market there is described as merely "warm," with prices little changed from ten years ago.

Atlantans were heartbroken, at first, to be losing the Braves, but they have recovered as plans were unveiled to extend the campus of Georgia State University into the area.

The plan would turn both of the Braves' old parks into on-campus stadiums, giving the school, which became a research university in 1995, the same footprint as its more-famous rivals, the University of Georgia (UGA) and Georgia Tech. The school already has nearly as many students as UGA and is continuing to add facilities by expanding into the old downtown core.

The move from office to campus is a long-term trend. Suburbs are losing their lifeblood, office buildings are becoming the modern equivalent of rust belt factories and growth is coming back into town.

By the year 2020 the Braves may find that, rather than fleeing the dilapidation, they just took it with them.

--Written by Dana Blankenhorn for MainStreet
