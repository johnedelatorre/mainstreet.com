80% of Workers Would Prefer a Vacation to a Raise

  • Eric Reed
  • Oct 9, 2015 12:42 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) -- Bosses, keep your pay raises. These days it looks like the American worker has other priorities.

For years in jobs circles the conversation has remained focused on why U.S. wage growth has significantly underperformed an otherwise healthy job market. Economists have pitched theories all across the board, from a long-term shift in growth to a vast pool of workers still hanging outside the workforce to something new altogether.

It’s an issue that dominated coverage of the recent Federal Reserve Board’s meeting and its decision not to raise interest rates.

Yet while bankers braced themselves to learn the Fed’s opinion on wages relative to employment, businesses have focused somewhere else, with a steady number zeroing in not on paychecks but rather employee benefits. Perks such as free lunches, longer vacations and generous parental leaves have made headlines for companies such as Netflix and Google.

Many observers theorized that these were just ways to attract talent without actually spending extra money on salaries, but it turns out the companies may have been listening to their work force all along.

Nearly four in five U.S. workers would take better benefits over a raise. For Millennials that number goes up to 90%.

We'll say it again: 80% of workers would rather get a vacation than a raise.

These are the results of a recent study by the employment site Glassdoor. Among the categories surveyed, employees chose better health insurance and more vacation time as their number one and two priorities.

People really want some time off, and who can blame them? Nearly a quarter of all workers still get no paid time off at all. Spend enough time swapping shifts at the Red Robin, and just about anyone would be ready to give up a raise in favor of a little bit of R&R.

The weird thing, though, is that this still doesn’t shake the fact that wages remain stagnant. Although millions of Americans struggle to get basic benefits like time off and health care from their employers, they’re still in the minority. So what’s with the overwhelming support for spending more time on the beach?

According to Rusty Rueff, a workplace expert and former CEO, it’s about inflation, or rather the lack thereof.

“There is no inflation,” Rueff said. “We have no inflation in the marketplace and the economy is strong, and when things like gas prices continue to drop or stay steady, we as consumers tend to look at that and feel like we have more money in our pocket. I don’t need 5% salary increases when what it costs me to live and do the things I want to do is staying flat or going up only 1%.”

This relationship between earning power and inflation may also help to explain why the Federal Reserve remains so concerned about inflation. Many skeptics of raising interest rates (including at times this writer) have pointed out that there’s no current signs of inflation significantly past two percent for the U.S. dollar, and that’s true as far as it goes. However if an inflationary cycle were to start and gain momentum, it would exert pressure on stagnant wages very quickly.

A rate of inflation that stays at or below spending power, however, keeps even flat-lined wages feeling healthy. That’s when workers start thinking about more than their take home pay.

Rueff compared it to the famous psychological thought experiment Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs. As workers have grown increasingly confident in their job security and purchasing power new priorities have assumed importance, namely health insurance, vacations and profit sharing.

Glassdoor’s study also reflects the two biggest areas of insecurity for most employees. It’s no coincidence that what most people want is better health insurance and more time off. Health care is a perpetual worry for American workers and one of the first lines of cost-cutting axed by many companies looking to save money during a recession.

As to time off, frankly it’s been a long time since a stressed out nation felt particularly good taking any time off, no less packing up the family for two weeks at Disney World. Leave your chair empty long enough, the thinking went, and you might just come back to find someone else in it.

As employment prospects have gotten better that concern has diminished, only to be replaced by a brand new one: email.

“The root word [of vacation] is vacate," Rueff said. "The problem with that is in today’s world that doesn’t happen. It’s a crazy statistic, some 80-odd% of people check in every day or have somebody from the office check in. The idea of true vacation or true getting away doesn’t happen so much anymore, therefore the value of quantity of time off goes up because I’m not getting quality out of my time away.”

As we’ve written about in the past, thanks to smartphones and tablets, the office has crept into just about every aspect of our lives: the boardroom has moved to the bedroom. Few people truly go home for the day, still checking in or picking up their email even hours after officially leaving the office. The same holds true for vacations, with many major companies going so far as to cover the cost of international plans for their employees who take a trip overseas.

Newly empowered employees are asking for time off again and would like more of it to make up for the perpetual electronic tether.

As confidence in the economy and job market has grown, workers have grown less worried about the bottomline, so much so that the overwhelming majority would prefer new benefits over a bigger paycheck.

For employers, it might be time to catch on to what Google, Facebook and other tech giants already know. If you want to attract the best people start focusing on quality of life.

And maybe add a dental plan.
