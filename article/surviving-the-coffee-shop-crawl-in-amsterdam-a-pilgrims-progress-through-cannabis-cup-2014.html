Surviving The Coffee Shop Crawl in Amsterdam: A Pilgrim’s Progress Through Cannabis Cup 2014

  • Marguerite Arnold
  • Dec 2, 2014 7:00 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

AMSTERDAM (MainStreet) - Americans post-turkey tend to the judgmental, and there are quite a few similarities to human behaviour when confronted with overdoses of turkey and trimmings as there are when faced with the same situation but an abundance of marijuana in place of bird.

There is a temptation, at least for novice users in particular, to not know when to get enough.

That said, it is far harder to overdose on smoked ingestions of the drug, although with the new crystals, hybrids and super potent concentrates, this is becoming more of a problem than it was even a few years ago. The issue of edibles is also starting to give even advocacy groups the willies to the point where several national organizations have issued guidelines for responsible use and labeling this year alone in the U.S.

Part of the alarm in Amsterdam last week was that drugs in general are a major problem for the tourist trade. Last Wednesday, neon signs blazed warnings in English about contaminated cocaine here all around the central train station in a way that is never seen in New York, for example, but maybe should be.

That said -- and despite such reports from people who work in the coffee shop industry in the city as employees -- there were no reported incidents of rowdy or uncivil behavior (if not worse) on this the "coffee shop crawl" designated day of Cannabis Cup 2014.

There was, truth be told, nothing else going on. Festival organizers at High Times seemed incapable, as a frustrated former sponsor who chose to remain nameless told MainStreet, "of doing anything more than showing their faces in one of the newest coffee shops for hours where they would be seen." Most other attendees at this level (here for professional interactions, even if they did not participate in the Expo) had similarly harsh, if not off the record, comments to make all week.

All previously scheduled lectures were summarily cancelled on Wednesday and attendees showed up to a "newly organized" event meeting place (a cafeteria style restaurant around the corner from the Melkweg) where everyone was informed that the crawl in fact was the only event of the day.

It is a job. Someone has to do it.

Along the way, there were, as with any great if not transcendental journeys (from the Shire to Smaug and back again or just to the next coffee shop on the list) fellow travelers who tagged along for a stop or two and talk shop.

This was very much therefore the tenor of the day as MainStreet braved the Cannabis Cup 2014 coffee shop crawl as a call of duty and talked to people along the way.

Read More: Mayor of Amsterdam To Cannabis Cup: You Are Not In Compliance

Attendees griped that High Times was incapable of telling them whether "rescheduling" meant that the magazine conference did not get the license, that the venue was "moved" or that the organizers just screwed up. One American grower reported his partner and he spent more than $25,000 (funds which may be partially refunded by High Times) only to miss the Expo and a centralized place to do business that was more professional and suited for the same than the city's crowded coffee shops. Particularly ones when the coffee shop crawl was also in process.

This amount spent by the two partners is similar to what other sponsors also spent to arrange for travel, product (including demonstration machines) and other expenses associated with exhibiting overseas no matter the business. Many were furious at High Times in particular for apparently not only taking the new city regulations less than seriously but starting to organize for the event very late in the game (by some reports less than five weeks out from the November start date). That said, all are mindful of the publication's power within the industry to want to make these statements off the record and anonymously.

Technically, the event was supposed to be conducted on Thursday and was, if you count the "crawl" which consists of different private establishments that are situated within obvious "rings" of the harbor city that is Amsterdam. Otherwise, nothing else went on. In reality, this is an excuse to channel more business into private coffee shops (if not the seed companies behind all of this who are the real power players in the game here) and which have grown markedly less generous this year now that they do not have to compete with mounds of free "samples" coming out of a centralized distribution point like the Melkweg.

The most immediate impact for a great many attendees? No "free" samples, although this policy varied widely and with what kind of event pass one had.

A "VIP" member of the press, particularly when accompanied by several other interesting participants, is still good for soliciting free samples (and from more than one location no matter how much individual coffee shops sometimes claimed to be the only ones giving away the same).

The MainStreet motley crew started the day at noon at mission central and was conveniently guided by a local who knew the geography well. By 5 p.m., participants collectively (three people) had easily collected about 5 grams of pot and hash samples of all kinds in sum. Minus what was consumed along the way, the party might have had 2 grams left each.

If it sounds like "trick or treat" for those over 18, well, that might be pretty close.

Those not so lucky and faced with the prospect of "having" to judge all festival entries spent up to $600 to buy the same kind of experience (or so went the urban legend circulated on Wednesday among pot shops themselves). Nobody in the MainStreet test group could understand this rumor, but we discussed it as we scooped up our next freebie and moved on. Ari, a 25-year-old dancer from Spain who is paying the bills by working in another coffee shop while she makes herself known to the city's artistic elite, cannot figure out the math.

"If I had to pay, darling," she says in hopelessly dramatic Spanish thisped English, "I would have been disappointed." As it is, she looks over her stash (which includes the obligatory bags, T-shirts and other non-cannabis products she also obtained during this process) and grins. "I had a great day and learned alot," she says. "I am going to talk to my manager about some of the ideas I saw today."

MainStreet was given the royal treatment at several establishments if not the immediate benefit of the doubt. Although the stops were few (less than 20% of the total Cup associated shops), those hours were meaningful. Occasionally refreshments from over sugared treats to free drinks were also offered.

Right along with advice.

At the end of the day, in direct contrast to another widely read experience with a legal pot market, all participants were feeling comfortable. This reporter has a movement disorder, and the amount of freebie pot snagged along the way (all smokeable and fairly powerful punch hash, pot and wax) was enough to allow me to keep up with a 25-year-old Spanish dancer who led the way.

And not only did I have fun, but also survived and woke up the next morning with a smile on my face and a few samples left that would make the next day a pleasure too. Quite remarkable, given the whole operation was Scotch-taped together amid city regulatory changes. 

The winds of change in Europe are strengthening. The seed companies who really call the shots in this market are beginning to respond to continental events too.

And tourists, whether they visit for Cannabis Cup or a similarly themed event next year or sometime in the future here, are still in for a treat.

This is a beautiful city, where past and present are ever recombined in new and different ways.

At the heart of this place is the understanding that going with the flow is a way of life here. Cannabis is just another plant. And those who want to enjoy that reality in a place that drips endless beauty everywhere you look, will be able to still find here in the future.

No matter who arranges it or what it is called.

And just one more handy hint? It is also very good when partnered with hot chocolate on a gorgeously crisp late November day.

--Written by Marguerite Arnold for MainStreet
