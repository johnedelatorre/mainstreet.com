10 Spooky Facts About the U.S. Economy

  • Kristin Colella
  • Oct 20, 2011 7:00 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

 

NEW YORK (MainStreet) -- Forget werewolves, zombies and evil spirits. As Halloween nears, nothing could be scarier than the current state of our economy.

Although the country has been out of recession for more than two years, recent statistics are still enough to send chills down the spine – from an unemployment rate higher than 9% to increased poverty numbers and a soaring national debt.

To make matters worse, many experts say we should brace ourselves for more tough times in 2012.

“In the fourth quarter of this year people will probably ramp up spending for the holidays, but when the bills come in and people realize the financial reality of their situation, they’ll probably cut back dramatically in the first three months [of 2012],” says Bernard Baumohl, chief global economist at The Economic Outlook Group. “That could trigger a downturn in the economy.”

Baumohl predicts at least a one-quarter contraction in 2012, and says we might even be “on the edge” of another recession, or two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth.

Read on to discover 10 of the most frightening facts about where we stand right now – and prepare to be spooked.

 

Lack of Jobs

The Bureau of Labor Statistics’ September jobs report offered little encouragement for those hoping for significant economic progress.

“There are some disturbing trends that would suggest that the labor markets are not only weak, but that they are likely to continue to be weak,” says Baumohl.

Unemployment rang in at 9.1% – about what it’s been since April – and those with less education were hit particularly hard. The unemployment rate in September for adults 25 and older with a bachelor’s degree or higher was 4.2%, versus 8.4% for those with some college or an associate’s degree, 9.7% for high school graduates with no college and 14% for those with less than a high school diploma.

“This is the population that really suffers the most when there’s an economic downturn,” says Baumohl. “At a time when employers really have their choice, they’re certainly going to go with the more educated workforce.”

Also disturbing, says Dirk Krueger, a professor of economics at the University of Pennsylvania, is the rising number of long-term unemployed workers. According to the report, the number of those jobless for 27 weeks and more was approximately 6.2 million in September 2011 (44.6% of the unemployed population), versus 6.1 million (41.8% of the unemployed population) the same month in 2010.

Baumohl also says that private-sector hiring has been on a steady decline during each quarter thus far in 2011. “In the first quarter you have an average monthly rate of 191,000 private new jobs being created, in the second quarter that number drops to 138,000 and in third quarter it’s 117,000,” says Baumohl. “This is certainly not a sign that employers are ready to ramp up hiring at this point.”

 

High Gas Prices

Americans have been feeling pain at the pump for the past few years, with gas prices jumping from an average retail price of $1.61 per gallon at the end of 2008 to $3.00 at the end of 2010, according to the Economic Outlook Group.

As of Monday, the average price of regular gas in the U.S. was $3.48 per gallon, according to the Energy Information Administration.

And it doesn’t look as though there’s any relief in sight: The Economic Outlook Group forecasts that prices will hit $3.70 per gallon by the end of 2012. Baumohl adds that “gas prices will likely fluctuate between $3 and $5 a gallon in the next three years. The point is that cheap gas is a thing of the past.”


Declining Household Incomes

It’s hardly surprising that incomes for American families dropped during the recession. According to data collected by two former Census Bureau officials in a report for Sentier Research, median annual income for U.S. households before taxes dropped from $55,309 when the recession officially began in December 2007 to $53,518 in June 2009 – a 3.2% decline.

But what may come as a shock is that in the two years after the recession officially ended in June 2009, median household income continued to plunge, decreasing by more than $3,500 to $49,909 in June 2011. That’s a decline of 6.7% in the first two years of the recovery, which is more than twice the rate of decline experienced during the Great Recession.

According to the report, groups hit particularly hard include the following:

  • Family households with a male or female head and no spouse present (many with children in the household), which declined by 7.3%
  • Households with a head under 25 years old, which declined by 9.5%
  • Households with a head looking for work or on layoff (unemployed), which declined by 18.4%
  • Households with a Black (not Hispanic) head, which declined by 9.4%


“The fact that median household income has dropped just tells you the enormous stress that households find themselves in at this point,” says Baumohl. “It will only lead to further caution in terms of spending, and that is obviously not something that’s going to help the economy.”

 

The Continuing Housing Crisis

The ailing housing industry has taken quite a beating during the past few years, and despite some recent signs of improvement, full recovery still seems a long way off.

On a positive note, according to the Standard & Poor’s Case-Shiller Home Price Index, the housing market showed four straight months of home-value growth through July 2011. And on Wednesday, the U.S. Commerce Department announced a 15% upswing in housing starts – or the number of new residential construction projects – in September. But despite these pieces of good news, the rate of homeownership in the U.S. decreased by 1.1 percentage points, to 65.1%, between 2000 and 2010, according to the latest Census Bureau data. The decrease is the largest posted since the decade between 1930 and 1940.

FICO’s latest quarterly survey of bank risk managers, released on Sept. 30, estimated that a U.S. housing recovery wouldn’t happen before 2020. Specifically, the FICO survey asked bank risk managers if U.S. housing prices would return to 2007 levels before 2020; 49% of the bank managers said no, while 21% answered yes.

What’s more, a new University of Iowa study shows that younger Americans are increasingly shut out of homeownership. The study says that Americans between the ages of 25 and 44 have suffered disproportionately in the economic downturn, and only 57% of that demographic owned their own home in 2007. That’s down from 61% in 1980.

“With banks demanding a far greater down payment than most young people can afford, it keeps home ownership out of reach for many,” says Baumohl. “Since an investment in one’s home historically represented an important source of wealth for Americans over the long term, being deprived of homeownership will slow the accumulation of wealth for this demographic group.”

 

Mortgage Fraud & Default Up

Declining home values have caused many homeowners to stop making payments on their mortgages, despite having the financial capacity to do so. The process is called strategic default, and FICO estimates that it’s a $20 billion problem for mortgage lenders that will likely get worse before it gets better.

As MainStreet has reported, a study from the University of Chicago School of Business said that 35% of mortgage defaults in September 2010 were strategic defaults. In March 2009, that number was just 26%.

“Too many homeowners are walking away or not paying their mortgage just because the value went down and they are under water, yet that was their contractual obligation,” says certified financial planner Laura Scharr-Bykowsky of Ascend Financial Planning. “If everyone just decided to walk away just because they were under water, the downward spiral would continue. This is a huge risk to the system.”

What’s also worrisome is the uptick in mortgage fraud cases. According to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network’s Mortgage Loan Fraud Update, there was an 88% increase in U.S. mortgage fraud cases in the second quarter of 2011 from the same period a year ago. The types of fraud that turned up the most included debt-elimination scams, false mortgage applications and identity theft – revealing the drastic measures people will take in these trying economic times to purchase or keep a home. 


More Student Loan Defaults

Students have been hit particularly hard by the ailing economy, as the grim job market prevented many from earning enough money to stay on top of their student loans. In a September report, the Department of Education reported that default rates for federal student loans had risen in recent years – from 7% in fiscal year 2008 to 8.8% for borrowers whose first loan repayments came due between Oct. 1, 2008, and Sept. 30, 2009, and who defaulted before Sept. 30, 2010. 

Baumohl says that the effect of defaulting can haunt students for years to come. “It’s going to be a big black asterisk on their credit history, which is a terrible thing since they’re just starting out,” he says.

Baumohl adds that it’s no surprise the Occupy Wall Street movement consists of so many young people. “It’s just a reflection of the anger, frustration and exasperation that so many students and graduates are having,” he says.

 

The Increased Poverty Rate

The Census Bureau’s annual report, Income, Poverty and Health Insurance Coverage in the United States, also had some scary findings: Americans living below the poverty line amounted to 15.1% of the population in 2010, up from 14.3% in 2009 and the highest level since 1993.

Since 2007, the poverty rate has increased by 2.6 percentage points, according to the report.

The results are hardly surprising given the nation's high unemployment and underemployment rates. According to the report, since 2007, the number of men working full time year-round with earnings decreased by 6.6 million and the number of corresponding women declined by 2.8 million.

Also unsettling is that the report says that young adults aged 25-34 who are living with their parents had an official poverty rate of 8.4%, but if their poverty status were determined using their own income, 45.3% had an income below the poverty threshold for a single person under 65.

 

Part-Timers on the Rise

The September jobs report found that both the labor force and employment increased in September, but if you look a bit closer, the findings aren’t all rosy. That’s because the number of involuntary part-time workers – or those who were working part-time because their hours had been cut back or because they were unable to find a full-time job – rose to 9.3 million in September.

In August, a little more than 8.8 million people counted themselves among the involuntary part-time workers, which amounts to an increase of more than 400,000 part-timers in a single month.

While any work is better than none in this economy, part-timers often do not get full benefits and must deal with multiple commutes if they are taking on more than one job, which can take a toll on both the individual and their family.

 

Access to Health Care Difficult for Many

According to Gallup’s monthly survey of more than 29,000 adults, the percentage of Americans with health insurance coverage declined 3.6 points in the past three years – from 85.9% in September 2008 to 82.3% in September 2011. 

The survey also found that in September 2011, some 78.3% of those surveyed had a personal doctor and only 64% had visited a dentist in the previous year, down by 4.2 percentage points and 2.6 percentage points since September 2008, respectively. This is likely due to scarce funds for many households as well as the decrease in the percentage of Americans with health insurance.

“While the recession officially ended more than two years ago, the effects on Americans continue to linger,” the study says. 

 

Historic National Debt

After being hotly debated by both houses, Congress voted to raise the debt ceiling to a historic high this summer, and today the national debt stands at a staggering $14.9 trillion. The decision has led many to wonder how the government will reduce this overwhelming debt in the years to come, such as through higher taxes and cuts on government programs.

As part of the debt-ceiling agreement, Congress passed the Budget Control Act of 2011, which called for reducing the deficit by a total of $2.1 trillion over the next decade, about $900 billion of which was approved at the time. The deal created a 12-member bipartisan committee to come up with a plan to reduce the remainder of that sum by Nov. 23, and called for a series of automatic reductions to take effect if the committee fails to come to an agreement.

As MainStreet reported last month, if the committee does fail, the automatic reductions would call for across-the-board cuts for discretionary and non-discretionary spending, with the Congressional Budget Office estimating that defense program cuts would range from 10% in 2013 to 8.5% in 2021, saving some $450 billion; cuts to new spending for non-defense programs would range from 7.8% in 2013 to 5.5% in 2021, saving nearly $300 billion; and Medicare spending would be cut 2% each year, saving $170 billion.

“Everyone is concerned about the historic buildup in public debt, but the bigger worry for me is if Washington rushes to make draconian cuts in government spending now when the economy seems so starved of domestic demand – and so close to slipping into recession,” Baumohl says.

Whatever decisions are made, cuts must come from somewhere, and the American public is likely to feel the pinch.
