Millennials Are Set to Play A Key Role In The Housing Recovery

  • Scott Gamm
  • Sep 29, 2014 8:30 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Even though Millennials are still feeling the impacts of the 2008 recession, their role in the future of the housing market could prove to be indispensable.

Compare Today’s Low Refinance Rates

According to a report from The Demand Institute, Millennials will spend a staggering $1.6 trillion on home purchases and shell out $600 billion on rentals over the next five years.

Plus, the report says there will be 8.3 million new Millennial households created over the next four years, and the majority will buy homes.

Compare Today’s Low Mortgage Rates

Millennials could be a boon to the housing market, which has shown recent signs of softening, both on the sales front.

But economists don’t expect the pent-up demand from Millennials to be released any time soon. Millennials have been suffering from the 2008 recession, which caused a delay in milestones such as marriage and home purchases.

"A fundamental question about millennials is whether their coming of age in the Great Recession has shaped their goals and aspirations to be different from those of previous generations," said Louise Keely, president of The Demand Institute and senior vice president at Nielsen. "We found that, while this generation has many unique characteristics when it comes to their housing choices, they share many of the same intentions as young adults in previous decades. As millennials' economic situations strengthen, their demand will be important drivers of the housing market."

Plus, the 2008 recession included an additional level of complexity for Millennials: student loans.

“Compared to the recession in 2001 or in the early '90s, this recession was vastly more deep and consumer oriented,” said Chris Christopher, director, U.S. macroeconomics and global consumer at¿IHS Economics.

Outstanding student loan balances have eclipsed other types of debt, including credit cards. Plus, such debt can’t be discharged in bankruptcy, causing young people to struggle with making payments for decades following college graduation.

The other financial insecurity facing millennials surrounds wages.

“Keep in mind, median household income adjusted for inflation in 2013 was still about 8% below where it was in 2007,” Christopher added. “We are forecasting that it won’t surpass its previous peak until 2018-2019.”

While the aforementioned report and Christopher’s forecasts seem to indicate light at the end of the tunnel in about five years, that will still be ten years since the 2008 recession.

Millennials could just be the spark the housing market has been asking for. The question is when will that spark ignite and how bright will it be?
