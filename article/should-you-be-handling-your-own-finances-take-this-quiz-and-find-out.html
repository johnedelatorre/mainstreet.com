Should You Be Handling Your Own Finances? Take This Quiz And Find Out

  • Eric Reed
  • May 4, 2015 6:25 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) - Becoming financially independent is one of the cornerstones of growing up. As long as you’re taking cash from someone else to pay the rent, it’s hard to feel like (or in fact be) an adult, even in an era when our parents’ and grandparents' generations still control an overwhelming percentage of the nation’s cash.

Yet while it’s important to earn a living, it’s not necessarily true that along the way we learn how to manage that money properly. In fact, judging by the average scores people get on financial literacy tests, quite a few of us haven’t quite picked up the hang of that just yet.

In other words, lots of people are so bad at managing their own money that they might even be better just handing that off to someone else. Even more troubling, most of them don’t even know it. So, to help our readers decide whether to stay the course or give their affairs to a financial advisor for a little hard-earned discipline, we here at MainStreet have put together a brief quiz. Check the bottom for the answers and decide: should you be handling your own finances?

1. Do you have a household budget?

  • a. Yes, written out and checked against our spending regularly.
  • b. We generally know how much we should spend, but nothing specific.
  • c. Sort of, but we’re not great at sticking to it.
  • d. I live dangerously.

2. Which of the following insurances do you carry?

  • a. Health insurance
  • b. Life insurance
  • c. Professional malpractice insurance
  • d. Car insurance
  • e. Alien abduction insurance

3. How do you use credit cards?

  • a. For the points, I pay it off every month.
  • b. For big purchases, to avoid spending that much cash at once.
  • c. Regularly. I carry a balance month to month, but it’s not too bad.
  • d. Whenever I don’t have enough cash.
  • e. How don’t I use credit cards? Did you know you can pay one off with another credit card? I have five new applications open right now.

4. Do you know how much you spent last week?

  • a. To the dollar. I count bills like Rain Man counts cards. I know how much was in the register at Starbucks this afternoon. I know where their security cameras are… I’ve said too much.
  • b. Generally speaking, within a margin for error.
  • c. No, but I check my account fairly regularly.
  • d. Haven’t the foggiest.
  • e. Joke’s on you! Can’t spend what you don’t have.

5. What’s in your emergency fund?

  • a. A year’s worth of living expenses, better safe than sorry.
  • b. Three to six months’ worth of living expenses.
  • c. One to three months’ of living expenses.
  • d. Rent money for next month if it came to that.
  • e. Jack Daniel's.

6. Have you started planning for retirement yet?

  • a. Yep, I’ve maxed out my retirement contributions at work.
  • b. I set money aside when I can.
  • c. No, but then again I’m only 20.
  • d. No, but then again I’m only 30.
  • e. I’ve got laundry money and half a pack of Skittles

7. What’s the 10 second rule?

  • a. If it’s on the floor for less than 10 seconds, it’s still good to eat!
  • b. Whatever you’re thinking, do it within the next 10 seconds before you have time to change your mind.
  • c. Stop and wait 10 seconds before picking up any purchase to see if it’s just an impulse. Better still, come back again tomorrow if you still want it.
  • d. It’s the five second rule, the amount of time a basketball player has to throw the ball back into play.
  • e. Something to do with porn, right?

8. How much do you make per hour?

  • a. What are you talking about? I’m salaried.
  • b. Minimum wage, plus tips that one time. Which is weird because I work at a Walmart…
  • c. Dividing my paycheck by 8 hours per day comes to…
  • d. Dividing my paycheck by how many hours I spend working each day comes to…
  • e. My 1040 says $1.45. That’s my story and I’m sticking to it.

10. Finish the following sentence: “Get rich…”

  • a. Quick!
  • b. Fast!
  • c. Immediately!
  • d. With lotto tickets!
  • e. Or die trying!

11. What is your monthly overage?

  • a. Almost nothing, I live month to month.
  • b. Less than zero. Whatever’s left goes to debt.
  • c. 10 to 30% of my income.
  • d. More than 30% of my income.
  • e. The result of streaming too many YouTube videos on my cellular plan.

12. What’s the difference between good debt and bad? (Short answer)

___________________________________________

13. What percent of your income goes to housing?

  • a. 60%
  • b. 50%
  • c. 40%
  • d. 30% or less
  • e. Gas money. I sleep in the front seat of my cousin’s Subaru.

14. Finish the following sentence: “Newer is…”

  • a. Better
  • b. Awesome!
  • c. Expensive
  • d. Risky
  • e. All that matters. Did you see that gold plated Apple Watch?

15. You want to start saving money, which of these are good places to start?

  • a. Shut down entertainment spending.
  • b. Review habitual spending.
  • c. Cut out the daily cup of coffee.
  • d. Start reviewing your utility bills.
  • e. I’m thinking blackmail.

16. Which of the following do you have?

  • a. Checking account
  • b. Savings account
  • c. Retirement account
  • d. Investments
  • e. Laundry money and half a pack of Skittles

Answers:

1. The answers are a or b. Spending without a budget is like skydiving without a parachute, just a bad idea.

2. Obviously all good things, except surprisingly for e. In our experience here at MainStreet extraterrestrials and finance rarely mix well.

3. The answer is a or b. Credit is for when you want time, not money. Rewards cards can be a fantastic way to get paid for ordinary purchases, but if you owe more than you can afford to pay it’s time to let the plastic cool off for a while.

4. Answers a through c are all good. No need to be neurotic about it, but if you don’t keep track of your spending how will you know if you’re sticking to the budget?

5. Answers b and c are just right. Everyone should have an emergency fund to cover catastrophic events like job loss or a medical emergency, but don’t keep too much money lying around in cash. Put aside a nice emergency fund, then start investing.

6. The answer is a. It is never too soon to start planning for retirement.

7. The answer is c. No comment on e.

8. The answer is d. You should always know how much you’re making per actual hour, and compare it to how much you think you’re worth. It’s a good test to see whether this is a job for the long haul or a stepping stone in your career.

9. As compound interest gets added to your account it becomes part of the principle. Future cycles of interest are then calculated based on the new balance, which includes both the original principle and accumulated interest. Not all accounts work that way, including many loans.

It is very, very important.

10. No. Just no.

11. Your monthly overage is how much money you have left free and clear at the end of every month. Obviously you want to make this as high as possible, either by spending less or earning more, making c and d the best answers.

12. Good debt is a loan that gets you something that builds equity over time, like a home or student loan. Depending on the terms, it can also cover something you need but can’t afford up front, like financing a car.

Bad debt is a loan for purchases that lose value over time, or that comes with a high interest rate. Consumer debt, payday loans and credit cards are all generally examples of this.

13. The answer is d. Most experts recommend spending no more than 30 percent of your income on housing.

14. The answers are c and d. Nothing wrong with early adopters, but if you’re looking to save money wait for the next generation to come out. Everything is more expensive and prone to error when it’s new.

15. The best answers here are b and d.

It’s not that a and c are bad ideas, but saving money is like dieting: your goal is to build a sustainable lifestyle. Crash courses that make you unhappy, like cutting out entertainment spending or small, daily joys, are hard to sustain in the long run and ultimately less helpful than making meaningful change.

Besides, the goal is to be well off, not miserable.

16. Skittles are awesome, but the answers are quite clearly a through d.
