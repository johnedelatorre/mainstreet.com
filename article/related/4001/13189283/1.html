Investors Rarely Trade Their 401(k)s, Even During Recent Selloffs

  • Ellen Chang
  • Feb 10, 2016 4:36 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

Investors rarely trade or even rebalance their 401(k) accounts, and their complacency could be saving them from making hasty decisions such as rushing to divest their assets by following the herd during the recent market selloffs.

Prompted by fear and following the masses, some investors do fall prey to the hype and sell their stocks at the wrong time, only to sit on the sidelines for too long. Whether they wind up moving their money into bonds or cash, many wait for the market to rebound before getting back into it. Being driven by greed is never a good reason to get back into the market, especially if it turns out to be at a high point.

The majority of employees chose mutual funds among a limited offering and refrain from making any trades unlike investors with IRA accounts. Only 10% of employees traded within their 401(k) accounts during 2014, which mirrors the behavior of employees during the past decade with a continued decline in trading, said Jean Young, a senior research analyst at Vanguard, a Valley Forge, Pa.-based investment company and the largest provider of 401(k) plans in the U.S., according to a survey from Pensions & Investments. In 2014, only 0.6% of assets shifted to fixed income, with most people making only small changes to their portfolios.

In that year, only 0.3% of all participants abandoned equities completely and moved from a portfolio with some equity exposure to a portfolio with none. Some of the decline is due to the fact that more people are investing in target date funds, since only 2% of employees who owned a single target date fund traded in 2014.

Despite the continued market volatility, these trends are expected to continue, Young said.

“In the period we are in now, employees might not know what to do,” she said. “Investors should avoid panicking, because they could be locking in a loss and timing the market is very difficult. Even if they are not comfortable with their current asset allocation, they should ride this out and wait until there is a calmer market.”

Why Lack of Trading is Not Good

While infrequent trading saves investors from making hasty and impetuous decisions, another risk is involved. The downside of employees not making changes in their 401(k) accounts is that they become too comfortable to the extent where they do not even rebalance their portfolio, said David Twibell, president of Englewood, Colo.-based Custom Portfolio Group.

The portfolio can wind up being overweighted in equities after long periods of gains in the market, neglecting diversification. Instead, portfolios should be rebalanced and examined a couple of times each year.

“It’s not perfect, but this approach will generally keep most investors on the right path and help them avoid major losses,” Twibell said.

Some investors are afraid of a pullback in the market and believe the best offense is a good defense by selling everything and then regrouping, said Carl Sera, a portfolio manager with Covestor, the online investing marketplace, and research director at Sera Capital Management, a registered investment advisor in Annapolis, Md. Following this strategy has its fallacies because these investors believe they will return back to the market “when things are better,” but this turns into a form of market timing.

“Unfortunately, they have no way of knowing when ‘things are better’ and by selling without a re-entry plan, they seldom win the stock market game,” Sera said.

Selling when the market is down instead of when returns are rising is also common, especially for younger investors. These investors are “blustery when markets are only down 10%, but when markets are down an additional 30% to 40%, they turn into Chicken Little and sell at even lower prices,” Sera said. These buy and hold investors bought into the strategy, but do not know how to react when a downturn occurs.

401(k) Trade Restrictions

Restrictions on the number of trades investors can make depend on the rules dictated by the mutual fund companies. If you buy and sell the same fund within a 60-day or 90-day period, it is deemed a round-trip and investors often receive a “nasty gram, telling you that you are a really bad person and you shouldn’t do that again,” said Bill DeShurko, a portfolio manager with Covestor, the online investing company, and founder of 401 Advisor, a registered investment adviser in Centerville, Ohio. The plan will often lock investors out of their accounts and not allow any trades for a period of time, he said.

Providers of 401(k) plans can deny access investors to their own money, which is a larger and more egregious issue than paying higher fund expenses, said DeShurko. An investor might sell Fund A in January, because he is nervous, but when his sentiment about the market rises by February, he buys Fund A again. Since this constitutes as one round-trip, the mutual fund will send out a chastising note, he said.

Let's say this investor holds onto the fund until September and has a trip planned where there is no Internet access and sells Fund A and goes into cash to be safe. After the vacation, if the investor buys Fund A once again, that means this counts as two round-trips and the account will be frozen for awhile.

“The investor could buy Fund B back instead of Fund A and avoid the issue,” DeShurko said. “But what if Fund A is the lowest cost index fund and that is what you want to own?”

Another issue which arises during periods of market volatility or declines in returns is that some employees will stop their contributions as their balances start to decline. Following this theory is a fallacy and the “worst” decision, because investors should “never, ever stop contributing up to the company match because the tax deduction is worth it,” DeShurko said. These consistent contributions help employees take advantage of dollar cost averaging by “forcing” investors to accumulate more shares at lower prices.

A market downturn provides opportunities for investors to purchase investments they have been eyeing up for a long time, said Matt Sadowsky, director of retirement at TD Ameritrade, the Omaha, Neb.-based investment company.

“Market volatility can be your friend or your enemy,” he said. “It can give you multiple opportunities to enter and exit. If you have a long time before retirement, it is not recommended that you check your account balance every day to avoid the panic and unease from market volatility.”
