$1 Is Here, But Not For Long

  • Ellen Chang
  • Feb 11, 2016 4:47 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

The volatility in crude oil prices amid the global glut has increased the downward pressure on gasoline prices with one station in Kansas dropping to under $1 a gallon on Wednesday.

Nusser Motors/Oil in Jetmore, Kan. lowered its prices to $0.99 a gallon as gasoline prices in 45 states such as New York also fell below $2 to $1.99 for the first time since 2008. In the U.S., 30% of gas stations are selling at $1.50 a gallon while the 7-11 convenience store in Norman, Okla. is enticing drivers with $1.08 gasoline, said Patrick DeHaan, a senior petroleum analyst at GasBuddy.com, a Boston-based provider of retail fuel pricing information and data.

Prices have plummeted by over 70% since 2014, reaching record lows due to concerns of a global slowdown led by China, sparking unrelenting declines and generating immense volatility.

The potential of gasoline prices weakening to a mere $0.99 per gallon throughout the U.S. is not likely at all despite the fact that prices have fallen precipitously low, such as at a Speedway gas station in Fraser, Mich., where prices dipped to $1.06, he said.

“If last week the odds were 1 in 100, they’ve now jumped to 1 in 100,000,” DeHaan said. “The inventory is still high and there is so much supply.”

Prices, though, should continue their downward trend, because too many bearish factors such as the massive amount of crude oil and gasoline for prices to start rallying.

“They have to get rid of the winter gas,” he said. “There is a big disconnect in the market. The market is getting whiplash.”

The volatility is evident as Chicago spot prices dropped by $0.05 a gallon on Monday and $0.13 on Tuesday, but reversed course on Monday and jumped by $0.18, a 41% move.

As refiners start to switch gradually to a lighter, summer blend, prices tend to move higher as long as long as crude oil prices do not decline further, said Timothy Hess, a lead analyst for the Energy Information Administration, the independent statistical arm of the Department of Energy based in Washington, D.C. The EIA expects the monthly average price of gasoline to reach a seven-year low of $1.82 a gallon in February before climbing during the spring.

“We’re just heading into that time of year from the first quarter to the second quarter with the typical seasonal fluctuations,” he said.

Volatility in crude oil prices remains high, and despite daily price fluctuations, the current prices are expected to be the lowest, said Hess. Any declines in prices of Brent crude, the global benchmark, or West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude, the U.S. benchmark, will encounter a six week lag to reach consumers. While a 10-cent decline on the wholesale market might occur in one trading session, prices at gas stations might decline a couple of cents the first few weeks, but the remaining decline will take several weeks, he said.

Brent crude oil prices averaged $31 a barrel in January, a $7 a barrel decrease from December and the lowest monthly average price since December 2003, said the EIA. In 2015, Brent crude oil prices averaged $52 a barrel, down $47 a barrel from the average in 2014. In 2016, Brent crude oil prices are forecast to average $38 a barrel and $50 a barrel in 2017. The forecast for WTI crude oil prices are expected to average the same as Brent in both years.

In 2016, regular gasoline price is estimated to average $1.98 a gallon and $2.21 a gallon in 2017, compared with $2.43 a gallon in 2015. In January, the average retail regular gasoline price was $1.95 a gallon, and the first time monthly gasoline prices averaged below $2 a gallon since March 2009.

With the national average price dipping to $1.73 a gallon, prices are likely to remain below $2 at least until the summer driving season when demand picks up, said Bernard Weinstein, associate director of the Maguire Energy Institute at Southern Methodist University’s Cox School of Business in Dallas.

“Crude oil and gasoline storage are at record levels and OPEC continues to pump full out while U.S. production has dropped only modestly,” he said.

Where Consumers Are Spending Savings

The long, gradual decline of gas prices since the second half of 2014 means drivers are unmoved by additional dips, said DeHaan.

“People have gotten used to it and don’t notice it," DeHaan said. "They either pocket it or save it, and very few are spending it.”

The average household saved $660 in 2015 from lower fuel prices and is predicted to save $300 this year, said Hess. Gasoline consumption rose slightly in 2015 by 3% from the previous year after a trend of flat to declining in recent years. The amount of consumption from drivers so far this year has declined and can be attributed to the inclement and frigid weather, he said.

When prices dropped in previous years, consumers spent their extra disposable income on goods and services, but this time around, the majority of consumers are using the extra money to pay down debt, said Weinstein.

“In fact, household balance sheets are in their best shape since before the Great Recession,” he said. “Lower fuel costs, combined with cheap financing also propelled new car and light truck sales to record levels last year.”

Retail sales are projected to increase by 3.1%, eclipsing the ten-year average of 2.7%, excluding sales of cars and at restaurants and gas stations, according to the National Retail Federation. Lower unemployment and gas prices coupled with higher wages are giving consumers “more discretionary income to save, pay down debt and spend on travel, eating out and personal services,” said Jack Kleinhenz, chief economist of the NRF, a Washington, D.C-based retail trade association.

Why $1 Oil Is Not Sustainable

Prices dipping to $1 a gallon across the country for an extended period is not feasible, because aside from the cost of crude oil, three other components including taxes, the margin for refining and the margin for the retail and distribution segment of the market contribute to the overall price, said Hess.

During the last four years, the three other fees averaged $0.67 per gallon along the Gulf Coast during the first quarter of the year with the lowest being the first quarter of 2014 at $0.60 per gallon.

Retail gasoline dipping to $1 per gallon means crude oil would have to sell for $0.33 per gallon at $14 a barrel in the average year or $0.40 per gallon or $17 per barrel when refining and retail margins are low, he said.

“Right now margins are above where they’ve been the past few years,” he said. “EIA doesn’t forecast crude prices getting to that level.

The taxes imposed by each state vary and in states such as New York and California where they are higher, it may not be plausible. The national average tax rate for gasoline is higher at $0.50 per gallon compared to $0.384 in Texas.
