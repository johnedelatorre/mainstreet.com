Sin City Grants Plenty of Grow Licenses: Las Vegas Gambles on Weed

  • Juliette Fairley
  • Oct 2, 2014 12:19 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — With more than 25 million tourists a year, Las Vegas is poised to be one of the top marijuana tourism destinations in the world once medicinal and recreational use is legal.

“The whole environment is geared to adults having a good time,” said Aaron Herzberg, marijuana legal strategist with Electrum Partners based Irvine, Calif. “Tourists spend money on gambling, going out to clubs, strip bars, drinking, and I imagine a large amount of people will add marijuana to that list of activities to do when visiting Vegas.”

But for now only medicinal use has been approved.

“The motivation of dispensary applicants is that Nevada will have full recreational by 2016 and the business opportunity for that is through the roof,” Herzberg said.

With his legal background, Herzberg assisted several hopefuls with compliance and legal review, legal entity and equity structure, tax issues, land use permitting and developing policy and procedures.

“Locating properties was a huge burden in a short period of time,” he said. More than 520 applications were received by the State of Nevada, and 70% were in Clark County where the city of Las Vegas is located.

“I saw people applying without knowing the extent of the commitment they need to make,” said Leslie Bocskor, founding chairman with the Nevada Cannabis Industry Association (NCIA). “The costs associated with assembling one of these applications in a comprehensive way can approach a million dollars or more and that is all risk capital. Then if you win a license, you need the people and money to execute the business model.”

Nevada differs from Colorado and Washington state in its legalization, because it will honor medical marijuana identity cards and recommendations that are issued by physicians from other states.

“Nevada is clearly more competitive based on merit,” Herzberg told MainStreet. “They have a whole ranking system on which they grant licenses to dispensaries. Whoever is ranked highest is likely to get approved.”

The maximum score of 250 points is determined from a combination of financial resources, experience, community impact, plans for education, security and transportation and tax paying history in Nevada for contributions made to the state.

“Each municipality had different deadlines, and depending on where you were applying, you had six to eight weeks to put together a 600 page document,” Herzberg said. While only 18 dispensary applications were passed on to the state from the Clark County Commission, nearly 100 grow facilities were approved.

“Grow applications experienced an easier process,” said Steve Sisolak, chairman of the Clark County Commission in Nevada. “Their locations are not as spread out because of where our warehouses are located. There is one particular area of the county that has an enormous amount of warehouses which is where grows will be located.”

In fact, the total number of square feet applied for cultivation sites in Nevada is three million.

“But the state has determined that the need for grows is only a million square feet,” said Herzberg. “Nevada state officials need to decide whether they will impose a cap on the number of cultivation facilities that are approved.”

Even if they are approved, however, Herzberg expects many grow applicants to drop out.
