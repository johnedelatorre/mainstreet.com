Washington's Marijuana Miasma, Taxes and Regulation Make State's Legalization Effort Go to Pot

  • Michael Tremoglie
  • Nov 3, 2014 3:00 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) - According to the state government, legal pot sales in Washington are valued at $26,052,900 year-to-date. The excise taxes due are $6,513,225. Sure, this is less than what was projected before the law was enacted. But this is not the only problem.

"Well [revenue] is less than projected," said Frank Cikutovich, a Spokane attorney who is one the recommended attorneys by National Organization to Reform Marijuana Laws. "But what they did not anticipate when they issued the licenses was that it was going to take them three to six months to get their crops going."

"There is a big supply and demand problem," he continued. "Meanwhile, all the business is going to the medical dispensaries which do not pay taxes. Even though you have to get paperwork to buy medical marijuana, you can get the paperwork for nearly anything. It is pretty much a farce. The law was it not written this way but this is the way it is. "

Cikutovich also noted that the black market is flourishing incredibly because of high taxes on recreational marijuana. He observed that there are black marketeers selling to regular pot smokers - defined as a recreational user that uses it the same way it uses alcohol or pain management or sleep management. These recreational users purchase pot on the black market to avoid taxes.

"For example, I have an app on my phone that tells me what marijuana is selling for," said Cikutovich. "There is a $9 per gram sale on to today at the dispensaries. But the state stores are averaging $40 per gram - because they have to sell the product after it has been taxed on the growing and then taxed again on the wholesale."

"Tax revenues will probably rise when more stores open (and prices fall), but so far it's a fraction of what was expected," said Kevin Sabet, co-founder of Project SAM.

He noted that tax revenue estimates were at $150 million to $200 million for 2015, according to BOTEC Analysis Corp., a Los Angeles-based company that helps public and private organizations "find solutions in the public interest for the problems of drug policy and crime control."

He also said the licensing of recreational marijuana stores has been a problem.

"The Washington Liquor Control Board first decided to measure the 1,000 feet by 'common path of travel,'" he said. "Under pressure from federal prosecutors, the LCB changed to a more restrictive 'as the crow flies' interpretation. The yellow zones tightened, and so there are far fewer stores in Seattle than there would normally be. So it's been much more slower going, and activists are upset."

But there is one huge factor that is going to affect the market - the licensing of medical marijuana stores.

Jake Ellison, a reporter for the Seattle Post-Intelligencer, told MainStreet that the city of Seattle issued a rule requiring medical marijuana businesses to have a state permit. But this rule was enacted in anticipation that the state would pass a law requiring a permit. The legislature, however, fell a "couple of votes short at the last minute." Ergo, there is no permit for medical marijuana stores to obtain.

But that did not stop Seattle's Department of Planning and Processing from sending out letters to 330 dispensaries with what is essentially a get-a-permit-or-close ultimatum.

The city extended the time limit to get the permits. The rule is that major medical marijuana businesses - defined as those growing more than 45 plants or having more than 72 ounces of usable marijuana on hand - have to get a state license. But the city council did not put the process on hold until they are on the same page as the state. So the medical marijuana owners have a deadline.

Cikutovich speculated that at some point - because all the medical marijuana dispensaries are technically illegal - the state will shut them down. This will transfer all the business to the taxed retail outlets.

"The black market is sitting back loving it," he added.
