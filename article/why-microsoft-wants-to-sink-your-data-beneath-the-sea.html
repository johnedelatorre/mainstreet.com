Why Microsoft Wants to Sink Your Data Beneath the Sea

  • Erik Baard
  • Feb 5, 2016 5:07 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

Microsoft might be sinking your cloud. The company’s special research division, which works on breakthrough technologies, recently concluded a test of its first seabed data center. The innovation aims to bring the data demands of coastal cities together with ocean currents that provide reliable cooling and electricity generation. It might also give users a faster computing experience.

Microsoft Research’s Project Natick could represent the convergence of two epoch-shaping realities in computing. The first is the massive shift of data storage and processing on to cloud computing’s remote servers; last quarter Microsoft reported that revenues at its Intelligent Cloud division were up $6.3 billion, or 11%. The company operates more than 100 data centers to support the traditional Internet and emerging “Internet of Things.” The second is the cooling off of Moore’s Law, which for decades projected rapidly doubling of computational power owing to smaller and smaller transistors on integrated circuits. Gordon E. Moore, an Intel co-founder for whom the observation was named (it’s not an actual scientific law), said last year that he sees silicon chip progress soon hitting the physical walls inherent in the materials used and the finite velocity of light.

“With the end of Moore’s Law, the cadence at which servers are refreshed with new and improved hardware in the data center is likely to slow significantly," said Ben Cutler, Project Natick Manager, with Microsoft Research NExT (New Experience and Technologies)."We see this as an opportunity to field long-lived, resilient data centers that operate ‘lights out’ – nobody on site – with very high reliability for the entire life of the deployment, possibly as long as ten years.”  Cutler adds that his small Natick data centers will use fewer materials and will be “fully recycled.”

The Natick prototype was placed 30’ deep in the Pacific, about a half mile off of central California. Proximity to where data demands are concentrated -- think a short sail from Wall Street -- would also give a speed boost that new chips might no longer deliver. Networks are defined by bandwidth, or capacity, and latency, which is the amount of time it takes for a packet of information to travel across it. Half of all people, or three-quarters of all large cities, are under 40 miles from the sea, according to the UN Environment Programme. In a Natick world, those billions would be clustered near their briney data.

“Often you need smaller data centers at the edge of the network, close to population centers, to ensure quality of service for end users,” said Adam Wierman, Ph.D., professor of computer science and executive officer for computing and mathematical sciences at the California Institute of Technology (CalTech). But around cities regulations, real estate costs and other challenges hamper development. “Using the ocean may be a win if the costs work out because it can overcome many of these issues,” he said. He added that as self-contained units, Natick modules should be relatively easy to deploy.

Given the inaccessibility of a Natick module, they’d better be as reliable and redundant as bricks. While the lone experimental module had monthly checkups by divers during its August to November 2015 run, commercial models would be installed in groups and are conceived as maintenance-free. “An undersea data center comprises a collection of vessels, each containing computers," Cutler said. "Computers are not swapped out but rather are decommissioned over time. When the end of life of an entire vessel is reached, the vessel is replaced with a new vessel, and the old vessel brought back to shore for refurbishment, which includes installation of new computers.”

Naticks have rounded surfaces, which resist colonization by organisms and corrosion better than sharp corners, and have no moving parts that can get gummed up. For security purposes, they’ve also got what amounts to impressive motes. Besides, the data centers would be in shallower waters than transoceanic cables, which are proven to be durable in their service as the depth-darkened superhighways of the Internet.

Another aspect of security is reliable energy. According to a Natural Resources Defense Council study, U.S. data centers will use 139 billion kilowatt-hours by 2020, or three New York Cities' worth. Electricity transmission grid snafus and seesawing energy prices spook information technology planners. Solar is too weak and intermittent, and reliant on even feebler energy storage, to provide full on-site generation for data centers. Naticks can be installed alongside offshore wind generators of the kind advocated by New York City by former Mayor Michael Bloomberg. Using the ocean itself, data centers could be powered by waves, tides or especially currents.

But a kilowatt saved is even better than a kilowatt well sourced. Take cooling needs, which university and industrial engineers interviewed agree remains a factor, though a diminishing concern as data center designers opt for systems that can operate at higher temperatures.

“The chips in data centers run quite hot -- too hot to touch -- and there is increasing interest in packing them ever denser,” Cutler said. The trend isn’t quite like lighting, where incandescent bulbs that cast off wasted energy as heat have yielded to cooler compact fluorescent bulbs and light emitting diodes. Burning fossil fuels to meet cooling needs would release carbon dioxide, which warms the atmosphere and oceans, and increases the acidity of oceans, damaging shellfish and corals. For fiscal and sustainability reasons, technologists are increasingly turning to water. One current approach is converting old mills and shoreline power plants (look to rapidly retiring coal-fired ones especially for this) into data centers, retaining their secure locations and cooling water intake systems. But each retrofit must be customized, so “a one-size-fits-all approach would certainly be appealing,” Wierman said.

Google has also taken to the seas, experimenting with floating data centers with components that reach toward the depths for cooling. Plunking a data center into the ocean might be a more efficient solution.

“Microsoft was always in the front in taking on operational cost reduction and didn’t shy from experimenting with extreme ideas," said Hana Godrich, Ph.D., an assistant research professor of electrical and computer engineering at Rutgers University. "While this research is in a very early stage, it is definitely an important one.” Godrich worked as a consultant and designer for data centers for ten years and focuses her research on sustainable energy applications. It’s on that front that she sees cause for caution. “These solutions might produce sustainability issues as the heat warms the water in its vicinity,” she said.

Andrew DeVogelaere, Ph.D., oversees the research program at the Monterey Bay National Marine Sanctuary. He dims Wieman’s optimism about the ease of making underwater site selections. Natick placements could get snagged in seaweeds of red tape over concerns about paints and coatings and the introduction of manmade structures into wild habitat, he noted. Much like accidentally sunken shipping containers or artificial reefs, “these may be innocuous in some locations, but problematic in others.” He anticipates Naticks will be “dealt with on a case by case basis,” he said, despite acknowledging their merits. Ancient corals can’t be replaced any more easily than old growth forests, and squids need soft sediment surfaces to spawn. Moreover, a Natick’s “outer casing could serve as stepping stones across soft sediment habitats for invasive species,” he said. Active seafloors and canyons could quickly turn today’s technological marvel into litter, he added.

“There are considerations about the type of material placed, where it is placed, and there should be plans to remove the equipment before it becomes debris,” DeVogelaere said.

Wierman will be watching. “It's hard to say how practical [the Natick] is without knowing more about the costs and reliability of the devices," Wierman said. "At this point, it seems like a compelling experiment, but the cost analysis will be where the rubber meets the road.” Or more gingerly, the seabed.
