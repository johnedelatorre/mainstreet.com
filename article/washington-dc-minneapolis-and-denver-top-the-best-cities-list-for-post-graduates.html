Washington, D.C., Minneapolis and Denver Top The Best Cities List For Post-Graduates

  • Juliette Fairley
  • Apr 30, 2015 9:44 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Matthew Eskarous works as a vice president at a bank in Manhattan, but he lives in North Brunswick, N.J. near Rutgers University, his alma mater where he earned a degree in economics.

“I don't like the commute,” Eskarous told MainStreet. 
 
 The 37-year-old rented an apartment in Manhattan last summer for $2,700 and has decided to relocate.

“I’m looking to sell my house and move into the city,” said Eskarous. “I don’t want to rent it because of the hassle of handling tenants. I just want to be done with it.”

But the new crop of graduates joining the real world this spring could learn from Eskarous's mistakes. Increased job potential was ranked as the primary motivation driving Millennials to move away from their college towns, according to a Rent.com survey.  

"It doesn't surprise me at all that job potential is the number one factor prompting millennials to move," said Paul Angone, author of All Groan Up: Searching For Self, Faith and a Freaking Job! (HarperCollins, 2015).  "Millennials need work and don't care if that means moving to a location they never dreamed of to make it happen."

Rent.com’s list of the top ten cities for post-grads includes Washington, D.C., Minneapolis, Denver, San Francisco and Boston but not New York City. That’s because Washington D.C. offers up 87 jobs per capita compared to New York’s 33 jobs per capita. On top of its greater job offerings, D.C. is also more affordable.
 
“While rent isn’t necessarily cheap in our nation’s capital, it by far surpasses the affordability of New York City,” said Niccole Schreck, senior brand manager with Rent.com. “In D.C., you can get a two-bedroom apartment for a little over $3,000 whereas the median cost for a two bedroom in New York City is upwards of $6,000.”
 
In addition to job availability, the best cities for post grads offer food and nightlife activities and a high concentration of other Millennials.

"Many 20-something-year-olds want to live in urban cities, because it is easier for them to rent," said Sue Nelson, a commercial and residential real estate expert. 
 
While Washington, D.C. is home to major industries, such as the federal government, education and tourism, Boston also has industries that make it an attractive place to live for post grads.
 
"Millennials have become the 21st century nomads, traveling great lengths to find job opportunities that have gone scarce in too many locations," Angone told MainStreet. 
 
Those seeking a career in aerospace, marijuana or telecommunications might opt for living in Colorado, a mountainous region. 
 
“Denver is known for its picturesque scenery, liberal government and outdoor activities,” Schreck told MainStreet. “Denver is also less expensive than D.C. but more expensive than Minneapolis-St. Paul."

Inevitably, post-grads will gravitate toward the cities that make the most sense for their goals and their personalities.
Rounding out the top ten list are Austin, Seattle, Raleigh, Baltimore and Cincinnati.

— Written for MainStreet by Juliette Fairley
