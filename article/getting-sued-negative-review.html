Getting Sued for a Negative Review

  • Hal M. Bundrick
  • May 23, 2014 10:41 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Businesses burned by bad reviews are fighting back. Online review sites like Yelp, TripAdvisor and Angie's List allow crowd sourced critiques to guide the purchasing decisions for millions of consumers. But while it's one thing to say the bed was too lumpy or the salad was wilted, it's quite another to claim "the owner smokes weed." Anonymous or not, libel is a crime.

Also See: Before You Get Sued -- Read Prior to Posting at Yelp, TripAdvisor

One of the latest lawsuits to be filed in such a matter is a $74,500 defamation claim by an Oregon hotel against a customer critic who posted a scathing review on TripAdvisor, according to the Oregonian. Anonymous writer "12Kelly" said the housekeeping staff was "either high or drunk," the front office attendant was having phone sex while on duty and the owner smoked marijuana. The author of the review is still unknown but will be revealed and named as a defendant in the suit once identified.

And it is not just restaurants and hotels that are fighting back against bad reviews. University of Wisconsin-Whitewater communications professor Sally Vogl-Bauer is suing a former graduate student for allegedly launching a "malicious" campaign attacking her teaching abilities, deployed through various websites, blogs and YouTube, according to the Janesville GazetteXtra.

Also See: The TripAdvisor Blackmail Epidemic

The dividing point in what defines a fair review from defamation is the difference between opinion and facts, according to one legal expert.

"Opinions cannot be the basis of a defamation claim, but facts can," attorney Travis Crabtree says on legal news website JDSupra. "That distinction can be a difficult one to make when in the middle of an online rant, so the main point is that reviewers should be able to back-up any factual claim, or claim that can be considered factual. Calling a dry cleaner lousy won't get you in trouble. Saying they refused to return your shirt because they were wearing it can land you in court. Calling them thieves gets a little trickier."

But even with the facts on your side, you can still be sued warns Jeffrey Lewis, an attorney for Broedlow Lewis in California.

"Even if your online review is completely true, you can still find yourself named as a defendant in a defamation action," Lewis writes on JDSupra. "Even if you prevail at the end of years of litigation, you may not recover all of your costs and attorney's fees and you certainly will never recover the time, stress and aggravation of being sued. Many states have enacted protection to weed out frivolous lawsuits, such as California's anti-SLAPP statute. These legal protections allow an online reviewer, in some cases, to quickly obtain a dismissal of a lawsuit and get the reviewer's attorney's fees paid for. However, the protections do not prevent an online reviewer from being sued in the first place and there is always the risk that an anti-SLAPP dismissal is not obtained quickly.

Lewis offers some advice for would-be ranters to consider before they pull the trigger on a caustic comment:

"Before posting a negative online review, consider running a Google search for the following four words: online review defamation lawsuit. The results suggest that the 'benefits' of posting a negative online review are usually far outweighed by the economic and other costs of being named a defendant in a lawsuit."

--Written by Hal M. Bundrick for MainStreet
