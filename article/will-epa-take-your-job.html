Will The EPA Take Your Job?

  • Eric Reed
  • Jun 23, 2014 6:33 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Recently the Environmental Protection Agency announced a series of major new regulations designed to combat global warming. Passed under the mandate of the Clean Air Act, these new rules require states to reduce carbon dioxide emissions from coal plants 30% by 2030. By almost anyone's account this is great news for the environment. How good is this news, however, for a still struggling labor market?

As usual, most politicians' answers depends on whose flag they fly, Democrat or Republican. The truth is far more complicated. Will the EPA regulations cost or create jobs? It will mostly depend on how states choose to meet the new regulations. The administration has taken a similar approach to clean air as it did to health care, allowing each state to choose how best to regulate its own industries.

"It would be next to impossible to impose a single flat emissions target across every state," said Jeremiah Johnson, a professor with the School of Natural Resources at the University of Michigan. "[Instead] the EPA looked at four categories of carbon reduction opportunities that the states could approach."

Each state can apply some combination of four general approaches to meet the new targets: improving efficiency of existing power plants (production side efficiency), switching fuel sources away from coal, increasing reliance on renewable energy and reducing power use (demand side efficiency). The EPA hasn't even imposed the same reduction standards on every state, since each has different energy issues. The 30% regulation is cumulative across every state.

"A single carbon emission standard would be already achieved in some states and next to impossible in others," Johnson said. "So some states will see targets that are more aggressive and some states will see targets that are less."

Adding to the uncertainty is the fact that the new rules will also allow for interstate carbon trading, meaning that even individual targets aren't necessarily predictable over the long run.

"The buzzword that we're hearing from the EPA is 'flexibility,'" said Johnson. "The states have a lot of flexibility with what they allow and how they define their program." Of course, all programs will still need to meet with EPA approval.

This is the problem with those who claim certainty about whether the new EPA rules will create or destroy jobs: how new regulations impact the labor market depends entirely on implementation. We simply don't yet know what these rules will look like on the ground.

For example, as the Economic Policy Institute's Josh Bivens points out, jobs might suffer if states simply make electricity more expensive across the board. Higher energy costs drive up the price of virtually everything in the marketplace, including labor, reducing demand for it. As households consume fewer, now more expensive, products and the tasks and tools associated with work get more expensive, employment would drop overall.

That's one scenario. Bivens is careful to note, however, that it matters quite a lot how those energy prices go up. For example if a state institutes new efficiency, inspection and maintenance rules on coal fired plants, companies will have to invest in workers and equipment in order to comply. This would create jobs to offset, either in whole or in part, jobs lost to rising prices. Or a state could double down its investments in renewable energy. This isn't always practical, as not every state has equal access to sources of renewable power; however, where facilities like wind or solar could replace traditional power supplies this could become significant sources of jobs. Creating new infrastructure not only generates employment from direct hiring but also from the ripple impact on industry suppliers and a growing consumer base.

These are just three examples. States could adopt some combination of these approaches, or none of the above, but they speak to a broader point. There is no way of knowing in a vacuum what the long term impact of the new EPA rules will be. State specific proposals can and certainly will be analyzed, but we have to wait for them to come out.

Just as importantly, we also have to consider that any job losses are set against a program to fight global warming. One of the major problems with the minority Republican platform has been its dedication to the idea of getting something for nothing, from tax breaks to health care. Policies are often ridiculed if they come at a cost, as though that alone were a deal breaker.

But, as the ancient Greeks said, nothing comes from nothing. Global warming left unchecked will have catastrophic consequences measured not just in treasure but in misery and human lives. While new rules may result in job losses, although far from a certainty, the benefits of these regulations still need to be measured against the greater harm they're trying to prevent. When the next generation looks to retire to the Florida Keys, it would be nice if we still had them.

For now, however, that cost remains purely speculative. We'll have to wait for the specific proposals before reasonable predictions can begin.
