The Top 10 Ways to Protect Your Personal Data in an Era of Epidemic Hacking

  • Robert McGarvey
  • Jan 20, 2015 7:30 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) —Sony. Home Depot. J. P. Morgan Chase. The list of hack victims keeps growing, and what this means is that your data is too often in the hands of the bad guys.

A more frightening question: have you been personally hacked? Are cyber criminals monitoring your every keystroke? That is what is happening to many millions of us, and of course the victims do not know it, not until their bank accounts are emptied by crooks who know the username and password because they copied it when the victim logged in.

The good news: take just ten steps, and you will be vastly safer. A few are do’s, a few are don’t’s, but none of the steps is burdensome.

For starters: never use obvious - dumb - passwords such as 12345678 or “My Password,” urged John Buzzard, a security expert with FICO, the credit scoring company. The reason: there are cheap tools that automate attempted criminal logins, and these tools usually are programmed to first try the obvious passwords. That means, if you use such passwords, a 15-year-old kid in Kiev can hack your account because you, in effect, turned over the keys.

Use many different passwords and store them, not on a piece of paper, but in a password manager app, advised Al Pascual, a security expert with Javelin Strategy + Research. There are two points here. First: when you use the same password everywhere, if XYZ gets hacked and you are a user there, the criminal now knows how to log into many of your other accounts - and those same automated hacking tools are set up to try on multiple sites username/password combinations already known to work at one site. That is why, when you read about a successful hack at a site where you are a user, it is mandatory that you go everywhere where you used the same login and change it immediately. It also is why you should use unique passwords, especially at important sites such as your bank.

Does it matter if you use the same password at every newspaper site? Probably not. But when your money is on the line, up the security with a one-off password.

As for the password manager, when a user gets above, say, a dozen sites, remembering log-ins just won’t happen. Download any popular password manager and use it.

While you are at that, PIN protect your phone. Data from credit reporting company Experian shows that three in ten smartphones are not PIN protected. That is crazy, especially with the proliferation of mobile payment tools and other ways to move money with a phone. Set up a PIN under SETTINGS.

Make it a habit to change passwords every 90 days or so, advised Pascual. Yes, it’s a bother but know that security-conscious organizations typically require password resets every 30 days. The advantage: if a hacker gets your log-in, there’s a chance that by the time he tries it, the password will already have been changed.

Beef up passwords by using multifactor authentication wherever it is offered, advised Steve Weisman, a lawyer who blogs at Spamicide.com. Log into Gmail from an unfamiliar device and, if the user has set up multi-factor, an SMS will be sent to a registered cellphone with a one-time use code. Type it into Gmail, and only then is access granted. It adds some seconds to logins, but it dramatically ups the break-in difficulty for a hacker. More sites will offer multifactor, because there is wide recognition that the password system we now use is broken and we have no alternative in plain view. We are stuck with passwords for some time, perhaps another decade and, until then, multifactor is a good addition.

When using ATM cards, always use one hand to cover what the other is doing when you enter your PIN. So-called skimmers instal equipment designed to capture the data on your card’s magnetic stripe, and with that plus your PIN, they can make a counterfeit of your card and empty your bank account. The mag stripe data minus the PIN just is a lot less lethal.

Advice from just about every expert is: do not click on attachments sent by unknown senders. Never. Much of the time those attachments carry a malware payload, and that is how hackers take over the computers of ordinary citizens. Often, anti-virus tools simply do not catch the malware, because good malware has continually changing signatures, making it hard for anti-virus to keep up. Simpler is: do not open attachments. And be very skeptical even when clicking on attachments sent by friends and colleagues. Did they really send it? Or has their email account been hacked?

Do not use public WiFi for financial activity such as online banking. Never. Coffee shop and hotel WiFi are great for checking stock quotes and reading the gossip on TMZ. If you want to buy on Amazon, or send a PayPal payment to somebody, create a WiFi hotspot with your phone. Cellular connectivity is much more secure than public WiFi. A startling Experian finding is that 38% of us use public WiFi for financial services. Just don’t.

While you are it, never use hotel business centers, not for anything that requires a password at login. There simply are too many known instances of malware installations.

All of this seems simple? Yep. None of these steps will guarantee you immunity from hackers, but following them will help you be vastly safer than most Americans. That's, at least, a start.
