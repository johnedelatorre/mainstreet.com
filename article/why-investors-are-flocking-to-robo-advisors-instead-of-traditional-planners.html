Why Investors Are Flocking to Robo Advisors Instead of Traditional Planners

  • Ellen Chang
  • Nov 11, 2014 1:23 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) – Investors are turning more toward robo advisors, online advisors who investment advice over the Internet at a lower-cost than brick-and-mortar advisories. 

New startups and major players like Charles Schwab are now offering greater online transparency on their pricing and fees and have invested in offering user-friendly websites and mobile apps. Even traditional brokerage service companies such as Schwab are now joining the sector and have unveiled a new low-cost online managed account dubbed “Intelligent Portfolios” while TradeKing, the discount brokerage firm rolled out a new managed account service dubbed “TradeKing Advisors.”

“The surge of established players entering the low-cost advice space is a major development and should raise alarm bells at full-service firms,” said Grant Easterbrook, an analyst who tracks financial startups at Corporate Insight, a New York-based financial services research and consulting firm. “Much of the coverage of these recent actions has focused on how the major hybrid players now threaten startups and will likely put them out of business. We do not view this as a zero-sum game and the ‘pie’ of investment dollars in the U.S. is large enough that there’s room for many different online advice players,”

Instead of creating their own robo advisor to offer their current clients and compete with them, some traditional investing firms are choosing to work alongside the online advisors. Fidelity announced recently an advisor partnership with Betterment, one of the first robo advisors. TD Ameritrade has also announced a relatively similar partnership to bring “robo” tech to advisors. Scottrade, the online brokerage firm, has plans to enter the competition in a limited capacity to offer a managed account service but has only alluded to it in its 2013 annual report. So far, they have not made any announcements.

While not all traditional retirement advisors are jumping on the bandwagon, several of them are targeting younger investors who have amassed less savings. Vanguard’s Personal Advisor Services unit is pushing to lower account minimums while Bank of America/Merrill Lynch plans to double its current number of 1,700 Merrill Edge advisors, which caters to less affluent investors, by 2016.

The recent actions of the major hybrid players are challenging the large full-service brokerage firms and the growth of lower-cost advice startups can no longer be dismissed by full-service firms as “minnows that don’t pose a serious threat,” he said.

“That is now changing as the large hybrid brokerages enter this space, accelerating the growth of lower-cost online alternatives,” Easterbrook said. “This increases the competitive pressure on full-service firms to become more transparent, to lower fees, and to offer better websites and mobile apps.”

Removing all human interaction can spell trouble for some investors, because sentiment is a “vital component of investing, said Taylor Winn, president of Buckhead Wealth Management in Atlanta.

“There has to be someone to talk people off the ledge and it takes a lot of coaching, advising and instructing,” he said.

While the computer-generated models are focused on expediency, a pullback in the market can spook some investors and they wind up divesting a valuable asset when they should have stayed in the game, Winn said.

“That could potentially hurt you in the long run because you didn’t have instruction and someone there to help you,” he said. “It’s a relationship driven business so face-to-face is important.”

Full-service firms cannot continue with their current strategies and “face major headwinds in the long-term,” because lower-cost rivals will continue to materialize, Easterbrook said. Another challenge for the full-service brokerages is that they are facing an impending retirement crunch as their most lucrative clients and advisors are retiring and exiting the workforce. At some point, they will have to win new business from more tech-savvy Gen X and Gen Y investors, he said.

A number of issues are stacked against the full-service firms, many of whom still offer poor customer websites, limited mobile capabilities, low transparency, high costs and an emphasis on in-person meetings.

“In the long-term, full-service firms will have to disrupt themselves in order to match the new crop of online advice providers and appeal to the next generation of investors," Winn added. "New online advisors can effectively and profitably serve younger and less affluent clients, providing them with more sophisticated services as they grow older and wealthier and their needs become more complex.” 

A hybrid model gives investors the ability to compare fees and services, said Asheesh Advani, CEO of Covestor, an online marketplace where investors can invest online completely, but also speak with client advisors by phone or even via Skype. Over 140 of the portfolios on Covestor’s marketplace are actively managed by experienced investors.

“People are gravitating to companies like Covestor, which offers actively-managed portfolios and alternative strategies that are less expensive than hedge funds with transparent holdings, verified track records, full liquidity and low minimums,” he said. “People are accustomed to shopping online for nearly all their daily needs.”

Another shortcoming is that many financial planners will not accept regular Joes as clients because they have not amassed enough wealth. Online advisors have changed the rules and often require very low minimum balances to get started.

The reality was that most people just starting out in their career never had access to financial planning and investment advice until recently, said Chris Costello, CEO of blooom, a 401(k) advisor in Overland Park, Kan. Companies now are able to build out user-friendly, scalable solutions to provide professional financial advice to the masses.

“Companies like blooom can offer someone with as little as $500 in their 401(k) the same investment advice previously only available to someone with a gazillion dollars,” he said.

The focus on financial planning has shifted and technology is now center stage, said Dave Welling, a senior vice president of the Black Diamond division at Advent Software, a San Francisco-based company which provides web-based portfolio management solutions for financial advisors. Robo advisors have appealed to Gen X and Gen Yers since it has provided a cost-effective way to invest and get started with a well-diversified investment strategy.

“Technology in financial services used to just be about making a financial advisor’s back office processing efficient,” he said. “Now it is a critical part of helping investors communicate with their advisors and have clarity and confidence in their financial future.”
