Smart Savers Acing Retirement, With a Little Help from Their Friends

  • Brian O'Connell
  • Oct 15, 2015 4:42 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) -- Can you really build a team of experts, or at least self-proclaimed experts, to turbo-boost your retirement savings?

It would appear so, as many Americans have turned to so-called "retirement advisory networks" to meet and exceed their long-term savings goals.

As a case in point, the most recent PNC Perspectives of Retirement Survey, which shows that with pensions receding and Social Security solvency in doubt, affluent Americans are feeling responsible for their own retirement and are thus building their own investment management networks.

More than nine in ten affluent Americans already have such a network, PNC told MainStreet, and those who have one feel "more confident" in their finances for retirement.

Of course, the proof is always in the pudding, and data shows retirement advisory networks do work, and work well. In its report, PNC states that Americans aged between 35 and 44, with at least $50,000 in investable assets who use a network have saved $800,000 for retirement, versus $510,000 in the same demographic without a network.

"Americans are taking retirement into their own hands and surrounding themselves with a network of people to help them plan," says Celandra Deane-Bess, chair of PNC's national practice group for retirement. "We see that those who have an effective advisory network have saved significantly more money and feel more confident in their retirement."



This isn't to say financial advisors don't have a role in such networks - they do. PNC reports that the "most important" people in a retirement savers advisory group is their financial advisor, followed by their wife or spouse. Other potential retirement network members may include accountants, friends, family and employers, the survey notes.

"People are turning to help from their spouse or partner and their financial advisor, as well as a number of resources such as news media, their financial institution's website, and online retirement calculators, which I think shows that people really want more information and are carefully thinking through these important decisions, which is a good sign," Deanne-Bass adds. "The survey shows that most are turning to their partner for providing encouragement, and to their financial advisor for building a plan or for specific expertise."

Deane-Bess says the best ways to create an effective retirement advisory network are to:

- Be deliberate: "Those with a more effective retirement advisory network say they planned their network deliberately," Deane-Bess says. "Those who have cobbled together their network do not have as much confidence that they'll be ready for retirement."

- Find those you can trust: "It is critical to find those you can trust to have your best interests at heart and who have expertise in retirement planning," Deane-Bess says. 

- Use tools and resources to your advantage: With so much to learn and decide, Deane-Bess says, be sure to take advantage of the resources available to help you navigate the complexities of retirement planning. "Online calculators, information on your financial institution's website, and workshops are good tools to check out," she says. 

- Plan for all circumstances: As more and more Americans doubt the solvency of Social Security, Deane-Bess adds, they are realizing they must have contingency plans. "It's important to think through all potential scenarios, including unforeseen health care expenses that often arise in retirement," she says. 

- Take responsibility. "We see that those who have taken the reigns, made retirement a priority and created a network to support them in their planning feel more empowered and confident that they will have enough saved to enjoy their retirement," concludes Deane-Bess.

Other financial professionals aren't so sure a network approach is the best path to a secure retirement, especially if you leave a financial advisor out of the network. 



"The answer is yes and no," says Robert Laura, president of SYNERGOS Financial Group, in Brighton, Mich. "I come across many 'do it yourself' investors who have done a good job saving and investing but many get to a point where they don't know what to do. Over a few years time they have collected a number of good investment ideas and themes but something generally tends to unravel."

For example, Laura cites the recent stock market pullback, which has been hard on income portfolios as many retirees tend to be overweight in energy, utilities, real estate trusts, and some bank stocks, that can pay good dividends. "Unfortunately, each area has been hammered, because of the drop in oil prices," he says. "Things were going great and do-it-yourself retirees generally have a great buy strategy but now aren't sure how to unwind or adjust their portfolios."

Many retirees also can't get the help they need from financial professionals. Layton Cox, founder of My Pathway, a Tucson, Ariz.-based robo-financial advisory firm, says the deck is stacked against retirement savers who don't have big portfolios and are thus left to fend for themselves in many cases.



"Most people who can't meet the $1 million minimum for most advisors either get bad advice or don't get any advice," Cox says. "I'm a Millennial. If I wasn't a financial advisor - and from what I've seen from all of my peers - I would get my financial advice from Google or my friends."

It seems like a growing number of retirement savers are doing just that, although most do add a financial professional to the mix. PNC says that's a good thing, and has some solid numbers to back that sentiment up.
