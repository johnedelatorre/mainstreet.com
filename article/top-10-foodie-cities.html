The Top 10 Foodie Cities in America

  • Robert McGarvey
  • Jul 11, 2014 10:31 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Tough luck, Santa Fe, you did not make the list.

Neither did Portland, Ore., nor Portland, Maine.

Not even Miami.

Now you can begin to grasp how high the bar is for making the top ten foodie city list issued by Livability.com, a site that primarily focuses on small to mid-sized cities ("generally under 350,000 population," said Livability editor Matt Carmichael in a Mainstreet interview).

Thus you also will not find New York or Los Angeles or Chicago, all too big to qualify.

Also See: The Priciest Grocery Store

Others did not qualify, suggested Carmichael, precisely because they make too many lists - that is, their inclusion is an obvious yawner. San Francisco, Seattle, and New Orleans are for examples.

So, how did Livability.com create its list? It started with a deep dive into data. Included were the percent of income spent eating out, ease of access to healthy food, the number of farmer's markets and how often people go to local restaurants as opposed to fast-food joints.

Also See: Food Truck Explosion -- It's Happened Before

In round two, Carmichael said Livability editors factored in qualitative metrics, such as a town's number of James Beard award winners - the Oscars for chefs.

Livability's struggle with this list in particular, said Carmichael, is that we have become a nation of foodies. "It would be easier to put out a list of cities that are not foodie cities because there now are so few of them."

Getting hungry?

Drumroll...#10...Washington, D.C. - which, Carmichael conceded, pushes the population ceiling (the District is home to 646,000), but it scored so highly, in so many categories, it had to make the list, he said.

You think of pork barrel, not pork roll, in the District?

Listen up: Julia Child's kitchen is on display at the Smithsonian's National Museum of American History.

To boot, the lobbyists, lawmakers, and tourists who flock to D.C. need places to eat, and the District is awash with them.

Where to eat? Livability particularly pointed to GBD (Golden Brown Delicious) in trendy Dupont Circle. Fried chicken, craft beer, and doughnuts make for a memorable culinary trinity.

#9: Omaha.

One word: steak. Said by some to be the country's best.

Not a town for vegans.

But beef eaters will rejoice.

#8: Burlington, Vt.

It's not only about crunchy granola, dude, said Livability.com, which explained that "restaurants in Burlington have shifted towards more sophisticated tastes but still make it a priority to use local meats, cheeses and veggies. Despite a short growing season, organic farms surround the city and remain quite profitable, thanks to the support they get from residents and restaurants."

Oh, and Vermont may make the nation's best artisanal cheeses, a fact made vivid by the annual Vermont Cheesemakers Festival in Shelburne, right outside Burlington. July 20 this year.

#7: Boulder, Co.

When you have the munchies in this Rocky Mountain State, get them in Boulder, where Livability.com says residents are every bit as excited about the opening of the farmer's market season as they are ski season.

#6: Berkeley, Calif.

Two words: Chez Panisse.

Or maybe two more: Alice Waters.

She founded Chez Panisse and spearheaded what's become the national focus on local farmers.

Berkeley is the epicenter.

#5: Traverse City, Mich.

Weren't expecting this, were you?

Babbo superstar Mario Batali summers here and, said Livability.com, he called Traverse City a "modern gastro-paradise."

If it's good enough for Batali, it's good enough for us.

#4: Asheville, NC

Great local beer. Great local food. 1 + 1 equals a "foodtopia," per Livability com, which added that this town "offers pure decadence by way of things that make you say 'yum.'"

#3: Boston

Another town that seems too big (646,000) but, said Carmichael, Beantown scored so strongly in the data, it couldn't be ignored.

And it's not about beans anymore. Nor is it about chowdah,

Said Livability.com: "Young chefs began changing Boston's restaurant scene from drab to fab in the early '90s. Today that scene has become more refined and broadened to include a range of ethnically diverse menus, upscale eateries and cozy cafes."

#2: Scottsdale, Ariz.

A wealthy suburb of Phoenix, Scottsdale is stuffed with restaurants - and many of them are formulaic joints aimed at diners with more money than taste buds.

But then there are ones that dazzle.

ShinBay (7001 N Scottsdale Rd.) has to rank on any list of the best Japanese in America.

And there's Barrio Queen (7114 E. Stetson in Old Town Scottsdale) - even Martha Stewart popped in for a bite on a recent trip to Phoenix. This is about reimagining tacos, guacamole and the other staples.

Don't be bored with Scottsdale food; explore, and good eats are plentiful.

#1: New Haven, Conn.

It's not just Yale dining halls.

Don't miss Louis's Lunch (261-263 Crown St.), which claims to have invented the hamburger.

The menu has eight items. Repeat: eight. That includes drinks. Have the burger (the only sandwich on the menu), chips, Foxon Park Soda, maybe a slice of pie and you are full for $12.75 plus tax and tip.

Free of charge are the bragging rights that you ate where the burger craze started.

Welcome to New Haven.

--Written by Robert McGarvey for MainStreet
