To Mortgage Applicants, Increasingly It’s Go Digital or Go Home

  • Brian O'Connell
  • Feb 2, 2015 9:00 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — It's official: Ninety percent of Americans look to the Web to get a mortgage, with one-third saying an online-only mortgage process is preferable to paper-based processes.

That figure comes from Discover Home Loans, which say the 90% "used some sort of online technology to help them with the home financing process" and suggests it knows why: 81% of Americans believe having financial information online levels the playing field with traditional mortgage lenders.

Discover rolls out some more data showing the Internet means convenience and efficiency to mortgage consumers (1,003 people in all were surveyed):

  • 86% felt comfortable sharing personal and financial information electronically with their lenders, despite recent high-profile data breaches at major consumer brands such as Target and JPMorgan.
  • 68% felt electronic communications made it easier to work with lenders.
  • 69% say working online helps keep track of key financial documents, while 83% say it helps them stay organized.
  • 92% of buyers who used online tools to submit documents say it saves time.

"As technology continues to become an integral part of our daily lives, it's only natural that buyers also use it to make the home financing process easier," says T.J. Freeborn, a senior manager at Discover. "Not only are homebuyers using the Internet to look at homes and neighborhoods, they're also using it to submit documents and complete applications online."

Read More: Banks Beat High-Tech Money Handling Because Security, Fees Matter

Americans still want a relationship with their mortgage banker, though, and 67% of consumers still meet their mortgage lender face-to-face at some point. But 54% said they filled out their mortgage application online and 88% say they rely heavily on email as the process goes on.

If you do go online for a mortgage, make sure you have all your relevant financial information lined up and ready to go before filling out the application, or you'll have to start all over again. Think employment history, debts and assets, and relevant tax return history.

Also, try more than one online lender. That will increase your chances of getting a loan approved. First check the mortgage lender's reputation, though, to ensure you're dealing with a company that has a track record of getting your mortgage through with minimum problems.

— By Brian O'Connell for MainStreet
