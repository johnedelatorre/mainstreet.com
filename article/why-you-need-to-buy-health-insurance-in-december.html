Why You Need to Buy Health Insurance in December

  • Ellen Chang
  • Dec 15, 2015 3:58 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

As the marketplace adapts to the Affordable Care Act and networks for doctors shift each year to cope with demand and costs, some consumers are left without knowing which health insurance plan to purchase.

In some states, some doctors are still negotiating costs with insurance companies, which can put consumers in a precarious state since they must choose a plan by December 15 in order for new coverage to begin on January 1. Patients who want to continue to see the same doctors next year must ensure they purchase the right plan or risk paying expensive out of network costs not just to see the physician, but also the ensuing tests and blood work ordered.

“The reality is with doctor networks narrowing this year, especially in Texas, it’s worth making the effort to verify your ‘must have’ doctors will be on a plan going forward,” said Jack Hooper, CEO of Take Command Health, an online health insurance exchange based in Dallas. “One of our consumer tools is that you can search for your doctor on the website and see what network he or she will be working with next year.”

Too many patients assume that their doctor won’t change plans for next year and they mistakenly believe they will be “auto-renewed,” he said.

One option is to call the doctor’s office or check their website to see if he has finalized his insurance agreement. Not all doctors signed contracts that started in January and end in December, which can make choosing a plan difficult if you need your plan to start by January 1.

“Ultimately, which insurance companies a doctor will accept comes down to negotiated prices and supply and demand,” Hooper said. “A doctor can say they will only accept the most expensive PPO plans [preferred provider organization] that pay out the most to doctors, but then he or she may risk losing a bunch of patients and will have a hard time getting new ones. If you and enough other patients call and tell your doctor which plan you will be using, it could have an impact.”

What To Do Next

Provider networks should be “pretty firmed up” by this time for the 2016 plan year, however there can always be last minute changes made, “so there are never any absolute guarantees,” said Nate Purpura, vice president of consumer affairs at eHealth.com, an online health insurance exchange based in Mountain View, Calif.

Ensuring that your coverage starts on January 1 means you must choose a plan by December 15. If you discover that your favorite doctors and specialists have selected another network, you can cancel your first choice and apply again for another one before the end of the open enrollment period on January 31.

“There’s nothing to stop you from changing plans as many times as you like during open enrollment,” he said.

Since there are still several weeks until open enrollment ends, you do not need a reason to change your mind. Once open enrollment ends, you can only change plans if you qualify for a special enrollment such as moving, changing jobs or were married or divorced.

“Your new plan may not start until February or March 1 depending on when you sign up, but you could keep your current plan for a month or two or drop it immediately, sign up for a temporary plan with your doctor, and then pick up the regular plan in February or March,” Hooper said.

Chose Alternative Plans

Purchasing a temporary plan can work only if you need insurance for a month or two because if you do not buy another plan and keep one for more than three months, you may be subject to Obamacare penalties of $695 per adult or 2.5% of your taxable income in 2016, whichever is greater. Most exchanges sell them and include national insurance companies such as Blue Cross Blue Shield or United Healthcare, but most of them exclude pre-existing conditions.

“Temporary plans are cheap and have great networks,” Hooper said. There are some restrictions in terms of what they will cover and if you have one for more, but they can be a great option to help bridge the gap until your new plan with your doctor starts.

A temporary plan is better than not having any insurance coverage in case an accident or injury occurs, because even a trip to the ER for a minor injury can easily run into thousands of dollars and major medical bills are the top causes of bankruptcies, Hooper said.

Another option is to continue to see your physician and pay for services and procedures in cash or money from a Health Savings Account (HSA), which is not taxed by the IRS and can be invested like an IRA. The advantage of HSAs is that any unused funds roll over each year and any remaining money can be used for retirement after the age of 65.

“You definitely don’t want to be out of network for big, expensive medical procedures and hospital stays,” he said. “If you need an annual physical or consultations every few months, you should ask about the cash price for a visit, which is usually around $100 for 30 minutes. Sometimes you can keep seeing your doctor without the insurance middle-man.”

Other plans such as faith-based ones like Medi-Share offer a PPO network and do not have not have an open enrollment period. These plans are ACA compliant, meaning you will not pay a tax penalty for buying one.

“They have a more robust group of ‘in network’ doctors than many expect,” Hooper said. “Their plans start the first of the month year round.”

Looking for a new doctor might not be your first option, but “it’s not as bad as you may think,” he said. “Every insurance company has to maintain access to primary care physicians and specialists for their clients in the client’s area.”
