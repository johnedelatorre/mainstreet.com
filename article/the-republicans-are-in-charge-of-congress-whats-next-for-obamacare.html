The Republicans Are In Charge of Congress: What's Next For Obamacare?

  • Eric Reed
  • Nov 7, 2014 9:03 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) - What's next for congressional Republicans? Thanks to a huge win on Tuesday night, the party has taken charge of the Senate and increased its already comfortable majority in the House of Representatives. Much, if not all, of the legislative agenda over the next two years will be set from the offices of Rep. John Boehner (R-Ohio) and Sen Mitch McConnell (R-Ky.). If the past, as well as several campaign slogans, are any guide that means one thing: repealing Obamacare.

Now, a full repeal of the Affordable Care Act simply isn't going to happen. Any law the new Congress passes has to clear Obama's desk, and it's inconceivable that he would sign legislation banishing his namesake achievement. That doesn't mean that the law's opponents are without options, however. Legislators like Sen. Ted Cruz (R-Texas) have suggested that one way to get around the Presidential veto will be to simply repeal the law's provisions "one at a time."

Some of these ideas might even manage to find support from Democrats. While no one outside of Capitol Hill knows precisely what the new majority has in mind, here are a few possible targets in the ACA and how their repeal might impact your health insurance.

The Individual Mandate

This deserves mention not because it might happen but because it's a target of special Republican rage. The individual mandate is unpopular with the public at large, rabidly unpopular among conservatives and has been the centerpiece of conservative attacks on the law since the first draft.

It is also impossible to repeal the individual mandate and keep Obamacare. If insurance companies cannot discriminate among customers based on health and pre-existing conditions and customers don't have to carry insurance when they're healthy, then everyone will do the rational thing and only buy coverage when they get sick. Along with premium subsidies, economists refer to this as the "tripod" of insurance reform. Pull out one leg and the whole thing topples, in this case leading to spiraling premiums and eventually a repeal of the entire law.

Even in the unlikely event that Republicans pick up enough Democratic votes to force cloture, they won't find the votes to overcome the President's inevitable veto.



The Employer Mandate

This is more plausible than repealing the individual mandate. The employer mandate requires that every employer over a certain size provide health insurance for all full time employees. It has sparked off some of the fiercest debates about whether the ACA kills jobs, the conservative argument being that many employers would rather reduce hours and lay off staff than pay for health insurance.

Read More: How the Republican Version of Obamacare Differs from the Original

Even among Democrats, the employer mandate isn't always popular, and Obama has already delayed implementation until 2015 and beyond. Getting the votes for repeal might actually happen, and the impact would be... fairly minor. According to one study by the Urban Institute, "eliminating the employer mandate will not reduce insurance coverage significantly." With it we'll have 251.1 million people insured in 2016, without it 250.9 million. This has been the consensus among many economists to study the provision: there just aren't that many uninsured people who will get new coverage through the mandate. 

Read More: Is the Affordable Care Act's Impact Over-Hyped?

Rolling back the employer mandate probably wouldn't mean much for individual insurance, but that doesn't mean it's an empty shell. Experts expect that penalties paid by companies under this mandate could bring in anywhere from $46 to $130 billion over ten years, revenue that would have to be made up somewhere. If Democrats and Republicans can agree on where to find that money, though, this could actually be in the cards.



The Medical Device Tax

For an esoteric source of revenue, this tax has gotten an enormous amount of attention.

Stripped of the rhetoric, the medical device tax helps pay for ACA subsidies by imposing a 2.3% tax on all "medical devices." It applies to anything from pacemakers to tongue depressors, and Republicans have been after it for years. They argue that it slows development by making products, both new and old, more expensive and that it increases the price of health care by adding a cost that will get passed on to the consumers.

This provision might be low hanging fruit for repeal. For all the noise generated around the medical device tax, ultimately it's a budgeting mechanic. There is no principle on which to fight here, so if the parties can agree on another way to raise $20 billion a year they're free to do so. The average insured American won't feel a thing.




Insurance Standards

Many Republicans have also opposed the minimum standards that the ACA imposes on health insurance plans. If you want to buy a coverage without mental health protection, they argue, you should be free to do so and save some money.

This, Democrats generally respond, is like saying cars should be free to come without seat belts. We put in minimum consumer standards to prevent a race to the bottom and ensure that everyone has access to some basic features, whether they're well off or not.

Republicans may well have their shot at stripping consumer standards out of the health insurance marketplace, or at least the chance to add "copper" plans to the marketplace's platinum through bronze levels. Where the current-minimum bronze plans pay for 60% of a person's medical bills, copper plans would be closer to standard catastrophic coverage and pay at most 50%.

Either of these measures, while potentially effective cost saving measures, could mean very real suffering. As far as copper plans go, they've been called "a false sense of affordability" by experts in the field who point out that paying half of your own medical bills can get ruinously expensive. The maximum deductible would probably have to go up to about $9,000 for these plans, enough to ruin someone without much savings. "If you ever have to use the plan," said Sabrina Corlette, project director at Georgetown University's Center on Health Insurance Reforms in an interview with the Kaiser Foundation, "you won't be able to afford it."

As for stripping out the minimum standards, the thing about health insurance is that it's meaningless if your policy doesn't cover your actual condition. Eliminating coverage for issues like mental health and physical therapy would ensure an entire cohort of people who are technically insured but functionally abandoned because they couldn't afford a higher tier plan. Anyone on a silver or bronze plan, about 85% of the marketplace, could see a very real reduction in their policy benefits if this actually passed.

Risk Corridors

Opponents call them "insurance bailouts," and... they're more or less exactly right. The risk corridors act as a safety net for insurers who get too many sick people on their plan. If the company takes a big loss on patients that it would have turned away pre-ACA, the government reimburses a portion of that loss. Republicans oppose this provision fiercely, arguing against it on the grounds of fiscal responsibility.

Rhetoric aside, though, risk corridors are in fact either budget neutral or even slightly profitable because the door swings both ways. Just as the government agrees to indemnify insurance companies against heavy losses for mandated patients, the insurance companies have to kick back some money from exceptionally profitable years.

Eliminating the risk corridors would mean less, possibly far less, choice on the exchange marketplaces. This would get rid of their safety net. Insurance companies don't have to participate in the ACA exchanges, and if the specter of big losses was put back in the picture, they could very well shy away leading to fewer plans available for everyone. For a relatively obscure budgetary maneuver, this could actually have a very big impact.




The Independent Payment Advisory Board

Finally, the Death Panel. The IPAB has the power to set new standards for Medicare payments. It was created as a cost saving measure, a board that could set lower price points as a way of reining in spending. Many critics have accused the program of rationing health care, presumably confusing the difference between limiting how much Medicare will spend on a procedure vs. how much Medicare will spend on a patient.

The health care industry, unsurprisingly, opposes it because theirs are the bills that the IPAB would chop, and Medicare already only pays about 86 cents on the dollar.

Either way the point has been moot. The advisory board is supposed to meet when Medicare spending exceeds ACA targets. Not only has that not happened, but thanks to the surprisingly slow growth in medical expenses, it's not looking likely any time soon. In fact, the 15-member board hasn't even been staffed yet.

What would happen if it were eliminated? Probably not much. It's possible that this will come up since the Death Panel slogan proved remarkably effective as a piece of political theater and whether Democrats would go along with it is difficult to predict. Some Democrats might vote to eliminate the IPAB just to avoid an ugly roll call. On the other hand, although the board hasn't been important yet it might become so in the future. Obama might push back on this out of long term thinking, but in the here and now, no one would really notice if it were gone.
