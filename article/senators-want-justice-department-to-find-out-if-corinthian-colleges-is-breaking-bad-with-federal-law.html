Senators Want Justice Department to Find Out if Corinthian Colleges is Breaking Bad With Federal Law

  • John Sandman
  • May 19, 2015 11:20 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — After newly-minted Attorney General Loretta Lynch took her oath of office, a letter was making its way to her desk at the Justice Department from seven U.S. Senators asking that the DOJ open an investigation of Corinthian Colleges executives to determine if they violated any civil or criminal laws.

The letter to Lynch came the week Corinthian filed for bankruptcy and ten days after it announced the shut-down of its remaining campuses with only a day's notice.

The senators asked the DOJ to do what the Department of Education (ED) says it cannot--hold Corinthian executives accountable for their actions. If the evidence warrants it, they want Corinthian execs charged with criminal conduct. Senators Elizabeth Warren (D-Mass.), Richard Blumenthal (D-Conn.), Jack Reed (D-R.I.), Ed Markey (D-MA), Dick Durbin (D-Ill.), Chris Murphy (D-Conn.) and Al Franken (D-Minn.) signed on to the letter.

Lynch's appointment may have been an opening to involve the DOJ, but the senators' ire was likely raised by Under Secretary of Education Ted Mitchell's remarks last month to the Los Angeles Times, when he said, "Our tools for holding (Corinthian) executives responsible individually are limited."

The seven senators aren't ready to take this lying down. “It is vital that Congress and the public understand what tools the Justice Department can use to hold such executives responsible individually,” the letter states. “If these tools are inadequate, Congress may need to act.”

The worrisome picture that emerged during the Corinthian melt down shows that even while ED was investigating Corinthian for fraud--and while the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau pronounced it a fraudulent operation--Federal money was flowing to Corinthian, which made up the majority of its revenue and which it could function without. Once the cash spigot was turned off a year ago, Corinthian could no longer function and its slow-motion collapse began.

Corinthian's executives were well-compensated even as enrollments declined and regulatory threats grew. According to a Bank Talk post in May 2014, the company's compensation committee created an initiative that would reward staff if Corinthian reported operating income of $50 million and if the company's executive compensation committee concluded that the company had met a "regulatory compliance gate," even as the CFPB, the Securities and Exchange Commission and 20 state attorneys general were running active investigations.

Corinthian reported $51.5 million in operating income for the 2013 fiscal year, and CEO Jack Massimino was rewarded with a bonus of $1.035 million. The same incentive system paid out target incentive awards of $1.4 million to four other executives. Regulatory threats raised no eyebrows among the comp committee.

Corinthian was looking to sell even before that. In 2011 it retained the Washington, D.C. law firm of Akin, Gump, Strauss Hauer & Feld to work on a potential sale. Linda Arey Skladany, who joined Corinthian's board of directors at the time of its 1999 initial public offering, is married to Akin, Gump partner Barney Skladany. It seemed possible that that Akin Gump was positioned to receive work on any transaction. In 2014, it hired Barclays to help find a suitor, as enrollment was plummeting.  Today, Corinthian is believed to owe hundreds of thousands of dollars in legal fees.

--Written by John Sandman for MainStreet
