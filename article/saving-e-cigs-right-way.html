Saving on E-Cigs the Right Way

  • Craig Donofrio
  • Oct 26, 2013 2:18 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — There's a lot to be said about e-cigarettes, and not all of it is flattering. Regardless, sales are booming: electronic cigarette sales in convenience stores are estimated at $1 billion this year, according to Convenience Store News. With cigarettes being so expensive, many smokers are looking to switch to e-cigs to save some much recession-needed cash. But how much can you save?

Since cigarette prices vary between states, we called six gas stations in six major cities and asked for the price of a pack of Marlboro Lights, with tax, to use as examples. If you're in Los Angeles, expect to pay $7.65; in Dallas, $7.24; in Indianapolis, $6.14; in Washington, D.C., $8.25; and in Brooklyn, $12.35. Using that data, if you're someone who smokes a pack every two days in Indianapolis, that's $1,121 a year. But if you live in D.C., your smoking habit costs you $1,506. And God help you if you live in New York City, because you'll pay $2,254 a year on smokes at that rate.

It's no surprise that one of the more appealing aspects of using an e-cig, known as 'vaping' among users, is that it saves money. However, some e-cigs habits are more expensive than others. The trendy ones seen in gas stations and pharmacies mimic a cigarette; their rechargeable battery is the white paper part of a cigarette, and their cartridge, which stores the flavored liquid known as e-juice, looks like the filter. However, they may not be your cheapest alternatives. Wholly disposable, one-time use e-cigs can go for $15, and should be avoided due to their limited use and expensive nature. So let's take, for example, the cost of investing in Blu eCig, which was bought by tobacco giant Lorillard (they make Newports) for $135 million last year.

First, you'll need a starter kit. Blu starter kits range from $70 to $80; they consist of two batteries, two chargers, an electronic carrying case (which actually rumble when you're near another Blu user, so you can decide on whether or not to strike up awkward conversation) and a five-pack of cartridges. In store, they cost $14.99 at my local Walgreens. Blu claims they supply a cigarette pack's worth of vapor per cartridge, although it really depends on how much vapor you pull. A Blu "Original" cartridge contains 1.2 mL of e-juice, or 6 mL per pack. Say you go through a pack of cartridges a week at $15. That makes $780, plus $70 for the kit, or $850 for the first year, not including tax. That's certainly cheaper than our two-pack a day habit. But it's not necessarily the cheapest option, especially if you smoke even more, as that's likely to transition over to 'vaping' a lot.

Andy McDonald, a former U.S. Army soldier living in Louisiana in his early 40s who smoked between one and one-and-a-half packs a day before switching, said he first used a Blu, but then "graduated" to the more technical e-cig options. Now, he says he goes "through 60 to 80 milliliters" a month. He quit smoking cigarettes almost a year ago, and says he's saved "close to five hundred, maybe more, dollars than buying regular cigarettes." McDonald uses two devices bought for between $80 and $100 each, he says. However, you don't need to spend that much on one device.

Instead, we'll stick to one of the commonly recommended styles, the Joye eGo. Starter kits can be found for $55 to $65 online; they come with two batteries and a charger, but instead of cartridges, the battery is threaded to work with several different methods to vaporize e-juice, so your eGo style battery can be used with many different attachments. For ease of use, newbies should start with something called a clearomizer, a clear plastic attachment that holds about 2mL of juice you fill up manually.

So how much can you expect to spend?

"Our average customer spends about $40 per month on e-cig gear," says Steve Johnson, director of operations of Butt-Out E-cigs, which operates both online and via its brick and mortar store in Phoenix. "This number has remained consistent, within a few dollars, for the past six months now, so we're fairly confident in it. We do have outliers – those few customers who will plunk down $100 plus each trip through the store — but for most it's strictly maintenance and occasionally a new shiny piece of hardware to reward themselves."

Maintenance refers to buying e-juice and replacing equipment. Clearomizers, for example, can last anywhere from one week to a month, depending on how much it's used and the quality of the brand; they go for about $6. Prices for e-juice vary by vendor, but typically fall between $4 and $8 per 10mL. Johnson Creek Smoke Juice, the same vendor Blu uses, sells 15mL bottles for $9.95.

Using Johnson's estimate of $40 a month, that's $480 a year, plus the purchase cost of $65 for a kit, or $545 for the first year, not including tax (or shipping cost) to use an eGo style e-cig. But if you're just starting out, you might want to budget another $50 - $100 to experiment with additional accessories and extra e-juice to find your favorite flavor and vendor.

Do it yourself

If you're looking to go dirt cheap, you can start experimenting with DIY e-juice. E-juice is made up of four components: vegetable glycerin, propylene glycol, nicotine, and flavoring. Please note that this involves handling nicotine, which is fatal if handled improperly. Therefore, it's recommended you pick it up as pre-mixed with propylene glycol and/or vegetable glycerin. There are sites that give you everything you need like the DIY Flavor Artisan Kit from OneStopDIYShop.com for $25.

Crunching numbers, you get a combined 150mL of propylene glycol, vegetable glycerin and nicotine, or 15 bottles of 10mL juice (provided you cut them all the exact same way). That comes to each bottle costing $1.67 each, not including any of the other items or flavoring liquids. Of course, making a tasty e-juice can be difficult, so be prepared to do some experimenting (and spending). Keep in mind that the e-juice pros have devoted a lot of time and money to get juice flavors just right. Bryan Peck, owner of the e-juice vendor Highbrowvapor.com, says his company has "spent countless thousands of dollars finding and procuring our flavorings which are expensive. Even more so than the nicotine itself in many cases."

Getting deep into the world of vaping isn't for everyone. It takes time to understand the dozens of mods, e-cig types and e-cig lingo you'll find on forums. You can certainly save money by making your own e-juice, but the hassle of trial and error might be too much for many people. Blu e-cigs can be more expensive than using the mix-and-match eGo style batteries, but require zero effort. Alternatively, you'll save the most using an eGo style e-cig, but you'll have to spend some time learning the ropes. Just don't be surprised if a convenience store e-cig is the gateway to mixing your own e-juice at midnight.

--Written by Craig Donofrio for MainStreet
