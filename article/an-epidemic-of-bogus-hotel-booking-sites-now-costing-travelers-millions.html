An Epidemic of Bogus Hotel Booking Sites Now Costing Travelers Millions

  • Robert McGarvey
  • May 21, 2015 11:21 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Bogus hotel bookings are an exploding scam - and the price tag this year may hit $220 million, for some 2.5 million bookings, according to a count by Maryam Cope, vice president for government affairs at trade group American Hotel and Lodging Association (AH&LA).

Some 400 participants from the hospitality industry lobbied on Capitol Hill to raise legislators' awareness about such deceptive practices, said Kerry Ranson, IHG Owners Association chair and Expotel Hospitality Services CEO, at a Washington,D.C. industry gathering in April.

"There are numerous instances lately where there are bogus websites misleading the consumer," Ranson said. "Consumers are looking at ads from travel companies and being led to websites that look legitimate, but are not. These are rogue vendors using false advertising to the consumer and leading the consumer to think they're actually booking a hotel reservation.”

Five members of Congress now have written the U.S. Department of Justice, requesting that DOJ investigate what the Congressmen call a “growing trend” for fake websites to sprout.

Understand this: part of the motivation for this fight is that many hotels just want consumers to book directly on their sites so that they save the commissions paid legitimate third party bookers such as Expedia and Hotels.com, which generally are said to charge fees ranging from 10 to 30%. Hoteliers hate those fees, and so they hate just about all third parties.

But there are good reasons to believe many of these bogus sites do in fact represent a consumer menace.

Congresswoman Kyrsten Sinema (D-Ariz, 9th) told KTAR TV that she wanted the DOJ to investigate, because this is a scam where travelers “book the room, they pay the money with their credit card and lo and behold they show up at the hotel with their printed out reservation and they don't have a reservation and they've lost all that money.”

In actual fact, the scam appears to come in two flavors, one bad and the other - described by Sinema - is a lot worse.

In the bad variant, the Internet increasingly is chockablock with websites that strive to look like the websites of specific hotels. A harried - or inattentive - visitor may not notice that the site is not the real McCoy, AH&LA claimed. That may be especially true for users on mobile phones with small screens. Even savvy users may get fooled, because the bogus hotel sites often steal identifying graphics - logos and photos - from the real site.

“They buy domain names of the big resorts and use the resort logos, photos, etc,” said Bob Winter, a co-owner of Wisconsin travel agency My Lake Country Travel 

In many cases, the site will actually make the reservation for the consumer. The crooks the commission and pass along credit card information. What generally gets lost in these cases are any special requests, from a kingsize bed for a honeymoon couple to an in-room refrigerator for a diabetic. These sites, said industry experts, do not have access to the hotel’s reservations system - where such requests are logged. Said AH&LA’s Cope: “Whether it be that you need two double beds, a handicap-accessible room, or are just expecting to get your rewards points - they promise to deliver, but the reality is they can't and won't.”

Bad as that is, the other scam is worse. According to Ranson, “there have been cases where guests arrive at a hotel with a confirmation, but it's not the legitimate hotel confirmation and the guests do not have a room available to them.” This can be catastrophic for travelers especially in peak occupancy periods such as Memorial Day and July 4th. That traveler may arrive to a sold out hotel that has no record of the reservation, and helpful as hoteliers generally try to be, there may be no nearby hotel with availability.

The traveler’s credit card has been charged, however, and clawing back that money may not be that quick or easy.

Shutting the shady sites is also not that easy, admitted Ranson. “They keep constantly changing their names so it’s very difficult to say which ones are the bad players," he said. "They have one name today and another tomorrow.”

Said Cope: “You shouldn’t have to be a detective to plan your vacation. These sites have a responsibility to be transparent about who they are and what they can offer.”

What self-defense can a consumer follow? Said Ranson, “My advice to the consumer - book directly from from the hotel brand's website and not some other site that might insert the brand name into their url.” Ranson further suggested calling the hotel directly to confirm that the website you are using is the right one.

“The consumer must be sure he or she is booking through a legitimate site,” added Ranson, and nowadays that means taking precautionary steps.

—Written by Robert McGarvey for MainStreet
