7 Qualities You Have to Have If You Expect to Succeed in the Restaurant Business

  • Kathryn Tuggle
  • May 11, 2015 5:48 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Any experienced restaurateur will tell you that the business can be as tough as it is rewarding. Owners are often faced with long hours, unexpected costs and frequent customer service concerns. If you're thinking of opening a restaurant, here's a look at seven qualities every successful restaurateur must have.

1. A passion for food

"Passion is key for success with any business, but with a restaurant, you truly have to love it and want to be there every day," says Jay Silver, owner and chef at Manhattan restaurant EJ's Luncheonette.

"Some people think it's cool to have a restaurant and have this great place for social gatherings, but that's not what it's about. You have to love food," he says.

People who don't love food are going to be less likely to experiment with different ingredients and may not be as attuned to the quality of food as they should be, he explains.

"If you're a super picky eater or if you wouldn't describe yourself as a 'foodie,' then this might not be the right industry for you," he says.

2. Excellent work ethic and an eye for detail

"You have to be willing to 'get dirty' and be willing to learn absolutely everything that comes with operating a restaurant," says Rodney Anderson, CEO and founder of Pancheros Mexican Grill.

You should know going in that you're going to work all the time — weekends, holidays and late nights.

"That's the nature of the business, Silver says. "You're married to it."

Successful restaurateurs are hands-on and look to hire similarly engaged workers, he says.

"You have to watch every little thing," he says. "Hundreds of little things go on in a restaurant every hour and you have to be aware of them. From a light bulb that's burned out to a fork missing from a place setting, these things may not seem important, but customers notice these details if they aren't right."

Think of yourself as someone who needs to "listen and see all," says Mark Estee, a restaurateur in Reno and Lake Tahoe, Nev., who owns restaurants including Campo Reno, Chez Louie and Reno Provisions. 

"Have ears and eyes on side of your head and in the back and make sure they are open," Estee says. "This will guide you in making decisions."

3. Organizational skills

As the restaurant owner, you'll have to organize all elements of purchasing and scheduling and you'll have to ensure that people, rent and taxes get paid, Silver says.

"Organization skills translate across a bunch of different channels. You have to train your waiters to be organized enough to know when to take an order, when to refill a drink and when to deliver food," he explains. "If you're scattered and all over the place, everyone who works at your restaurant is going to be scattered and all over the place. It trickles down."

Many successful restaurant managers have a checklist that they go over every day when they come in for work, Silver says. The list might include things such as: turn on A/C, turn on computer and make sure dining room is set.

"These are the mundane but essential things that you have to do every day that can make or break a business."

4. Ability to teach

A successful restaurant owner is able to show people exactly how to do their job, Silver says.

"You can't just expect them to know what their job is. You have to spend a few weeks training them and teaching them one on one. Yes, this is a time commitment, but if you don't do it, what kind of business are you really running?"

Even if your employees have years of experience at other restaurants, they still need training in your specific operations and protocols.

"You may have a cook who has been doing this forever, but you still have to show him how to make all your dishes so he can get it right," Silver says.

As a teacher, it's important that you know your mission statement and follow it, Estee says.

"You set the course and do it right and all will follow," he explains. "There are many types of leaders. Be the one you are, and be consistent. Don't be a hard-ass one day and a pushover the next. When you're consistent you can expect consistency."

5. Ability to inspire and lead

Every day, you have to show you love your business, says Billy Sewell, owner of 26 Golden Corral franchises in North Carolina, South Carolina, West Virginia, Kentucky, Virginia and Ohio.

"Employees want to see their leader or owner with excitement, enthusiasm and a desire to succeed," he says. "If you care, they will care."

When making decisions for your employees or your business, be firm.

"You need the willingness and ability to make decisions, stand by your decisions and communicate your decisions in order to be an effective leader," he says.

Sometimes, inspiring people can be as simple as asking their opinion, Silver says.

"Talk to them about what you love about a dish or a wine, and try to evoke that passion in them. Ask for their opinion about what would make the restaurant better, or how they would do things if it were their business."

6. An understanding of your customer

You've got to know your customer, Silver says. You need to know where they like to eat, what they want to eat and the kind of dining room setting that most appeals to them.

"You may have your heart set on opening a Mexican restaurant, but if there are five others in the surrounding area, that's probably not a good idea. You have to make sure your restaurant isn't redundant," Silver says.

Before you lease a space or choose a theme for your restaurant, walk the neighborhood and try to get a feel for the existing establishments and the customers, Silver recommends.

"What's needed in that neighborhood? You need to serve the kind of food people can't get elsewhere."

7. Ability to budget and control costs

Labor and food are the two biggest costs for any restaurant.

"If you don't have a handle on labor, it can be a disaster. It's so easy to be overstaffed, or find yourself understaffed with people working overtime That adds up quickly," Silver says.

Smart restaurateurs are constantly bargain shopping for supplies, often turning to several different vendors to find the best deals on essentials such as paper products and condiments, Silver says. They also look to eliminate waste wherever possible, including turning off the lights in storage areas and bathrooms and making sure bartenders aren't offering too heavy a pour.

"It's a nickel-and-dime business, but all those nickels and dimes add up to lots of dollars," he says.
