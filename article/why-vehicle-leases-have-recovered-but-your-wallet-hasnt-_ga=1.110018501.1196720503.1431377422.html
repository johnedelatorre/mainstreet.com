Why Vehicle Leases Have Recovered But Your Wallet Hasn't

  • Jason Notte
  • Feb 23, 2015 6:45 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) – The car lease has become the big-ticket equivalent of the smartphone contract, but that doesn't mean everybody can just sign and drive.

According to auto pricing and analysis site Edmunds, 27% of all new car transactions in 2014 were leases. That's up from a low of 16.6% at the bottom of the recession in 2009 and even well above the pre-recession high of 19.5% in 2007. As lease transfer site Swapalease.com notes, though, credit approvals for leases in the secondary market sat at 69% for 2014, down from 77.7% a year earlier. In December, that rate cratered at 60.9%, down from the 73.3% approvals in December 2013.

“We're doing lease transfers as opposed to lease originations and lease transfers are going to be held to a little bit higher standards and have a few less options involved,” says Scot Hall, executive vice president of Swapalease. “If you're taking over a lease, you're signing on to where other people are leaving off, and most companies are looking to stay in either the same credit range or even improve it.”

Despite overall improvements in the economy, even the shorter terms and lower initial payments of a secondhand, transferred lease are escaping a growing portion of the new vehicle market in the U.S. Even with the Consumer Price Index noting that the cost of leased vehicles was down 0.1% in December from the same time last year, that's not making it easier for many consumers to slip into a lease. The Bureau of Labor Statistics unemployment rate of 5.7% looks nice and all compared with the recession-era alternative, but with the key leasing demographic under 35 years old, a 9.8% jobless rate for folks 20 to 24 and a whopping 18% unemployment rate among driving-age adults younger than 20 isn't helping.

Neither are used car prices that Manheim Consulting's Used Vehicle Index says were up 1.8% from a year earlier. In January, that index finished the year at its highest rate since 2012, when a combination of recession-driven cuts in rental fleet purchases and post-bankruptcy lease abandonment by General Motors and Chrysler dried up used car inventory and set prices soaring. New car sales that plummeted to roughly 10 million in 2009 (compared with almost 16.5 million last year), also didn't help. When those leases finally returned, Edmunds senior analyst Jessica Caldwell notes that even potential lessees with good to fair credit were being turned away at times.

“We don't really track that kind of financial data, especially credit profile data, but, anecdotally, I think you do see a lot of that,” she says. “That's a segment of the market that's underserved and something that companies like Chrysler and Nissan are trying to get into. They're not subprime, but prime applicants who've been turned down in the past, and they haven't been in the new car cycle.”

When she has seen those buyers goaded back into the leasing market, Caldwell says it's been by brands including Mitsubishi, Fiat and Dodge that offer them 10% interest rates on their lease. That's far higher than the lease rates offered to lessees with slightly better credit, and also a shame given the direction of the lease market. Caldwell notes that the lease rates for subcompacts, typically the least costly option available, jumped from 5% of all leases before the recession to roughly 17% today. From 2008 through 2013, leases of subcompact vehicles increased 187%, while compact cars saw a 131% spike.

Their window is also closing. As Caldwell points out, a big reason automakers embraced leasing so wholeheartedly after the recession was to build up their used car supply and balance out a secondary market that was once dominated by luxury vehicles. With leases of even pickup trucks and crossover SUVs seeing 10% to 53% gains since the height of the recession, Caldwell believes used vehicle inventory will balance out and that lease prices will eventually rise as a result.

Then again, with one in four new car buyers getting their vehicle through a lease, Caldwell and Hall believe that leasing may just be a natural fit for modern consumer culture. Just as consumers have begun shirking ownership of their music, movies and devices for streaming subscriptions and contracts, Caldwell and Hall believe car buyers are adopting leases in greater numbers to keep up with technology. As GPS navigation improves and features including backup cameras and in-car Wi-Fi become standard, standard leases and lease transfers become attractive options for quick updates.

“The changes [in vehicle leasing] really suit American purchasing, more so than they have in the past,” Caldwell says. “Most people now shop on a monthly payment instead of saving up a bunch of money to make a big down payment or buy a car in cash. It seems like it's going with the times.”

Those times have proven a lot tougher on some potential lessees than others.
