The Best and Worst States to Retire

  • Scott Gamm
  • Sep 4, 2014 5:13 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — A successful retirement isn’t just a matter of the state of your finances, but also which state you retire in.

Read More: More Than One in Three Americans Haven't Saved Anything for Retirement

A report from WalletHub looked at 150 of the nation’s highest populated cities and ranked each location based on 25 different factors, including adjust cost of living, number of recreational centers for seniors per capita, weather and health care costs, among other factors.

“We wanted to find the places where retirees could stretch their dollars, but still live comfortably,” says WalletHub’s financial writer Richie Bernardo.

Current retirees and those about to enter retirement have been on a rough road lately, as the 2008 recession wiped out 401(k) and IRA balances on the heels of the precipitous drop in asset prices.

Even though the stock market has recovered and consumers are contributing more to their IRAs, retirees living on a fixed income can’t solely rely on rising stock markets or higher equity in their home as sources of income. Costs need to be addressed, too.

Leaving high-cost states in favor of areas with lower taxes, cheaper home prices and health care costs is prudent given how fragile the economic recovery is.

The top cities to retire include:

  • 1. Tampa, Fla.
  • 2. Grand Prairie, Texas
  • 3. Orlando, Fla.
  • 4. St. Petersburg, Fla.
  • 5. Scottsdale, Ariz.
These cities had ample job opportunities for those over age 65 and are also in states with some of the lowest tax rates in the country.

“People work in the northeast for their professional careers, but tend to downsize and move south for retirement,” Bernardo adds.

States that weren’t as conducive for retirement were unsurprisingly located in the northeast and in parts of California.

The worst cities to retire are:

  • 1. Jersey City, N.J.
  • 2. Baltimore, Md.
  • 3. Fresno, Calif.
  • 4. Worcester, Mass.
    • 5. Stockton, Calif.

On the flip side, these cities are known for higher tax rates, fewer employment opportunities for retirees and high costs of living. The report found the adjusted cost of living for retired persons in New York City is two times higher than in Nashville.

For retirees, if you currently live in a city with a high cost of living, start crunching the numbers to see if downsizing makes sense. “Keep in mind, moving costs are a one-time expense,” says financial expert Gail Cunningham with the National Foundation for Credit Counseling. “Plus, plenty of retirees still work during retirement and stay active, so you can work part-time to pay yourself back for those expenses.”

Start looking at what comparable homes in your area have sold for to give you a sense of your home’s value. Next, look into what homes cost in the new city you plan to retire in. “Many feel as though they are attached to their current homes, but downsizing may allow you to put away a significant chunk of money into savings,” Cunningham adds.
