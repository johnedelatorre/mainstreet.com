The Best 529 College Savings Plans — Handicapping the Rankings

  • John Sandman
  • Oct 24, 2014 8:36 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — This week Morningstar released the results of its annual survey of 529 College Savings Plans. Morningstar puts each fund into its Good, Better, Best ranking system, dubbed Bronze, Silver and Gold.

“In general, the 529 industry has taken great strides to improve its offerings over the years, and it's rare to see plans with weak underlying investments or extremely high fees,” Morningstar said.

The four Gold-rated plans were Arkansas’s T. Rowe Price Savings Plan, the Maryland College Investment Plan, Nevada’s Vanguard 529 College Savings Plan and the Utah Educational Savings Plan.

In addition to these tiers, Morningstar also assigned 32 neutral ratings. While these plans are unlikely to deliver standout risk-adjusted returns, they are also unlikely to significantly underperform the market. These plans may have shorter track records or untested components. Relatively expensive plans with meaningful state benefits may also earn a neutral rating. While the price tags will be a drag on results, they could have appeal for in-state residents.

Three plans earned negative ratings, an indication that the plans have one or more significant flaws that are likely to hold them back over the long term. Those were Arizona’s Ivy Funds InvestEd 529, the Kansas Schwab 529 College Savings Plan and South Dakota’s College Access 529.

Morningstar upgraded five plans and downgraded ten, compared with seven upgrades and one downgrade in 2013.

Although more plans moved down than up this year, those on the rise lowered expenses or increased the quality of their investment lineups. Meanwhile, the downgraded plans' investment options either had deteriorated or were unproven.

“You get a really great tax benefit for investing in a 529,” said Kathryn Spica, a Certified Financial Advisor and author of the report. “All of them are exempt from Federal capital gains tax. So any growth of your assets is exempt from that tax.”

Many states also offer tax benefits with these plans. “For many, you can get a tax credit or deductions on your contributions,” she said. “So it will really reduce your taxes at the state level.”

Spica added that 529s were “a little more restricted than a taxable account" and that "you have a limited set of investments and the fees can be higher” compared to a similar mutual fund. In most cases, she said, tax benefits will outweigh additional costs. Withdrawals can always be made, but they are taxed and there is a 10% penalty on money taken out for non-educational purposes.

Read More: 529 Plans Are Ever-Necessary But Remain Obscure

“You want to look around in your home state, so you know what type of advantage you have," she said about choosing a plan. "If you have a tax deduction, that’s a compelling reason to stay. If you don’t have one, or you just want to see if you’re getting the best value, look around nationally. You want to look for states with plans that have strong oversight and a strong manager.”

“The gold and silver plans include those that I believe are among the best,” said Mark Kanrowitz, senior vice president and publisher of Edvisors.com. “I’m surprised Pennsylvania isn’t on one of those lists.”

Apart from the return on investment of individual funds or the amount of money individual savers can contribute, there is an important distinction to be made between people who start saving for an infant and those who wait until middle school — or later.

“With newborns, the focus should be on minimizing fees, not state income tax benefits,” said Kantrowitz, “because the tax credits or deductions are amortized over more years, diluting their value. When college is just a few years away, the state income tax credit or deduction have more of an impact compared to fees. Overall, one seeks to maximize the net return on investment, taking into account the investment returns, the risk tolerance, the fees and state income credits or deductions.”

The net return will vary based on the balance of fees and state tax benefit as well as asset allocation.
