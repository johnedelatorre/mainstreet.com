First Cannabis License Lottery Applicants Approved In Clark County, Nevada

  • Marguerite Arnold
  • May 19, 2014 8:53 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — While the cannabis business is hardly the most controversial industry to become legal in Nevada, it already seems to be one of the most popular. The state legalized marijuana use for medicinal purposes in 2001, but it took another 12 years for state approval of legal dispensaries. Brian Sandoval, state's Republican governor, signed the measure last June.

The first lottery for dispensary licenses just came to a close in Clark County in southern Nevada, the largest (albeit unincorporated) county in the state. The lottery attracted 206 applications for medical marijuana businesses from 100 in-state and national enterprises and individuals before the final deadline in late April. Applicants include those interested in establishing dispensaries, grow farms, production facilities and testing facilities. There are only ten dispensary licenses available although currently no limit on cultivation or processing facilities. All owners will also have to pass background checks. Clark County has to date two-thirds of the existing medical marijuana facilities in the state.

Winning license holders will also have to submit a non-refundable $5,000 license processing fee and be well underway in the zoning process to finalize license approval. While this may sound expensive if not onerous, it is cheap compared to similar proposals in Las Vegas for the same. Per current guidelines now being drafted, successful Las Vegas applicants will have to pay over $100,000 in dispensary applications, approval and licensing fees and other costs associated with compliance (including security measures).

Clark County's early efforts, however, may set the tone for the state both in places where the industry flourishes and where it does not. Two cities within Clark County (Henderson and Boulder City) have voted to ban medical marijuana sales altogether.

Cultivation facilities in areas which allow the new business will be limited to formerly industrial zones and prohibited from opening within 660 feet of residential areas. Dispensaries will also be required to purchase products from county growers.

Applicants for the licenses in Clark County's lottery are also attracting attention because they include well known individuals, former politicians, judges and even a former NFL star. What is truly remarkable about this line up is how fast the reputation of the medical cannabis vertical is becoming not only respectable but drawing established names from other industries.

In Nevada, this includes people like Sig Roglich who not only publicly led an anti-medical marijuana ballot initiative via his public relations firm in the last decade, but worked as a presidential advisor to two Republican presidents known for ramping up the "War on Drugs" (Ronald Reagan and George H.W. Bush). That said, Clark County is not alone in seeing many former politicians, even ones with a noted "anti-drug" stance, lining up for a shot, even from a medicinal perspective, at the "green rush."

Clark County may lead the state in gauging both interest from monied, politically connected business people if not being first out of the gate in moving forward with the state cannabis initiative, but several municipalities are not far behind. Las Vegas is still drafting regulations and other jurisdictions are still voting to allow sales at all. Reno, the state's third largest city has yet to move forward on approval of zoning regulations.

That said, in two out of three of the state's largest cities now about to take the plunge, green rush real estate speculation is well underway. In Reno, prices on potential grow areas have already seen increases of between 10% and 30% even with no guarantee that the same will qualify for licenses or obtain approval to use them. Such speculation is not likely to dampen anytime soon in the wake of City Council actions to lift the dispensary ban in Reno last week.

Overall, while Nevada's medical cannabis industry remains in many ways behind front runners in Colorado and Washington, local development of the industry could still have national import if not impact. Democrat Senate Majority Leader Harry Reid, the state's senior federal senator, expressed support for medical marijuana in January of this year for the first time. With a booming local business that will include many former highly placed political lobbyists if not former politicians including some on the federal level, however, it is clear that the political winds for the industry are changing fast now.

And while Nevada may not lead the country in implementing regulatory infrastructure or changing hearts and minds for the average person, it is a safe bet to predict that what is happening in Nevada in its own way will add to a national debate almost 100 years in the making at the end of America's longest, if not most costly, prohibition campaign.

--Written by Marguerite Arnold for MainStreet
