Why Tricking Out Your Car Can Hurt Its Resale Value

  • Jason Notte
  • Sep 14, 2015 4:13 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) – You can dump hundreds to thousands of dollars into filling your car with optional amenities, but none of it guarantees your car will be worth more when you sell it.

In fact, many of those extras may only help the values your vehicle depreciate faster.

The folks at Hearst Business and National Auto Research's auto pricing service Black Book note that features including navigation, a power sunroof, leather seating, oversized wheels, power/heated seats and rear-seat DVD entertainment systems can have great difficulty holding their value. Anil Goyal, vice president of automotive valuation and analytics from Black Book, notes that a fully loaded car that comes at an $8,000 premium above the base model can see the value of that premium decrease to $1,500 in just three years.

“Technology from yesteryear is aging at an advanced rate and can have limited appeal within the used market,” says Ivan Drury, senior analyst for vehicle data and pricing site Edmunds.com. “This is almost like someone asking for more money because their vehicle is equipped with a built-in car phone: at one point this was seen as luxurious, now it would be an oddity. I wouldn't go so far as to say the latest batch of navigation and technology features are like the car phone just yet, but they may go that route faster than the car phone did.”

Just about every automaker updating its entertainment and information systems to touchscreen consoles. General Motors and Chrysler have begun adding in-car Wi-Fi to their vehicles. Even Chrysler's venerable minivan is upgrading its rear-seat DVD players to Blu-ray compatibility -- just as passengers have figured out how to watch downloaded and streaming video on their handheld devices. All of this is driving an increase in vehicle leasing as consumers treat advancing vehicle technology much the same as they treat advancing smartphone techonology. However, it isn't doing a whole lot for the old technology's resale value.

“New technology has the added burden of not being new for very long,” says Eric Ibara, director of residual values for vehicle pricing site Kelley Blue Book. “Although the focus may be on navigation and adaptive cruise control today, years ago we were talking about airbags and ABS. Kelley Blue Book typically doesn’t increase residual values for these types of equipment partly because they are so difficult to measure.”

Since new technology isn't added in every vehicle, it's viewed as part of a trim of premium items -- which makes it difficult to measure value of the same vehicle with and without that technology. Also, as that technology becomes more common, it reduces the value of that perk in vehicles at auction. Still, it takes a bit of time for that technology to work its way through the system. Edmunds's Drury notes that consumers buying used vehicles from dealerships picked up vehicles with an average purchase age of 4.5 years old. If they had a traded in their old vehicle (as 29% did during spring of 2015), it was typically 9.2 years old.

"Depreciation on these features doesn't differ much from other options,” he says. “For many buyers of used vehicles this might be the first time they've had navigation or TV-equipped headrests in their vehicle.”

This doesn't mean that other options are holding their value all that well. KBB's Ibara notes that options and equipment added to a base vehicle typically do not hold their value as well as the base vehicle itself. If that equipment gets some “value pricing” and doesn't cost a whole lot at the outset, it may not depreciate nearly as much. However, Ibara notes that there's routinely a wide gulf in the price new car buyers are willing to pay for the exact options they want and the price used car buyers will pay for those options secondhand three to five years later.

The power moonroof is perhaps the best illustration of that steep drop in value. Ibara notes that a moon roof can lose roughly 70% of its initial value just three years later, which would bring down a cars entire value based on its overall sale price.

“This would occur because the moon roof is priced at $1,000 new but retains only $300 after three years,” Ibara says. “The base car, on the other hand, could carry a residual value of 50%. Therefore, the car with the moon roof could have a residual value of 49%, one point lower than the car without the moon roof.”

If you want to trick out your car while still retaining as much resale value as possible, it helps to pick the right options. Power seats and heated seats are becoming more prevalent and vehicles and add little residual value, even if they're considered a premium feature now. Swapping out those cloth seats for leather, however, will likely pay off nicely.

“Leather seats installed at the factory typically holds value over time,” Ibara says. “I believe it is generally understood that this is something that has value over cloth seats. There are notable exceptions -- black leather in the South -- but certain equipment like leather and sunroofs tend to bring incremental dollars at auction.”

Also, if you're choosing between power trains, just keep your vehicle in mind. The difference between a four-cylinder Toyota Camry and a six-cylinder model likely isn't going to be all that great by the time it hits the used market. However, if you're looking at a pickup truck, sports car or a high-performance engine for any of the above, that can certainly make a difference.


“Trucks with optional diesel engines retain much of that diesel engine option value, especially given diesel's long standing reputation for power, durability and fuel economy,” says Drury. “The same can be said for sports cars and special trim levels like the BMW M3 or Honda Civic SI. A notable exception is when fuel prices fluctuate, hybrids and EVs can lose or retain value at a more volatile rate compared to their standard counterparts.”

Ibara notes that dealers who go to used care auctions often walk the lot prior to the start of the sale to see exactly what equipment and options are on the vehicles they're interested in. If they're in a snowy region, an all-wheel drive sport utility will bring more money than a two-wheel drive model. If those 20-inch rims aren't factory installed and don't quite line up with the car you've attached them to, however, that's not going to help your case.

“I think we’ve all seen either outrageously over-the-top wheels that would likely detract from the top dollar that the vehicle would otherwise obtain at auction,” Ibara says. “On the other hand, elegant factory-installed 22” wheels on a Cadillac Escalade could look really sharp. In the latter case, it will likely bring more money for the seller at auction.”

However, just because they diminish a car's resale value by being attached to it, that doesn't mean that after-market accessories like chrome wheels and spoilers are necessarily bad investments. You just may not want to sell them with the vehicle in question.

“If someone is going to sell a used vehicle with aftermarket wheels, and he/she still has the [factory] wheels on hand, then in most cases selling the aftermarket wheels separate and reinstalling the [factory] units is the way to go,” Drury says.
