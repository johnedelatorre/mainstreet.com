Low-End Housing Boost Means Millennials Moving Out of the Basement

  • Jeff Brown
  • Jan 29, 2015 10:00 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — You'd think homebuyers, given their druthers, would rather prices stayed low. But, in fact, a recovery in the low-end home market is good news, and not just for folks looking for an inexpensive property. The entire market will be healthier if the lower end of the market does better.

Fortunately, it is. A report from Zillow.com, the data and home-sales site, says the low-end market is recovering nicely, with prices in the cheapest third of the market growing by 6.8% in 2014. While that's just a tad higher than the 6.6% gain for all homes, it is encouraging because until last year the low-end market has been slower to rebound.

"In many ways, for the housing market to fully normalize, it has to start at the bottom," Zillow Chief Economist Stan Humphries says. 

Though the average low-end home is still priced at 17% below the peak value before the recession, this is a marked improvement from the 31% loss when the market hit bottom in January 2012. The gains mean that fewer of these homeowners are underwater, owing more than their home is worth. Getting back in the black makes it possible to sell a home for enough to pay off the mortgage, drawing more sellers into the market.

With more homes for sale, potential buyers have more choices, causing more of them to get into the market. And many people selling low-end homes are likely to buy homes costing a bit more. Rising rents are encouraging many renters to buy, Zillow says.

"More lower-end home sellers will help meet demand from entry-level buyers, and these sellers in turn will re-enter the market in search of a slightly pricier home, which will entice more middle- and upper-tier sellers to list their homes," Humphries says. 

"As the economy gets stronger, we expect more young adults to strike out on their own, moving out of friends' and parents' homes. This will create strong demand in coming months, especially for less expensive homes," he says.

Says Zillow: "Going into the home-buying season in 2015, homebuyers can expect to find more homes on the market and less competition from all-cash bidders. Metros with the biggest jump over last year in low-end inventory are Las Vegas, with 66.9% more low-end homes on the market in December 2014 than December 2013, Riverside, with 47.3% more, and Washington, D.C., with 45.7% more."

In the bottom third of the market, the median home now sells for $101.400, compared with $84,100 three years ago.

Some markets, of course, are doing better than others. Price gains around 20% were registered for low-end homes in markets such as Miami-Fort Lauderdale, Atlanta and Denver. Gains were lower than 3% in St Louis, Mo., Pittsburgh, Cincinnati, Cleveland and Columbus.

— By Jeff Brown for MainStreet
