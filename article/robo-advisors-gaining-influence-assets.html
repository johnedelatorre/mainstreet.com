Robo Advisors Gaining Influence, Assets

  • Hal M. Bundrick
  • Apr 10, 2014 11:03 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Eschewing regular face-to-face portfolio reviews and higher fees, a growing number of investors are turning to online investment advice, rather than traditional financial advisors. And the trend is particularly driven by young adults – Gen X and Y – the very generations advisors are seeking to replace an aging clientele.

The online startups, frequently referred to as "robo advisors" by the financial services industry, perhaps in a transparent ploy to diminish their credibility, are now gaining assets under management at an increasing clip. Corporate Insight, a financial services research firm, tracks over 200 of these "fin-tech" startups, and analyst Grant Easterbrook was able to quantify the impact of these firms.

Compiling data from just 11 of the leading online advice firms, Easterbrook found that they directly manage nearly $2.6 billion of clients' assets, while providing paid investment advice for another $9 billion -- a total of over $11.5 billion under management or advisement.

While that amount is a tiny sliver of the $33 trillion of assets under management as of 2012 in North America according to PwC, it is from a seed planted just a short time ago.

The 11 firms from which the data is based include the best known online investment advice providers, including Assetbuilder, Betterment, Covestor, Financial Guard, FutureAdvisor, Jemstep, MarketRiders, Personal Capital, RebalanceIRA, SigFig and Wealthfront. Corporate Insight says these startups are using technology to provide investment advice at lower prices and with lower minimum investment levels than traditional financial advisors.

"Members of Generations X and Y value online and mobile services more than quarterly face-to-face meetings," says Easterbrook. "They also tend to be more concerned about costs and transparency than their parents are. These factors benefit startups, which provide more engaging digital services and more affordable advice than traditional wealth management firms offer."

These new-breed investment firms offer a range of services, from algorithm-based analysis of a client's portfolio, to human financial advisors that never meet clients face-to-face. Easterbrook's research predicts that algorithm-based advice is likely to have the biggest impact, at least in the immediate future.

"It addresses a gap in the mass affluent investor market, [that is] the lack of affordable, objective and rigorous portfolio analysis and investment advice," he says. "After algorithm-based advice, trade mimicking probably holds the strongest near-term promise. It's well suited to active traders and sophisticated self-directed investors who want to benefit from the success of other traders/investors."

The distinct advantages of the platforms, according to Easterbrook, are:

  • Transparent and affordable pricing -- Most of these firms offer greater transparency on pricing compared to major financial institutions, especially full-service brokerage firms that tend to hide commissions and fees.
  • Better consumer-facing technology & performance tracking -- These new online services tend to have more modern and user-friendly online platforms than many established brokerage firms, particularly full-service brokerage firms.
  • Passive index investing -- Most of the startups tend to direct clients to passive low-cost ETF investments. This reflects a broader industry trend away from active management toward passive management

    • However, Easterbrook says the rising robo advisors will still face challenges gaining broad acceptance.

    "It's one thing to buy a book or a t-shirt from an e-commerce site – it's another thing to trust one's life savings with a small company that has no track record and next-to-no human element," he says. "This makes it all the more troubling that many investing startups do little to prove their investment expertise and trustworthiness. They may highlight their lower fees or explain modern portfolio theory, but they rarely devote significant website real estate to establishing the credentials of the people behind their products."

    --Written by Hal M. Bundrick for MainStreet
