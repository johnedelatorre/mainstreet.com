Why It's Good That Homeowners and Appraisers Are Thinking Alike

  • Jeff Brown
  • Dec 16, 2014 8:08 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — What's a home worth? Unless it's a cookie-cutter condo it's hard to know for sure, because there's too much variation. You don't have thousands of comparable trades like with corn, wheat or oil, so value's always debatable.

That's especially so when the housing market is in a collapse or bubble, when views lag reality and ride a roller coaster of emotion.

So today's perception gap — or, more accurately, lack of one — is notable, showing that homeowners and professional appraisers have dramatically narrowed their differences in home-value views since the depths of the housing market bust in 2008. It's another sign of a return to normal.

From the end of 2006 though the first three quarters of 2013, appraisers typically valued homes lower than homeowners did, according to a study by Quicken Loans. The widest gap was in the second half of 2008, when a home valued at $200,000 by the owner was appraised at less than $186,000 by the pros. 

The gap narrowed until late last year, when appraisers started coming up with higher values than homeowners. In November, a typical home valued at $200,000 by the homeowner was appraised at a little over $202,000.

"It is reassuring to see the gap between appraiser opinions and homeowner opinions narrow, and if we had to choose a side of the fence, it makes for a much smoother mortgage process if appraisers are valuing homes above homeowners' estimates like we're seeing, as compared to the opposite," said Bob Walters, Quicken Loans' chief economist.

The value-perception gap of recent years is easy to understand. When prices rise or fall rapidly, appraisers who watch the market constantly are likely to have more up-to-date views than homeowners who don't.

Homeowners' guesstimates can be skewed by the prevailing sense of optimism or pessimism, but appraisers aren't always on the money, either. The profession was widely criticized for overvaluing homes in the early and mid-2000s, only to be accused of becoming too conservative after the collapse revealed the earlier mistakes.

Today, emotions aren't running as high, eliminating one of the wild cards in estimating home values. But more important, the home-price rebound has slowed. An appraiser or homeowner can therefore look at sales further back in time to analyze current values, which allows estimates to be based on a larger number of sales, increasing the odds of finding examples that are truly comparable.

Aside from being a sign of normalcy, the narrowed price-perception gap reduces risks for all concerned. Buyers can be more confident they are not paying too much and sellers have fewer second thoughts about their asking price, allowing them to hold firm against lowball offers.

Mortgage applicants, whether buying or refinancing, can shell out application and appraisal fees confident that they won't get a lower-than-expected appraisal that will torpedo their application.

— By Jeff Brown for MainStreet
