Rich Americans Would Rather Pay for Scholarships Than Senate Seats

  • Jason Notte
  • Nov 17, 2014 8:30 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) – With the midterm elections over, the vitriolic ads off the air and the nation's lawns free of campaign signs, it turns out rich U.S. residents not named Koch or Soros or quite as wealthy as them didn't want to sink their cash into politics.

According to PNC Bank's Wealth and Values Survey — distributed to 945 adults, including 476 with assets of $1 million or more — U.S. millionaires would rather focus on charitable causes than political contributions. Almost 70% of wealthy Americans support charitable initiatives focused on poverty and hunger in America and nearly two-thirds support scholarships for children of lower-income families. Meanwhile, only about 31% prioritize contributions to political parties — putting them near the bottom of their shopping list.

Also, unlike more famous billionaire political lightning rods, the majority of the millionaires (52%) surveyed shy away from wanting public recognition rather than seek it out (as 45% did). Of those who donated to a political party or candidate in the past year, 41% give less than $500, with 21% giving $500 to $1,000. Only 35% gave more than $1,000.

“These findings show the wealthy realize that our society is better when everyone is in the game and earning – and that economic inequality can have negative consequences,” said Thomas P. Melcher, executive vice president and head of PNC’s family office, in a press release. “Their commitment to charities focused on poverty and education show they care about the future and want to make an impact with their contributions.”

In fact, 64% of millionaires are concerned about economic inequality in America and cite it as the reason for preferring charitable donations to political contributions. Roughly 96% have made a financial contribution, volunteered or donated goods or services to nonprofit organizations in the past year. Two-thirds (66%) have encouraged their children to give time and money to philanthropic organizations.

Along those same lines, 49% support raising the minimum wage (38% oppose it) while 44% would like to see taxes raised on top income earners (though 41% aren't enamored of that idea). Nearly half are extremely concerned about U.S. military involvement in global conflicts (46%) and immigration reform (44%).

This all runs counter to what Ken Stern, the author of With Charity for All: Why Charities Are Failing and a Better Way to Give found in his research. As he told The Atlantic last year, Americans with earnings in the top 20% of income levels contributed, on average, 1.3% of their income to charity in 2011. Those at the bottom 20% donated 3.2% of their cash to charity. Stern points out that the latter don't generally itemize their tax returns as the former do, which means they aren't in it for the charitable tax deduction.

The Chronicle of Philanthropy backed him up in August 2012. It found that people who make between $50,000 and $75,000 give an average of 7.6% of their discretionary income to charity, compared with an average 4.2% for those making $100,000 or more. Stern also noted that while the wealthy donate to service organizations and scholarship funds, the likelihood of them choosing universities and cultural institutions over human services and relief organizations was greater.

No, they're not lining up for political party dinner fundraisers and a chance to shake hands with a senator, but that doesn't mean the nation's rich aren't particular about how they're doling out their charitable contributions.
