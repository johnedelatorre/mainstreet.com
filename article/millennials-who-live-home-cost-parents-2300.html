Millennials Who Live at Home Cost Parents $2,300

  • Ellen Chang
  • Jul 1, 2014 3:06 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Andrew Snead, 23, headed back home after graduating from Bentley University with a degree in finance and economics so he could bolster his lackluster savings.

Snead, who works at Private Client Resources, a Wilton, Conn. software company, has managed to save more than half of his salary in the past six months and has contributed the maximum amount of $5,000 to his IRA. He has been able to accumulate a large sum of savings, because he does not have any debt and his parents are footing the bill for him to live at home and save money.

Snead said he is fortunate that his parents pay 95% of the groceries, his $40 monthly cell phone bill and his health insurance. His expenses include food and his health insurance co-pays and deductibles.

He estimates it costs his parents over $2,500 a year so that he can start off his career without accumulating any debt. By next summer, Snead plans to have $30,000 in savings so he can move cross-country and remain debt free.

"I want to get ahead a little bit," he said. "I feel like people are missing out on having a safety net."

A survey by vouchercloud, a U.K.-based coupon company, showed that adults age 21 and over living at home cost their parents $2,300 on average every year in the U.S., with food consisting of the biggest expense. The survey revealed that 41% of the parents said they spend over $2,000 each year for their children to live at home while only 8% of the 2,582 respondents said they spend less than $1,000 annually.

Food cost an average of $1,250, followed by $440 in utilities and $250 for a cell phone contract.

The survey found that 63% of parents said their children do contribute to household expenses while 37% received nothing. The respondents said 66% of the adult children who lived in the family home were male and 34% of respondents said their female children resided with them. The parents also said that 42% of their children living at home were between 21 to 24 years old, 37% had children between 25 to 29 years old and 21% had children over the age of 30.

"With the current economic situation and the recent rise in house prices, it is no surprise to see a number of American adults still living at home with their parents," said Matthew Wood, managing director of vouchercloud. "It's interesting to see the average young adult seems to favor staying at home with mom and dad."

Young adults ages 18 to 34 are increasingly living at home, making it a new norm. In 2012, 36% of people 18 to 31, the so-called Millennial generation, lived with their parents, according to a Pew Research Center analysis of U.S. Census Bureau data. Prior to the Great Recession in 2007, only 32% lived at home and 34% of the same-aged counterparts were residing at home in 2009 when the recession ended, the 2013 analysis found.

In 2012, there was a record total of 21.6 million Millennials living with their parents, an increase from 18.5 million in 2007, including at least a third and "perhaps as many as half are college students," according to the Pew Research Center analysis.

Living at home may have become a necessity for some Millennials. When asked whether they would prefer to live at home with their parents and have health insurance or live alone and go uninsured, 56% reported in a recent eHealth survey that they would prefer to live on their own even if it meant that they had to go uninsured.

"The cost of health insurance can be an additional burden for young adults these days," said Carrie McLean, director of customer care at eHealth.com, an online health insurance exchange based in Mountain View, Calif. "They value it and know they need it if they want to avoid a tax penalty, but the costs of coverage are a challenge."

More than six in ten of the young adult policy holders surveyed said that they cannot comfortably afford their health insurance premiums. A similar number say that they do not have enough money saved to pay their annual deductible in case of a serious injury or hospitalization.

"In order to find the plan best suited to their personal needs and budget, we recommend that young adults review all their coverage options and make use of online comparison shopping tools like those at eHealth.com," she said.

For the past four years, Daniella DiMartino has been living at home with her parents and diligently tracking every bill and expense in a spreadsheet to ensure she is not overspending. DiMartino contributes by paying for her cell phone plan, car insurance and a portion of her parents' property tax.

Living at home remained her only option after accumulating $58,000 in debt from student loans, credit cards and a car loan, which supersedes her annual salary. After paying her bills each month, DiMartino is left with $100 that she socks away in a higher-yielding savings account for emergencies.

"My goal is to help my parents as soon as possible, but they want me to pay off my loans as soon as possible so they are more understanding and willing to help me out," she said. "I live at home simply because I cannot afford to live on my own."

While she lives on a tight budget and rarely eats out or goes out with her friends, the 26-year-old account executive at a public relations firm in Westchester, N.Y., has reduced her debt by $16,000.

"With my current salary it has been difficult," she said. "Once I make more money I will probably still be stuck at home, but hopefully will be able to chip away more at my debt."

 

Millennials should focus on paying off unsecured credit card debt first, said Bob Gavlak, a certified financial planner with Strategic Wealth Partners in Independence, Ohio. Credit card debt is usually the "result of consumption spending and also usually carries the highest interest rate," he said.

"You pay the most for debt that does not have a physical asset tied to it," Gavlak said. "You should then focus on all other debt such as auto and student loans and prioritize them based on the interest rate."

Millennials who find themselves living at home after graduation should consider it a temporary situation, he said.

"I feel that getting out as soon as possible is the most reasonable assumption as it forces the individual into making the difficult budgeting decisions," Gavlak said. "Living at home can sometimes lead to complacency and with the extra income you can often fall into just wasting it on clothes, food, or entertainment as opposed to real life bills. There's a fine line between getting onto your feet and taking advantage of the situation."

--Written by Ellen Chang for MainStreet
