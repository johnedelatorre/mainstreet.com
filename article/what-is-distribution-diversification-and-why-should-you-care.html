What Is Distribution Diversification? And Why Should You Care?

  • Mark Henricks
  • Aug 18, 2015 3:52 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — You have finally gotten the idea that you need to diversify the assets in your retirement portfolio. You have saved regularly and often and invested in a balanced mix of stocks, bonds and other assets. As a result, you have reduced risk and improved your odds of enjoying a comfortable retirement. You're not done yet.

Now you need to learn about another kind of diversification: distribution diversification. This is the flip side of the equation -- how and when you’ll take money out of your retirement accounts to spend it after retirement. Basically, you want to plan so that you pluck the fruits of your foresight in such a way that the tree doesn’t go bare until you no longer need earthly riches.

Distribution diversification is asset allocation’s lesser-known sibling, but it’s no less important. A bad distribution decision can cost you dearly. To give one example, once you reach age 70.5, the IRS requires you to withdraw at least a certain amount from your retirement accounts each year. Penalties for failing to take required minimum distributions could be 50% of the amount that should have been withdrawn. So if you should have taken $10,000 and you took zero, you may be out $5,000.

“Some of these things seem subtle but they’re profound,” says LouAnn Schulfer, a financial planner in Stevens Point, Wis. “It may look the same on the outside, but the mechanics of it and the long-term effects are bigger than it appears.”

The basic idea behind distribution diversification is to give yourself enough flexibility that you can, taking into consideration taxes and changing costs for essential as well as optional expenses, make your retirement last as long as possible. “Now it’s not about growth,” Schulfer says. “It’s about how you’re going to receive lifetime income from that nest egg you’ve worked so hard to build.”

The simplest ways to take a distribution from a retirement account is in a single payment. Some 401(k) plans require participants who withdraw any of their funds to take all of them. Others allow partial distributions, either regularly on a pre-planned schedule of, say, $5,000 a month, or on an ad hoc basis. Large withdrawals can create burdensome tax consequences, to name just one consideration.

After-tax retirement accounts such as Roth IRAs and Roth 401(k)s provide a good way to build in distribution diversification. These let you withdraw contributions as well as earnings without paying any taxes on them. Having a significant portion of your retirement nest egg in after-tax accounts can add a lot of flexibility to manage your post-retirement tax bill.

Annuities that provide you with a set monthly amount of income for life also offer opportunities to gain post- retirement distribution diversification. In addition to the annuities sold by life insurance companies, you may get regular guaranteed income streams from corporate and public employer pensions. The reliability of annuities encourages some planners to recommend that some retirement savers invest some of their funds in them. “You don’t want to put all your money in that one bucket,” Schuler says. “But it’s a diversifier from other investments.”

Almost everyone participates in another guaranteed income stream -- Social Security. Your distribution diversification decision here probably revolves around the age at which you should apply for Social Security benefits. Generally, people who retire later are eligible for higher benefits, but whether any given retiree should wait or apply as soon as possible can depend on several factors, including local costs of living and expected medical costs.

“There are some significant benefits you can gain by deferring and putting off taking your Social Security, “ says Marina Edwards, senior retirement consultant with Towers Watson in Chicago. “How much and the amounts vary from person to person. That decision can be very difficult for the average person to handle.”

Generally speaking, distribution diversification is hard for any average person to handle. The interplay of shifting, tough-to-predict factors such as future tax rates, life expectancy and investment returns -- coupled with the stark fact that, for most retirees, their wage-earning days are over -- all combine to make pre-retirement asset allocation seem relatively simple. “It’s terribly complex,” Edwards says.

Some distribution diversification advice is counter-intuitive, similar to the way asset allocation theory says investing in bonds that yield less than stocks can improve long-term portfolio performance. Yet as retirement savers approach the point at which they will become retirement spenders, diversifying distributions becomes ever more important.

Schulfer says she’s seeing more and more people who have worked hard, saved hard and invested assets wisely but still don’t know exactly what to do now that retirement is at hand and distribution, not accumulation, is the issue. “They get to a point where they want to retire in two years and ask me, ‘What do I do with this?’”
