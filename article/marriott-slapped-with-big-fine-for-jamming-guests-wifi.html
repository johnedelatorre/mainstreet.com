Marriott Slapped with Big Fine for Jamming Guests’ WiFi

  • Robert McGarvey
  • Oct 7, 2014 11:44 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — You are not being paranoid when your personal WiFi Hotspot fails to work at a hotel and you think - just maybe - the greedy hotelier is trying to force you to shell out cash to use its typically anemic WiFi. The Federal Communications Commission now has said, loudly, that hotels sometimes block personal WiFi, and the FCC has also said that is unlawful.

It recently slapped the big hotel chain Marriott with a $600,000 fine for blocking personal WiFi at its Opryland Hotel and Convention Center in Nashville.

“Consumers who purchase cellular data plans should be able to use them without fear that their personal Internet connection will be blocked by their hotel or conference center,” said FCC Enforcement Bureau Chief Travis LeBlanc in a statement. “It is unacceptable for any hotel to intentionally disable personal hotspots while also charging consumers and small businesses high fees to use the hotel’s own Wi-Fi network. This practice puts consumers in the untenable position of either paying twice for the same service or forgoing Internet access altogether.”

The FCC elaborated that it opened this case in March 2013 when it received a complaint from a consumer who claimed his access had been blocked at the Marriott facility.

Read More: How Good Are the Deals on Hotel Tonight?

The FCC went on: “After conducting an investigation, the Enforcement Bureau found that employees of Marriott...had used features of a Wi-Fi monitoring system at the Gaylord Opryland to contain and/or de-authenticate guest-created Wi-Fi hotspot access points in the conference facilities. In some cases, employees sent de-authentication packets to the targeted access points, which would dissociate consumers’ devices from their own Wi-Fi hotspot access points and, thus, disrupt consumers’ current Wi-Fi transmissions and prevent future transmissions. At the same time that these employees engaged in these practices, Marriott charged conference exhibitors and other attendees anywhere from $250 to $1,000 per device to use the Gaylord Wi-Fi service in the conference facilities.”

Marriott, for its part, told the FCC to buzz off. A statement issued by Marriott reads thusly: “Marriott has a strong interest in ensuring that when our guests use our Wi-Fi service, they will be protected from rogue wireless hotspots that can cause degraded service, insidious cyber-attacks and identity theft. Like many other institutions and companies in a wide variety of industries, including hospitals and universities, the Gaylord Opryland protected its Wi-Fi network by using FCC-authorized equipment provided by well-known, reputable manufacturers. We believe that the Gaylord Opryland's actions were lawful. We will continue to encourage the FCC to pursue a rulemaking in order to eliminate the ongoing confusion resulting from today's action and to assess the merits of its underlying policy.“

Especially Orwellian about the Marriott response is the assertion that, somehow, the Marriott provided WiFi is more secure than a guest’s own HotSpot (typically created with a smartphone or a MiFi card). The opposite is more often true, multiple security experts told Mainstreet. Hotel WiFi networks have often housed criminal intruders, whereas personal HotSpots - run over cellular data networks and almost always requiring a password for login - are typically much more secure. Smart travelers often call up a personal Hotspot before doing anything that requires a password to login such as banking or even shopping at Amazon.

Travelers, for their parts, expressed outrage about Marriott’s practices. Travel blogger Johnny Jet - who characterized Marriott’s behavior as “disgusting” - took particular umbrage with Marriott’s statement. Said Jet: “That’s just a bunch of baloney. The person(s) who made the decision to block the WiFi was just trying to extort money out of their own guests.”

Marketing guru Sherre L. DeMao said: “I could not be more pleased that FCC has taken action. I have found this [hotel blocks on personal WiFi] to be more and more the case, rendering my personal hotspot/MiFi useless when I travel.”

Public speaking guru Dianna Booher said she had just experienced exactly the same at a Las Vegas strip hotel - not a Marriott.

“I ran into this problem for the first time this week at the [Famous Hotel] in Las Vegas-and it made me furious," she said. "As a professional speaker, I travel more than 100 nights a year and stay in the most upscale hotels. But when I discovered that the [FH] was blocking my Wi-FI everywhere in the hotel and charging $15.99 per day for access to their Internet to get email, I was quite shocked.”

Bottomline: Almost certainly this is not a practice limited to Marriott, so next time your Hotspot refuses to work at a hotel - and you are redirected to pony up double digits for the establishment’s own WiFi - good advice from travel experts is to dig in your heels at the front desk and demand free WiFi. Cite the Marriott case, invoke the FCC position and just maybe reason will prevail.

At least you can hope.
