5 Most Lucrative Side Hustles for Professionals Needing Higher Income

  • Kathryn Tuggle
  • Mar 11, 2015 7:00 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — With rising rents and hefty student loan bills to pay, it's no wonder many Millennials need a secondary source of income just to stay afloat. After the 9 to 5 grind is done, more workers are turning to a "side hustle," or second job, many of which can be done from home. If you're thinking about taking on a part-time gig, here's a look at five of the most lucrative, and how to stay within the boundaries of your full-time employer's "conflict of interest" policy.

1. Per diem attorney

If you have a law degree and your schedule allows it, working as a per diem attorney can be a great way to earn extra cash, says Christina Stoneburner, a member of the labor and employment department at Fox Rothschild.

"Per diem attorneys are often hired to cover a court appearance or a deposition, or other off-hours hearings," Stoneburner says.

Before accepting any position with another firm or company, check with your employer to see if there is a conflict-of-interest policy, she says.

"There may be issues with you working for someone else. Clients contact us all the time because they find out they have employers who work elsewhere. Most of the time it doesn't impact their regular job, but sometimes it does."

Read your employee handbook thoroughly to see if there is any language that prohibits a side job. Most all conflict-of-interest policies forbid working for a competing company or creating competing products.

"If you're working at a law firm and you knit scarves and sell them on Etsy, you could safely say that is not a conflict of interest, but sometimes the line isn't so clear," she says.

2. IT developer

Information Technology is one of the most common fields for side jobs and freelancing, Stoneburner says.

"So many people write code and do Web design on the side," she says. "It's only going to become more prevalent in the coming years, with people creating apps, designing storefronts and more. People aren't going to hire someone full time if they need an app, they're going to hire an independent contractor."

Even independent contractors must be careful with regard to conflict of interest. You can't design a custom app for one client and sell the exact same app to another client, Stoneburner cautions.

For many people, one of the main selling points of IT and Web development is the flexibility of schedule.

"It's the kind of thing you can do for two hours in the morning, an hour at lunch and again for two hours in the evening. If you have a job where you never know if you're going to be working from 9 to 5 or 9 to 8, then having something with flexibility of schedule is key," she says. "You don't want to be in a position where you're leaving early from your first job and arriving late to your second job."

3. Tutoring

Whether you work with an established tutoring brand such as Barbri or Kaplan or strike out on your own to teach local high school kids, tutoring can be very lucrative, Stoneburner says.

"If you don't want to worry about building a client base, then work for one of the brands," she says. "It's easy to get in with younger kids, but the more advanced they get, when they're looking for help preparing for the GMAT or LSAT, the less likely they are going to reach out for a private tutor."

If academics aren't your strong suit, look into teaching voice or music lessons.

"If you're doing something once or twice a week, after school, it's easier to coordinate with your regular job. If you have that skill, use it, share it," she says.

4. Art

Online platforms such as Etsy have changed the way the creative arts are monetized, says Ted Murphy, CEO at creator marketplace Izea.

"We are seeing people sell their art everywhere. Some are even joining stock photography sites and selling their photos or illustrations," Murphy says. "There are hundreds of sites out there that allow people to do this. It just depends on what your skill set is."

When opening an online shop, make sure you're prepared for order volume and tax day, Stoneburner says.

"It's only going to be fun if you have some inventory and you don't have to worry about keeping up with orders," she says. "Also, most sites report your earnings, so you'll probably be getting a 1099 at the end of the year."

5. Blogging/tweeting/marketing

Increasingly, people are monetizing their social media accounts and helping brands build audiences, Murphy says.

"There is a huge demand for that, and you can do it completely independent of location, right from your home," he says. "Brands are willing to pay top dollar for people who are influential online and create quality content through YouTube, Vine and others."

One of the most valuable skills someone can have is understanding marketing and branding, Murphy says. Thousands of people have online storefronts but often have no idea how to get the word out about their products. Today, freelance marketing and PR consultants have an opportunity like none other to truly make a name for themselves.

"A lot can be done from home," Stoneburner says. "Not every company needs a full-time blogger or a full-time social media coordinator, but they're happy to pay someone to do that for them a few hours each week. All that person needs is a computer."
