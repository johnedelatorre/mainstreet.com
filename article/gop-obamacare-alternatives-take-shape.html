GOP Obamacare Alternatives Take Shape

  • Eric Reed
  • Aug 19, 2015 4:34 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) -- One thing stands between Republicans and a successful repeal of Obamacare: the 2016 election. Even drawing a Senate filibuster wouldn’t stop a truly determined majority without the threat of a veto, so a successful Presidential bid is key to achieving the party’s long standing goal of repeal-and-replace. Wisconsin Governor Scott Walker and Florida Senator Marco Rubio are the first to float concrete Obamacare alternatives in their campaigns. 

On Tuesday, Walker brought his own candidacy one step closer, releasing a seven-page health care proposal that would strip away most of Obamacare’s regulations and replace them with a system based on tax credits and incentive plans. 

Politically this is a huge step for the conservative movement. Despite the Republicans' fractious primary season, it’s no stretch to imagine a candidate like former Florida Governor Jeb Bush or Walker winning next year’s election and putting his name on a brand new piece of health care legislation.

Since starting to wage war on Obamacare, Republicans have presented relatively few ideas for what would come after, holding 50 votes to repeal the law while proposing little aside from the occasional detail-light white paper. Walker’s plan is a serious one that for the first time has a legitimate shot of someday becoming law. The party may have to stand by these words in a way it never would have during an Obama presidency, and it shows.

In addition to a full repeal of the Affordable Care Act, Walker's Day One Patient Freedom Plan is based on three main elements:

First, it would implement a series of age-based tax credits that would replace Obamacare’s income-based subsidies for everyone who doesn’t receive health insurance from his employer.

Second, it would  eliminate state-based coverage restrictions and allow Americans to buy insurance nationwide.

Third, it would replace the ban on pre-existing conditions with a series of incentives and high-risk pools.

Compared to Obamacare, Walker’s plan would emphasize personal choice for individuals on the non-group market.

Calling his plan a “tax cut of about a trillion dollars” at a Minnesota campaign stop, Walker would replace the direct payments that insurance companies get under Obamacare with annual tax credits that consumers could use to shop for insurance on an open marketplace. These credits would scale by age at a flat rate, starting at $900 for those 0 – 17 up to $3,000 per year for anyone 50 – 64, and would be sent out regardless of income.

Under Obamacare, subsidies are awarded on a need-based calculation, which factors personal income and local cost of living. According the Kaiser Foundation in 2014 approximately 3.5 million people qualified for some form of financial assistance, with an average subsidy of $2,890 per person. 

The campaign also has said that it would reduce premiums considerably by allowing consumers to shop nationwide for insurance, driving down prices through competition.

Walker’s plan would also shift coverage for individuals with pre-existing conditions away from an absolute guarantee and onto a system of (as-yet unspecified) incentives and high-risk pools.

Consumers unable to get insurance because of health problems would be addressed on a state-by-state basis, with an emphasis on government subsidized plans specifically designed for risky or unhealthy individuals. Approximately 34 states have experimented with high risk pools in one form or another since 1976, with middling financial results.


As of a recent study, slightly less than 200,000 people nationwide were enrolled in high risk pools as of December 31, 2008 (before the provisions of the ACA began to kick in). This pool cost approximately $2 billion to subsidize, and individual premiums cost 125% to 200% of normal.

Rubio's plan is similar in its approach to Walker's.

First, he wants to create an "advanceable, refundable tax credit" for Americans that buy health insurance. Rubio proposes that the value of these credits increase each year. 

Second, he wants to lower insurance regulation costs and protect those already infirmed by promising coverage for people with pre-existing conditions and for creating a series of incentives and high-risk pools. Rubio advocates for innovation in health care coverage and emphasizes "consumer-centered" vehicles like Health Savings Accounts.

Third, he wants to strengthen Medicare and Medicaid to push them toward "fiscally-sustainable paths."


Of course, Rubio also emphasized the drain of Obamacare to the American taxpayer with the $1.2 trillion tax hike. But still developing their strategies, Walker and Rubio have promised that their plans will be “budget neutral” while repealing all of Obamacare’s tax increases and suggests paying for it with cuts to Medicaid and taxes on expensive health insurance plans. After all, both candidates have signed Grover Norquist's pledge never to raise a dime of money through new taxation.

This does not, however, address the roughly $800 billion in new spending on Medicare that repealing Obamacare would trigger, nor does it address the Congressional Budget Office’s estimate that repeal would increase the deficit anywhere from $137 to $353 billion. 

In doing away with the much-hated individual mandate, the plans Walker and Rubio have floated will likely score considerable points with his base, which has long pushed for a right wing alternative to the Democratic law. In the long run, however, the plan continues to have questions that will need to be answered.
