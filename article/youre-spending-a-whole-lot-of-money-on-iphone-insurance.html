You're Spending a Whole Lot of Money on iPhone Insurance

  • Jason Notte
  • Oct 3, 2014 5:21 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Consumers who camped out for Apple's iPhone 6 may have been able to upgrade for free, but they're paying quite a bit to protect their phone.

In 2013, holding company Assurant's smartphone insurance provider Protect Your Bubble saw a 157% increase in mobile device policy sales in the two weeks following the iPhone 5s/5c launch. The company is expecting a similar uptick this year, with a whole lot of consumers buying plans to protect their phones from water damage and cracked screens. Protect Your Bubble expects cracked screens to represent 58% of 2014 smartphone gadget claims, while water damage is expected to account for 30%, with manufacturer malfunctions representing the rest.

That all contributes to what the staff at Warranty Week estimates is a roughly $12.1 billion smartphone insurance industry. That's up from just $5.1 billion in 2009 and is driven largely by the rise of the smartphone.

In the past five years, Apple's share of all smartphone insurance plans rose from 5% to 17%. Warranty Week notes that AppleCare and AppleCare+ warranty protection for Apple products accounted for $2.1 billion of Apple's $16 billion in software sales and services last year. That's a big number, but still minor compared with the roughly $8 billion that carriers AT&T, Verizon, Sprint and T-Mobile managed to bring in through their own service plans last year, up from $3.4 billion in 2009. One administrator, Asurion, accounts for more than 80% of that $8 billion. Retailers including Best Buy, Wal-Mart and Target and third-party insurers such as Protect My Bubble and SquareTrade account for another $2.2 billion in smartphone insurance plans.

Altogether, that's 100 million smartphones protected by insurance plans in 2013 compared with just 25 million in 2009, while the number of insured feature phones dropped from 34 million to just 12 million. Why so many? As Asurion points out, roughly 80 million phones get lost, stolen or damaged each year. Without insurance, consumers are left to pay the full value to replace their phones. Asurion says that adds up to an average of $485 per phone, not including the value of any data left on the device. Even worse, 80% of phone damage occurs within the first year of ownership, leaving owners with few options.

“When it comes to iPhones or any mobile device, accidental damage is like Murphy’s Law in that if it can happen, there’s a good chance that it will happen,” says David Anderson, director of product development for Protect Your Bubble.

Still, Warranty Week estimates there are still 200 million mobile devices out there that aren't covered by insurance. That represents not only a whole lot of growth potential for insurance companies, but a proud consumer base that's just a bit overconfident in its ability to keep their hands on their devices and fix them if necessary. In a recent Protect Your Bubble online survey, 45% of respondents said they were unsuccessful when trying to fix their own phone after an accident. Another 20% just outright bought a new phone. Only 8% were actually able to fix a damaged smartphone on their own.

That isn't great when only 39% of those surveyed would still use a damaged phone if it functioned. A full 32% would have it either repaired or replaced immediately.

Yet buying insurance for a smartphone is a gamble in its own right. AppleCare+ extends the warranty on an iPhone and covers two cases of accidental damage, but costs $99 up front and just raised its accidental damage costs from $49 per incident to $79 last year. Most carrier, retailer and independent plans don't have an upfront cost, but feature a monthly payment of $6 to $10 per month and a deductible for broken or stolen devices ranging from $100 to $200.

Still, with the price of a contract-free iPhone 6 starting at $650, the cost of replacement can far exceed the deductible consumers pay for a damaged phone. The smartphone owners insuring their devices still far outnumbers by those who aren't, but as the value of smart devices jumps beyond the $750 of a contract-free iPhone 6 Plus and more smartphone owners crack their screens while taking selfies (as 20% of all smartphone owners who broke their phones have), that divide is shrinking.
