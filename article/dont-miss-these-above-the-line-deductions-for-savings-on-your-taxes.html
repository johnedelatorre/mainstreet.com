Don't Miss These Above The Line Deductions For Savings On Your Taxes

  • Eric Reed
  • Apr 14, 2015 6:45 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Taxes are due on Wednesday. The bars will be open on Wednesday night. Tequila is not, under most circumstances, deductible.

With that reminder out of the way, now’s the time to talk about above the line deductions.

These quirks of the tax code are the silver medals of the 1040 Olympics (just behind credits, which are quite literally free money). An above the line deduction according to Lindsey Buchholz, principal tax research analyst with H&R’s Block’s Tax Institute, is one that taxpayers can take while also still taking the standard deduction.

This is unlike a Schedule A deduction, for example charitable giving or homeownership, for which people have to itemize their taxes. This means both foregoing the standard deduction and putting up with the fairly painful tasks of line-item accounting and digging out old receipts. In fact, for most people itemization isn’t even worth it; the standard deduction is worth more than they could qualify for otherwise.

“What usually gets people above the standard deduction threshold, which is when you’d want to itemize, is typically owning a home or paying state income taxes,” Buchholz said. “People who do not have the ability to itemize are kind of left out of the ability to deduct, without having above the line deductions.”

In what is increasingly becoming a nation of renters, the standard deduction is generally a good idea. That’s what makes above the line deductions so valuable. The government lets you take them without giving up anything in return.

Not many people know this, though. This class of deductions flies under the radar, which is a shame for the average consumer. Don’t leave that money on the table. Keep an eye out for some of these deductions that the IRS is giving out free of charge...

Student Loan Interest

“I think the student loan interest deduction is one of the most popular,” Buchholz noted, and it’s dishearteningly true. The overwhelming majority of Millennials will qualify for this credit, which is applicable to the first $2,500 of interest paid on student loans in the past year.

Payments on the principal do not count toward this deduction.

The American Opportunity Credit

This program allows you to deduct qualifying, current educational expenses, including tuition and supplies, up to $4,000. Taking the AOC can be a little bit complicated and requires choosing between it and the lifetime learning credit, but the IRS has a fairly thorough guide available here.

Business Related Moving Expenses

Move for your job this year? Then congratulations, you’ve got an above the line deduction.

Some of the expenses that qualify for above the line deductions are, to put it charitably, eccentric. Why one expense qualifies and another has to go on Schedule A is a mystery best left up to Congress, but this one made it through. Moving expenses that are work related can be written off as long as it required more than 50 miles of travel.

The best part, Buchholz pointed out, is that this doesn’t matter what line of work you’re in. Get a new job, pack a moving van and write that rental off.

Reservist Travel

Military reservists who have to travel more than 100 miles to do their reservist duty can deduct the costs involved with doing their duty.

Why a deduction and not a credit? See above regarding the mysteries of Congress.

Performing Artist Deduction

If you are

  1. a performing artist, who
  2. worked for two or more employers in the past year, and
  3. made at least $200 per employer, and
  4. paid at least 10% of your income to job-related expenses, and
  5. have an adjusted gross income of $16,000 or less,

then you can claim those job related expenses as an above the line deduction. Enjoy!

Educator Expenses

Teachers who work in K-12 can deduct classroom expenses for which they weren’t reimbursed, taking at least a little bit of the sting out of having to spend their own money to cover budget gaps.

Self-Employment Taxes

Social Security and Medicare taxes are doubled for anyone with the audacity to work for themselves, as they have to cover both the amount paid by the employee and the employer (themselves). In professional terms, it’s what we call “getting hosed.” The tax is smoothed out a little bit, though, because the IRS allows the self-employed to deduct half of this tax from taxable income.

It’s not as good as getting that money back, but it helps.

Health Care and Health Insurance

Payments made into a Health Savings Account qualify, as do health insurance premiums for the self-employed. Not only do you get to stay healthy, but you can save a little bit of money too.

Alimony

Divorce sucks, and our system of alimony invites a lot of criticism. For the time being it’s what we’ve got, though, and like other categories of dependents the IRS lets you take these payments off the top of your taxes.

Probably small solace to everyone cutting those checks, though.
