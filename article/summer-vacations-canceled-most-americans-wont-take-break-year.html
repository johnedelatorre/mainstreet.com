Summer Vacations Canceled: Most Americans Won't Take a Break This Year

  • MainStreet Staff
  • May 1, 2014 9:25 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — How do you tell the kids that there won't be a family vacation this summer? That's the quandary many parents are apparently facing this year, according to a new survey conducted for the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA). In spite of an improving economy, most U.S. adults (55%) admit they don't plan to take a family break this summer.

Money worries are taking a toll on Americans, as 59% say their financial tension during the summer matches or exceeds the stress they feel during the Christmas holiday season.

Parents may be spending the warm weather months close to home, but the kids may still be able to salvage some summer fun. Most parents of children under 17 (62%) plan on shelling out an average of $1,400 for their children's summer activities including sports camps, day camps, sleep-away camps and day care. Unfortunately, there may also be tutoring and academic courses for some kids, too.

Nearly a quarter of parents (23%), say those costs may include a weekly allowance of about $19. For the few Americans that do have summer vacation plans, more than four in ten (41%) say they have budgeted an average of $3,000 for transportation, lodging, meals, activities, entertainment and pet care.

There are some ways to trim the summer fun-time budget, according to the National CPA Financial Literacy Commission:

  • Plan summer activities now and consider all of the costs involved: registration fees, uniform costs, amusement park tickets, pool memberships, etc. Knowing how much money you'll need ahead of time can help you aside the funds necessary to keep the fun rolling.
  • Avoid the weekend rush when possible. Plan midweek adventures to take advantage of the cost savings of weekday discounts that are usually offered by amusement parks, golf courses – and even hotel and rental car companies. Plus, you'll probably find shorter lines and fewer crowds.
  • Don't pay full price for water and theme parks or other entertainment. Look for deals in the form of discount coupons at supermarkets, convenience stores and even hotel brochure racks. The savings can be 25% or more.
  • When choosing accommodations for your vacation, consider the following perks that reduce costs: Does the hotel offer complimentary transportation to the airport, restaurants, or local attractions? And does the room rate include breakfast?
  • Have a summer family clearance sale. Sell unused items stashed in closets, the garage and in the attic on eBay or Craigslist -- or better yet, have a garage sale. You'll raise cash and clear clutter at the same time.

"During stressful financial times, paying with cash is a great way to keep your spending under control," says Ernie Almonte, chair of the AICPA's National CPA Financial Literacy Commission. "By using dollars instead of plastic, consumers are much more aware of their outgoing expenses. When you pay for a vacation with a credit card, you come back with more than photos. You come back with debt, and your vacation could wind up costing considerably more than you had budgeted."

--Written by Hal M. Bundrick for MainStreet
