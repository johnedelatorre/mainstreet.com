Legal Marijuana Businesses Complicate the Federal Tax Code

  • Marguerite Arnold
  • Apr 16, 2014 1:39 PM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — The initiation of recreational marijuana sales in Colorado as well as in Washington State this summer (at minimum) has not entirely cleared the legal and financial haze from the business end of things. In fact, this has only made the redress and resolution of outstanding legal quagmires on both a federal and state level even more immediate.

Thus voters in two states are finally bringing to a head a long-standing federal and state face off on the issues of legal marijuana sales and taxation if not classification and labeling of cannabinoids in particular if not in general.

At stake in all of these confrontations are the still unclear definitions if not interpretations and implementation of federal and state banking and tax regulations and laws where they intersect with marijuana as a federally controlled substance.

The 26 U.S. Code section 280E concerning "expenditures in connection with the illegal sale of drugs" goes as follows:

"No deduction or credit shall be allowed for any amount paid or incurred during the taxable year in carrying on any trade or business if such trade or business (or the activities which comprise such trade or business) consists of trafficking in controlled substances (within the meaning of Schedule I and II of the Controlled Substances Act) which is prohibited by Federal law or the law of any State in which such trade or business is conducted.

The federal Treasury Department released a seven page memo on February 14, 2014 providing updated guidance for those engaged in providing banking services to those in the marijuana industry. Per industry conventional wisdom at least, this seems to indicate a new promise from federal prosecutors not to target legitimate marijuana businesses or banks that process their financial needs. This issue in particular has been one of the most-cited if not feared aspects of offering or engaging in financial activities of any sort in this new sector of the economy.

That said, given ongoing federal raids after 2009 on medical marijuana dispensaries, particularly in Californa and even after President Obama promised to stop this activity, those in the cannabis sector and those who might do their banking business are treading cautiously.

One of the most immediate aspects of the business and financial requirements facing new businesses post legalization is also the issue of paying taxes and deducting legitimate business expenses to lower that burden which is particularly large in Colorado. Including where to deposit the same in order to make tax payments.

The combined state tax rate (including sales and excise taxes) is 25% for recreational marijuana users. Those who use it medicinally under a doctor's care are still only required to pay state sales taxes of 2.9%. Regardless, the retail shop owner, his supply chain and workers, must all still pay taxes too. And so far, all business transactions, including paying taxes, must still be made in cash.

That said, beyond being on the hook for state tax collection and forced to operate in a cash-only transactional basis, there are more issues marijuana businesses face. They also cannot deduct legitimate business expenses, again per conventional advice about application if not definitions in the statute. The fear is the oft-repeated claim that marijuana is in fact an "illegal" Schedule I drug (one with no known medical efficacy).

Per the most recent intent of the Treasury Department, however, at least on a theoretical level, the caution is now more misplaced than it was. That said, despite the raids and prosecutions of the recent past and ongoing legal confusion, does the federal government really still consider "marijuana" as defined by the Act above, a Schedule I drug?

On the legal/regulatory front, the answer may be surprising. Per the strict definitions of the 44-year-old legislation, marijuana is a controlled substance and one regulated by the Act. This is also the approach specifically taken by the recent Treasury Department memo despite the mandatory statements prohibiting use or sale of "marijuana" on federal property.

Even this is technically not true. Every VA clinic in the country, usually attached to a hospital, is partially federal property, and those clinics are legally allowed in every state to dispense at least Marinol.

Part of the confusion here stems frequently from the fact that there is no distinction made on the federal level in this statute at least, between different parts of the marijuana plant except that used for commercial, non-medical purposes. Specifically hemp. And further that "marijuana" is perpetually categorized as a Schedule I drug.

Also See: Marijuana Stock Index Outperforms S&P 500

Per section 802(16) of the 1970 Controlled Substances Act, what is considered marijuana: "The term 'marihuana' means all parts of the plant Cannabis sativa L., whether growing or not; the seeds thereof; the resin extracted from any part of such plant; and every compound, manufacture, salt, derivative, mixture, or preparation of such plant, its seeds or resin. Such term does not include the mature stalks of such plant, fiber produced from such stalks, oil or cake made from the seeds of such plant, any other compound, manufacture, salt, derivative, mixture, or preparation of such mature stalks (except the resin extracted therefrom), fiber, oil, or cake, or the sterilized seed of such plant which is incapable of germination."

The reason, however, that the definitions of the Act run counter to regulatory reality now is drug reclassification. On the regulatory front, the definition of marijuana as a Schedule I drug as implied above is no longer true and actually has not been true for some time. In fact, the false if not misleading characterizations of tetrahydrocannabinidol (THC) should actually have gone out with shag carpets.

As of the mid 1980s, THC, at least, was considered both a Schedule II drug (with known medical efficacy) and being prescribed both to cancer and AIDS patients. Legally. In all 50 states. Trade name Marinol.

Cesamet, a widely used anti-Parkinson's drug, also contains THC, and is likewise classified. By federal definition then, THC has been both a Schedule II drug for over a quarter-century and therefore not "illegal" or without recognized medical efficacy.

It is also worth saying that for the past quarter-century, Marinol has been legally manufactured, bought and sold (even on credit cards connected to real bank accounts) by clients from businesses who also have access to the same. That also includes the federal government via approvals of drug purchases of those on benefits.

By extension then, the federal government has not only recognized marijuana and its components as a useful drug, but further has approved long-standing (interstate) business to trade and transact in the same.

Then, as of 2010, a year after Colorado legalized medical marijuana sales, Marinol became a federal Schedule III. It would stand to reason, per the strict definitions of the statute, that THC would also be so reclassified.

The fact that the Treasury Department has now issued guidelines about regulatory reach if not interpretation also seems to indicate that the federal government is now signaling that it will respect not only this new classification, but as a result, also the rights of state voters to engage in state-level, regulated and approved sales.

But it is precisely this two-prong no-man's land between the nexus of medical necessity and legal regulatory requirements that have been traditionally less than clear (much less logical) where the Controlled Substances Act was concerned. These vague guidelines have proved to be a legal stumbling block to defendants in the past, even those engaged in what they thought were legal and therefore protected activities.

The good news is then by definition as well as legal history, therefore, is that Colorado businesses and those in Washington state this year who face such issues have precedent and legal standing on the federal level that has yet to be fully exercised.

Only time will tell how the next chapter of the development of this business vertical will fully play out however. Those now operating in this vertical or those thinking about doing so should work closely with established business, legal and tax experts now offering their services to a growing and eager market.

--Written by Marguerite Arnold for MainStreet
