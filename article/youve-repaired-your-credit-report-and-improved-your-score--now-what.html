You've Repaired Your Credit Report and Improved Your Score - Now What?

  • Nicholas Pell
  • Nov 3, 2014 9:06 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) - If your credit took a hit in 2008, you're not alone; the financial crisis was hard on a lot of Americans. Still, that was six years ago, and your credit has probably seen some improvement in no small part because of your hard work. So now that you have your credit back on track what do you do going forward?

Step One: Pull Your Credit Report

Kevin Gallegos, vice president of new client enrollment and Phoenix operations with the Freedom Financial Network, points out that you're entitled to a free credit report from each of the major reporting agencies once a year. That's a credit report every four months. Take advantage of it. "Some of the things you've already fixed can pop up again, so keep up on it," Gallegos said.

Gerri Detweiler, director of consumer education with Credit.com, concurs.

"You need to monitor your account," she said. "Settled accounts that you thought you'd taken care of come back onto your credit report," she says. "You need to contact them with proof that you paid it. If you don't see this, you can't do that."

Step Two: Review Your Rates

"If your credit score has improved, you want to review all the interest rates you're paying," says Detweiler.

This is because you probably have better credit than when you opened these accounts. As such, you're paying an interest rate more in alignment with what your credit was like before you repaired it. Detweiler points out that while many people know about mortgage refinancing or even adjusting rates on credit cards, they don't know that, for example, car loans can be refinanced.

Step Three: Convert Secured Cards to Unsecured

A common tactic for people looking to boost their credit scores is to get an unsecured credit card. While these can accomplish the goal of improving your credit, they tend to have low limits. What's more, you're basically just spending money you already gave the credit card company. To that end, Detweiler urges people to convert to unsecured accounts as soon as possible.

Step Four: Set Up Autopay

The biggest factor in your credit score is always timely payments. So Detweiler suggests that it's always a good idea to set up autopay for any bills going forward. "You don't want to end up with late payments on your credit report, because they slipped your mind," she says.

Step Five: Watch Your Utilization

Keeping an eye on your credit utilization ratio is particularly important for people who are dipping their toe back into the pond after a credit fast.

"The biggest piece of your credit score is payments," says Gallegos. "The second biggest part is utilization." Ideally you don't want to be using more than 30% of your credit at any time."

One quick and easy solution? Apply for more credit - but not too much.

"Apply when you need it, because applying too often will negatively impact your credit," he says.

Other than that, just be responsible.

"Maintaining credit is just common sense," he says. "Paying your bills on time. Staying on top of them and not overextending yourself. That's really the key to keeping a good credit score."
