iPhone App Store Backs Down After Pot Leaders Rally for MassRoots

  • Juliette Fairley
  • Feb 23, 2015 1:54 PM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — The iPhone App Store gave the LinkedIn of Pot a run for its money when it banned MassRoots from its selection of available apps.

“We were pulled the same day voters in Oregon, Alaska and Washington D.C. voted to legalize the adult-use of marijuana even though we had no issues with the App Store during that time,” said Isaac Dietrich, the 21-year-old founder of the social networking website.

With 250,000 users, the MassRoots app had been freely available from July 2013 to November 4, 2014 but after it was withdrawn, the App Store requested that MassRoots transform into a strain-guide or dispensary locator.

“We don’t want to be another Leafly or WeedMaps,” Dietrich told MainStreet. “We are a social network and want to stay that way. So, we started a campaign to change the App Store's marijuana policies in early January.”

The rally that occurred as a result forced Apple to reconsider its ban on MassRoots based on its App Store Review Guidelines.

“We submitted an appeal to the App Store through iTunes Connect,” said Dietrich.

Leaders of the legalization movement also weighed in with a policy letter signed by the ArcView Group, the National Cannabis Industry Association and more than 30 cannabis business leaders who asked CEO Tim Cook to re-evaluate Apple's marijuana policies.

“Most attacks on the legal cannabis industry and movement have been from governments but companies like Apple, Facebook, and Google in many ways can be even more powerful then governments,” said Troy Dayton, CEO with the ArcView Group.

Some 10,000 fans of MassRoots wrote letters to Cook as well, causing The App Store to restore the cannabis social apps availability in legal medical marijuana states only.

“Just like Uber, Lyft and AirBnb utilize their broad user-base to help stand-up to attacks on their business so is MassRoots galvanizing the support of its users in ways that allow it to continue,” Dayton told MainStreet. “It's a brilliant business strategy that will also help the cause.”

But before getting back on the Apple app map, Dietrich was asked to implement a hard geo-fence so that only users in 23 states that have legal medicinal or recreational marijuana can access the MassRoots app.

“Before registering for an account, the MassRoots app will now pull a user’s location and verify they are located in a state where medical marijuana is legal,” Deitrich said.

In successfully petitioning Apple to change its stance on the Massroots Application, the national cannabis movement has proven itself to be a powerful force to be reckoned with.

“With this decision, Apple seems to be saying that cannabis is more socially acceptable than pornography or graphic depictions of violence,” said Leo Shalit, a cannabis attorney in New York.

Currently, MassRoots is averaging some 1,000 new users per day split equally between iPhone and Android app users.
