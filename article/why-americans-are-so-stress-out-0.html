Why Americans Are So Stress Out

  • Laura Kiesel
  • Aug 12, 2014 7:40 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — If you've encountered a lot of stress recently, you are far from alone — especially if much of your stress is caused by your health.

A joint study conducted by the NPR/Robert Wood Johnson Foundation/Harvard School of Public Health (HSPH) and released last month found that nearly half (49%) of the people surveyed suffered from a major stressful event or experience in the past year, with 43% of those respondents reporting that the experience was health-related.

To come to these results, researchers conducted telephone interviews of a sample of 2,505 adults through March and early April. Of the total sample, 633 said they have experienced a great deal of stress in the past month.

"It is not widely recognized how many Americans have a major stressful event over the course of a year, or how often health problems are the cause," Robert J. Blendon, Professor of Health Policy and Political Analysis at the Harvard School of Public Health, said in a press release.

In particular, more than half of respondents stated they either were juggling too many responsibilities (54%) or were experiencing financial problems (53%).

More than a third of those who reported a lot of stress identified either their own health problems (38%) and/or the health problems of their family members (37%) as the most prevalent contributing factors to their stress. In addition to those who have undergone major stressors in just the past year, more than a quarter (26%) reported experiencing a "great deal" of stress in just the past month, with the biggest indicator being poor health. Specifically, the study found that people suffering from a health condition were more than twice as likely as the general public to report major stress in the past month — or 60%.

People with disabilities were also much more likely to report a great deal of stress in the past month, at a rate of 45%.

"Those living with a disability or who are in poor health not only report their own health problem as a cause of their high-levels of stress, but they have higher levels of stress caused by their family's health problems and problems with their finances," says Blendon.

Other groups that reported a great deal of stress included individuals with chronic illness (36%), those who make less than $20,000 a year (36%), those who face job insecurity in their current positions (36%), single parents (35%) and parents of teenagers (34%).

MaryKay Bridgers, 39 of Georgia, falls into multiple categories. A widow who suffers from several disabling conditions including fibromyalgia and Degenerative Disc Disorder, she is trying to raise her young son on her meager Social Security survivor benefits.

"I am very worried about how I am going to manage financially," says Bridgers about losing survivor benefits when her son turns 18. "It is a huge source of stress and worry for me - the financial future of my family."

Since Bridgers's health problems prevent her from being able to work, it limits her options for achieving financial security, which in turn causes her more emotional stress. But she has a lot of company in her situation.

Of those who responded that they had recently suffered from stress, 63% reported an adverse impact on emotional well-being as the most prominent impact. Having difficulty sleeping was found to affect 56% of respondents, while difficulty in thinking, concentrating or making decisions affected half. Also, more than half (53%) of those who reported a great deal of stress stated that it aggravated the symptoms of their chronic illness or disability, while 52% said it interfered with their ability to properly manage their condition.

Stress also seems to negatively impact or interfere with social ties, with four out of ten dealing with major stress reporting it made it harder for them to spend quality time or get along as well with friends and family members. Meanwhile, 51% said stress had muddled their ability to concentrate at work.

Those who reported stress in the past month also reported trying to deal with it by employing a number of methods. Namely, the vast majority reported spending time outdoors or devoting time to a hobby they enjoy as their most successful coping mechanism, at 94% and 93%, respectively. About seven in ten said that regularly spending time with family and friends reduced their stress, while others (57%) listed prayer and meditation as a coping strategy.

However, less than half of respondents took certain steps to reduce their stress that are often recommended by experts, such as regularly exercising, eating more healthy or regularly getting a full night's sleep.

"I have a support system of my mother-in-law and my late husband's extended maternal family, who have helped me take care of my son in so many ways," says Bridgers.
