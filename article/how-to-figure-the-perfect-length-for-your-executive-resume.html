How to Figure the Perfect Length For Your Executive Resume

  • Kathryn Tuggle
  • Nov 18, 2014 7:30 AM EST
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — What do hiring managers and executive recruiters really want to see on a resume? While some executives feel longer resumes with more substantial descriptions of duties and accomplishments are best, others say short and sweet is always the best policy. We checked in with experts to find the ideal length for a resume that says it all, but not too much.

"There is no standard across the board, but I like to see two pages," says Greg Gerson, market leader in the life sciences and health care services practice at executive search firm Korn Ferry.

Gerson says he has seen resumes as long as five pages, but that's "way too much." By the same token, a one-page resume for someone with several years of work experience just doesn't quite cut it.

"When I see a one-pager [from an executive] I question why I'm only seeing one page," he says. "The resume should concisely tell the story of what a person has done, without being long-winded."

Although you don't want to be wordy, you do want to highlight career progression and accomplishments, says Joe Weinlick, vice president of marketing at career network Beyond.com.

"Your resume is your professional marketing tool, not an autobiography," Weinlick says. "Aim to be concise and make every word support your key selling points, but don't fear using more space if your experience and skills justify it."

A two-page resume, Weinlick says, is just long enough to incorporate significant successes but short enough to avoid bogging down the reviewer with cumbersome details.

"When I see a resume without a lot of clarity that doesn't show goals and achievements, that starts to raise some red flags," Gerson says. "Your resume is supposed to be a framework for discussion that gives the hiring manager or recruiter a glimpse into who you are. I've seen long resumes that go into pages of detail that still don't tell the story."

In general, there's no need for resumes to have a lengthy summary at the top describing who you are, the role you're after or what you've accomplished.

"The resume should speak for itself. Tell your story by detailing your experience and job progression," Gerson says.

When you're describing your accomplishments, stick to the facts, Gerson says. Think about where you really made your mark in the organization. If someone asked you what your main three to five successess were in each role, what would you say?

"Recruiters prefer quick quantifiables that validate claims about skills and achievements," Weinlick says. "Knowing how to translate your expertise into numbers is vital to keeping your resume out of the trashcan. If you exceeded your annual sales goal, by how much did you exceed it?"

If you feel you need more than two pages to highlight your accomplishments, try a "juiced up" cover letter that goes into more detail, recommends Steven Raz, co-founder and managing partner at executive search firm Cornerstone Search Group.

"It's not uncommon for executives to have a very general CV and a custom-tailored cover letter that really highlights their experience and skill set as it relates to a specific job," Raz says.

Whether you're preparing a resume or a cover letter, Raz says the most important question to ask is, "What is the goal?"

"The goal is to get to the next step in the process, whether that's a phone interview or an in-person interview. Your resume is your marketing document. It's there to help you get in the door or secure a meeting," he says.

Before you apply for a job, do your research to understand exactly how your experience and skills would be valuable to the employer, says Kevin Burns, director of the undergraduate business career center at the W.P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University.

"Recruiters want to see brief reports showing ways that you created measured value for current or former employers, specifically relevant to the job for which you are being recruited," Burns says. "Mercedes-Benz sells cars with 30-second commercials; certainly a one-page summary of the benefits you can provide to an employer should be able to do the same."

Keep in mind that your age will affect the length of your resume only slightly. For example, a 35-year-old and a 55-year-old may both have a two-page resume.

"Someone with 30 years of experience shouldn't have a resume that's six times as long as someone with five years of experience," Raz says. "By the time you've got 30 years of experience, you don't need to have every little detail listed on your resume."

One way to keep resumes to two pages is to outline accomplishments with bullet points only — not paragraphs, says Paul McDonald, senior executive director at professional staffing firm Robert Half.

"Include a brief company profile for each position listed. This profile is particularly important if the organization is not a well-known brand," McDonald says.

The description might include the size of the company and industry, the number of total employees, the number of your direct reports and the scope of your role.

"These points will give the reader a solid understanding of the duties for the various titles you have held," he says.

If you're looking for ways to cut your resume down further, you may be tempted to scrap the "hobbies and interests" or "community involvement" sections, but that's a mistake, Gerson cautions. Those sections show personality, and might give the hiring manager a much-needed glimpse into who you are.

"You don't want to use humor on resume, but you do need to give them some sense of what kind of person you are," Gerson says. "Let them know if you do volunteer work, if you're involved in your community or if you sit on a board or committee."
