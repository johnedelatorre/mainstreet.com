These Are the Cities That Keep You Stuck in Traffic the Longest

  • Jerry Kronenberg
  • Apr 2, 2015 6:40 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Traffic adds more than 20% to how long trips take in America's most-populous cities, and here's a look at the five metro areas that GPS maker TomTom says suffer from the absolutely worst gridlock.

"These are all lively, successful cities in terms of employment and population growth — but you can't build your way out of congestion. Just adding a new freeway won't really help," says Nick Cohn of TomTom, which recently released its fifth-annual Traffic Index.

TomTom — which makes global-positioning systems and software for cars and smartphones — analyzed 2014 trip data from its 400 million users to study traffic levels in 218 major cities around the world, including America's 53 most-populous metro areas.

The good news is that the worst U.S. communities place no higher than 10th worldwide for traffic, far behind the "winning" city of Istanbul, Turkey.

On the downside, TomTom found that gridlock in the U.S. metro areas studied added 21% to the average trip's travel time. That means a journey that would take an hour with no traffic — say, in the middle of the night — actually requires 72.6 minutes on average.

TomTom also discovered that rush-hour trips in America's major cities are far worse. Trips taken during evening peaks require 46% longer than normal on average, while morning commutes need 33% extra time, the study found.

Cohn says U.S. cities with the most gridlock are all coastal communities that have strong job and population growth, but little available land for more roads, rail lines or high-density development that could reduce congestion.

Bill Eisele of Texas A&M University's Transportation Institute says the resulting gridlock not only makes drivers see red, but also hits them in the pocketbook. A 2012 report by Eisele and his colleagues estimated that traffic costs America around $121 billion a year in wasted fuel and work time, or $818 per driver.

"Traffic costs us time that we could better spend with family or hobbies or even doing additional work," he says.

Read on to check out the five metro areas that TomTom found have the worst traffic among America's largest cities.

All percentage figures refer to how much extra time a typical car trip took during 2014 in a given metro area vs. what it would take in times with no traffic. The estimated number of hours that commuters waste annually on gridlock assume that a person works 230 days a year, travels during rush hours and has what would be a 30-minute commute each way without traffic.



Fifth-worst U.S. metro area for traffic: Seattle

It's little wonder that Starbucks calls Seattle home, as the Emerald City's drivers need plenty of coffee to stay awake during traffic jams.

TomTom found that gridlock adds an estimated 30.9% to the time that a typical Seattle trip takes. That includes a 74% increase in travel time during the evening rush and 51% extra in the mornings.

All told, someone with a 30-minute commute each way can expect to spend an extra 89 hours a year traveling to work due to Seattle's rush-hour traffic.

Cohn says the gridlock stems not only from Seattle's tech-related job and population growth, but also from a transportation network that hasn't kept up with either. "The main roads were built when Seattle was much smaller," he says.



Fourth-worst U.S. metro area for traffic: New York City

Plan on spending a lot more than a New York minute stuck in traffic if you drive in the Big Apple.
TomTom estimates that congestion adds 31.2% to the typical New Yorker's car trip, including a 56% increase in evening-rush trips and 48% extra during the morning. Add it up and traffic jams cost the average commuter 79 hours per year stuck in traffic.

Cohn says Gotham's extensive public-transportation system helps reduce problems, but can't compensate for the fact that New York is basically a series of islands and peninsulas linked together by a limited number of tunnels and bridges.

"Everything to and from the Holland Tunnel always has bad traffic, and the George Washington Bridge is the busiest in America," he says.



Third-worst U.S. metro area for traffic: Honolulu

There's trouble in paradise when it comes to Honolulu traffic.

Drivers in Hawaii's state capital can expect to spend 32.2% longer than usual on trips because of congestion, costing the average commuter 88 hours of wasted time per year. Evening rush-hour trips take 68% longer than normal, while morning-peak drives will run you an extra 51% in travel time.

Cohn says much of Honolulu's traffic troubles stem from the fact that it has just two major east/west routes (H-1 and the Nimitz Highway) and limited space to add more roads or public transportation.

"They're building a rail line there, but Honolulu is just very physically restricted and there aren't a lot of good options," he says.



Second-worst U.S. metro area for traffic: San Francisco

When the lights go down in the city and the sun shines on the bay, you might still be stuck in traffic from the night before.

After all, TomTom found that San Francisco drivers spend 34.4% longer on average on car trips due to congestion.

Evening-rush trips take an extra 68% of time due to gridlock, while morning commutes run 53% longer than normal. That eats up 87 hours per year of the typical commuter's time.

Cohn says Frisco's booming tech economy has sent the population soaring — bad news for traffic, given that the city sits on a peninsula linked to much of the mainland by a finite number of bridges.

"San Francisco just has physical limitations in terms of how to get into the city," he says. "You also have a lot of people doing a reverse commute out of San Francisco and down the peninsula, so there are reverse traffic jams as well."



Worst U.S. metro area for traffic: Los Angeles

The City of Angels is a real devil when it comes to traffic.

TomTom discovered that the typical La-La Land drive takes 38.9% more time than usual because of traffic.

Trips also take 80% longer than normal during the evening rush and 60% more than usual in the morning peak period. All told, the average Angeleno spends an added 95 hours a year on commuting as a result of gridlock.

Cohn says Los Angeles has done a lot in recent decades to fight traffic by adding public transportation and reining in its famous sprawl, but that upgrades now in the pipeline will "take a very long time to build."

— Written by Jerry Kronenberg for MainStreet
