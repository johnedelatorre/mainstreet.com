How To Get Your Kids a Good Credit Rating -- Or None At All

  • Nicholas Pell
  • May 13, 2015 6:15 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — The average college debt is just shy of $30,000 at graduation. So when your kid graduates, the last thing he needs is more debt, bad credit and a heavier burden to carry. When your kids go out into "the real world," you're not going to be able to set them up for success in the same way you will before they make that leap. So what are some things you can do to help ensure the financial success of your children as it relates to credit and debt when they’re going out into the world?

Getting Them a Good Credit Rating

Ellie Kay, a family financial expert based out of Southern California and the mother of seven, has helped all of her children to establish their credit. But early on, she made a common rookie mistake: when she got her child an additional card for her and her husband's account, she didn't give the company a Social Security number. "If you don’t give their Social, it won't go on their credit report and they won’t be building credit," she says, lamenting her mistake. "With child number three when it came time to check his credit score, he didn't have one, because we didn’t give the right number."

She's quick to note that, under current federal law, you can't get a credit card until the age of 21 without a cosigner. "Unless they have a job that pays really well -- and most at their age don't -- you can really control how your kids use credit," Kay says. This allows you to act as training wheels for your kids as they start establishing a pattern of responsible credit use. This holds true whether your kids are getting their own cards or just getting an additional card on your account.

Still, Kay prefers the notion of getting a card and adding kids on as additional account holders. It makes it way easier to check in on how your kids are using that credit. What are they spending money on? Are they making the payments on time?

Speaking of timely payments, if you want to help your kids build a good credit rating before graduation, you need to make timely payments of your own. "There's no point in putting them on an additional account if you’re not going to make payments on time," Kay says. That's just going to dig your kids into a deep credit hole before they get out on their own -- the opposite of what you’re trying to do by adding them to your accounts. Additionally, you should avoid carrying balances and keep the utilization low.

But the child who just doesn't seem like they can handle credit can also get a boost from you before they leave the nest. In this case, Kay recommends that you get a credit card attached to your account and don't give the child the card. When they start demonstrating financially responsible behavior, you can give them the physical card. Until then, you just use your own financial prudence to get them started on the right footing.

These strategies allowed all of Kay’s children to graduate from college with credit scores in the “good” range.

Getting Started In Life Without Debt

But do your kids even need credit? Rachel Cruze, author and speaker (and daughter of anti-credit guru Dave Ramsey) says "no."

"Your credit score is all based around debt," says Cruze. “Living debt-free means you won't have a credit score." She explains that when you don't have any credit for 12 to 18 months, your score slowly dips down until it becomes "undetermined." While that's scary to a lot of people, Cruze believes that it only becomes problematic when buying a home, something far in the future for college grads and in any event, something you can get around.

If your kids aren’t going to use credit at all, they’re going to need to learn how to be savers. Cruze recommends a $1,000 emergency fund to start, because kids in college and recent grads just don’t typically have big of "emergencies" looming before them. The big idea, however, is to stay out of all forms of debt, including student loan debt. To that end, Cruze recommends that, once the $1,000 starter emergency fund is in place, kids use as much of their money as they can to pay current tuition and graduate without any kind of debt.

Upon graduation, Cruze says the main goal is to increase that starter emergency fund to the traditional three to six months of living expenses. And while retirement might be a ways off, now is the time to start putting extra money away into retirement. "The earlier you save, the better off you’re going to be," Cruze says.

Cruze recommends that recent grads keep perspective, even if living off of Ramen. "You're in your 20s, not your 50," she says. "If you're broke, that's O.K. Don’t come into it with the expectation that you need to have the same living standard as your parents. You’re not going to have any of that, you’re just starting out.”
