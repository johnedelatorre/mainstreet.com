Marijuana Industry in Nevada Is Rife With Political Jockeying

  • Marguerite Arnold
  • Aug 13, 2014 10:11 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — Nevada has shaped up to be one of the most difficult states for marijuana reform. The state legalized medical use 14 years ago (along with Colorado) by voter-driven amendment to change the state's constitution. Since then, reform has lagged, primarily because of the influence of the deep-pocketed gambling industry, all of which is heavily regulated by federal law.

This year however, like the rest of the country, Nevada is moving forward on the issue inexorably, if more slowly and cautiously than advocates would hope.

This year the focus in state is to set up a regulated medical dispensary and production system. Next year advocates hope the state legislature takes up full legalization. If that fails, plans are already on the table to collect signatures to put the issue on the ballot in 2016.

The state is also home to heavy national political hitters who in the last year have made headlines with their surprising statements about medical marijuana in particular. Both Senate Majority leader Democrat Harry Reid as well as deep pocketed Republican donors like gambling magnate Sheldon Adelson have come forward in the last year to voice support for some aspect of medical reform.

Read More: Cannabis Dispensary in Nevada Secures Bank Account

This year the state market is shaping up to be one of the more expensive in the country. The state's dispensary distribution lottery process has also been marked by charges of undue influence by well-connected and deep-pocketed hopeful marijuana entrepreneurs both in and out of state.

It is a charge that Joe Brezny, executive director of the Nevada Cannabis Industry Association, takes issue with. "The state system is the most merit based and least political I've seen," he said. "The law was written specifically to have a merit based review and the state regulators have implemented the law very well."

That said, Brezny admits there are some places where implementation has been less than ideal. "The local system has seemed more murky and possibly corrupted," he said. "As local lobbyists and self-appointed policy experts try to influence the local elected officials in order to win their approval on a location."

In Clark County, the first county in Nevada to open dispensary rights and other aspects of the business up for lottery selection, three prospective companies interested in those rights have donated funds to county commissioners who are overseeing the lottery process. Nevada City is proposing to let the state rank the city's license applicants.

Read More: Medical Marijuana Program Expands in D.C.

"The state law allows for a merit based review at the state level, but local elected officials can ban it outright or limit zoning in their town," Brezny said. "That has led to some aggressive local lobbying efforts that are not nearly as clean and above board as the state review system. What you saw was the mark of aggressive amateur consultants in the process that are giving bad advice. Elected officials aren't going to be purchased for a contribution, especially right before a vote."

Las Vegas also just closed its first application process for lottery applicants. A studded line-up of hopefuls includes a former city councilman, a former state senator, a former Assembly speaker and a retired police chief.

Read More: First Cannabis License Lottery Applicants Approved in Clarke County, Nevada

Brezny believes, however, that this is just an inevitability of the process. "The more regulation and state control, the more possibility there is for influence," he said. "But we can't just have a wide open market. This isn't like regulating tomatoes. Tomatoes don't get you high. We need good, smart, strong regulation to eliminate the black market and get this away from kids. But we need that regulation to be clean and corruption-free."

While this kind of political jockeying for influence is not limited to the state, Nevada appears to be on course to have one of the most politically influenced medical industries in the country simply because of who wins dispensary licenses during the lottery process.

Brezny agrees to a point. "Tough systems can either be squeaky clean or a way to allow cronyism to occur," he said. "Nevada has a state system that is clean and above board. I would watch the strict regulatory systems in Florida, New York and New Jersey to see how they come into the market. They can be models for the nation or ways for political heavyweights to get into a new game."

Given the high stakes in state for a huge potential recreational market in and around the legal gambling industry, it is highly likely that those who end up on the business end of reform will have not cut their teeth, politically or otherwise, within the world of cannabis legalization but rather other verticals. How this will also shape the market as it moves towards greater reform is also still hard to predict.

That said, according to Brezny, at least one market in Nevada, Las Vegas, presents a unique challenge unlike any municipal market in the country. "Our medical law has reciprocity," he said. "Nevada has a small population, but 40 million people a year visit Las Vegas. 24 states currently have medical laws, so this translates into tens of thousands of cardholders visiting Nevada every week."

That inevitably makes Las Vegas into a market unlike any other.

"Full retail cannabis legalization in a few years will solidify the state as a destination for cannabis aficionados," Brezny said.
