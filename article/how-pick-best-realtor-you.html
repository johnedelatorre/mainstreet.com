How to Pick the Best Realtor for You

  • Robert McGarvey
  • Aug 27, 2014 8:36 AM EDT
ADVERTISEMENT

NEW YORK (MainStreet) — There is a right realtor for you and there is a wrong one.

Read More: Is the Realtor An Endangered Species

The key is knowing how to pick the right one and knowing how to dodge the bad ones.

There is no magic to it. The process is straightforward.

How not to do this is to follow national rankings, such as the recent J.D. Power Home Buyer/Seller Satisfaction Survey.

Read More: Americans Are More Confident About the Housing Market

That survey, incidentally, pegs Century 21 highest in customer satisfaction. J.D. Power noted: "Buyers and sellers choose a real estate firm based on its reputation (30% buyers vs. 35% sellers), past experience with the agent/salesperson (21% vs. 25%), and/or recommendation (24% vs. 21%)."

The last bit is right on. Ignore surveys, go instead with individual reputation of the realtor because that - much more than the reputation of the firm - will determine your happiness.

Buyers and sellers need different agents. With different skills.

In Jersey City, N.J. when I sold a house a year ago, I used a realtor whom I had known for eight years, mainly because he was a neighbor who lived three doors down. I knew he understood the block - where prices are sharply higher than neighboring blocks because the street had a long reputation for housing Hudson County power brokers - and I knew, because he had invested substantially in renovating his own home, that he saw a bright future on the street.

In Tucson when I sold a house ten years ago, I used a husband-wife realtor team who lived down the hill from me, in the same gated community. They needed no convincing about why it was good to live there.

This is critical: particularly when you are selling a home, pick a realtor who deeply knows the location, right down to the specific block. You want a realtor who is an evangelist for your property and that takes real knowledge.

You don't know that agent? No problem. When sifting through would-be listing agents for your property, always ask: where do you live? How long? You want somebody who lives in your zip code and who likes it there.

"Tell me three things that make my block great." Ask that. Listen. You will hear the right agent.

Incidentally, what you don't want is an agent who accumulates listings but does nothing to market them. They are legend in the industry and, often, they make very good money, because when the house sells - by virtue of the sweat of the buyer's agent - the listing agent still gets 2.5% to 3% of the sale (minus whatever share their agency grabs).

Always ask the marketing plan. It does not have to include open houses - which rarely sell houses - but should include a private showing for other realtors, usually involving cocktails and canapés.

What else will realtors do? Hire a professional videographer to shoot a home tour? A pro photographer to take pix (pictures sell houses, that is fact)? Create a website on the home? A Pinterest board? Suggest what you'd like and if the realtor is dragging butt, do it yourself.

Take that hands on approach and your house will sell even in a sluggish market.

You want to buy?

You need a different realtor, and the one who sold your house may be all wrong for the buy side.

Word of advice: make yourself into the expert on any neighborhood you want to buy into. Listen to realtors, but depend on your own knowledge.

What you want from a realtor when buying are the ability to quickly "run comps" - find what comparable houses have sold for when you find one you want to make an offer on -- and also the energy to take you to eight to ten houses this Saturday...next Saturday...and the Saturday after that.

Finding a right priced house today is not fast. You need to sort through a lot you don't want to offer on and you need a realtor with the energy to stick with the process.

I have bought two houses and a condo in the past 13 years, and, always, I found the places I wound up buying myself. With so many online resources it is easy to sift inventory, a realtor is not needed for that.

Oh, you also want a realtor who is not embarrassed to make an offer that is sharply below the asking price. Many flinch at offers that are 10% and more below asking. You don't want that realtor.

Ask for the last three to five units a particular realtor has helped buyers buy. Use Zillow to rapidly see how much the selling price was below the listing price. See big numbers and that is your realtor.

It really is this simple.

--Written by Robert McGarvey for MainStreet
